# Universal Algebra, Syntax Algebras, Representations, Evaluation, and Generated Structures

## 0. Orientation

This treatise develops the algebraic theory of formal syntax from the standpoint of universal algebra. The central objects are signatures, algebras of a fixed similarity type, homomorphisms, subalgebras, congruences, quotients, free algebras, term algebras, concrete presentations of syntax, evaluation homomorphisms, generated subalgebras, kernels, presentations by generators and relations, substitution, relational generation, logical syntax, derivation witnesses, effective parsing, and categorical repackagings.

The development proceeds from abstract algebraic structure to concrete representations. Trees, strings, tuples, and related encodings are treated as presentations of an already-defined free algebra, not as the primitive foundation of syntax. Evaluation into a concrete algebra is treated as the canonical homomorphic extension of a generator assignment. The concrete subalgebra generated by the chosen semantic values is then the homomorphic image of the free syntax algebra, and its algebraic identifications are measured by the kernel congruence of evaluation.

No proofs are included. Named items state definitions, constructions, criteria, and structural results with explicit hypotheses.

## 1. Ambient Setting, Size Conventions, and Notation

### 1.1. Foundational Conventions

> [!definition] Definition 1.1: Ambient Universe
> Fix a foundational universe of sets sufficient to contain all carriers, operation-symbol sets, indexing sets, finite products, quotient sets, trees, strings, and recursively generated sets used below. Unless a section explicitly states otherwise, every carrier is a set and every operation is a set-function.

> [!definition] Definition 1.2: Finite Ordinals and Tuples
> For $n \in \mathbb N$, write
> $$
> n=\{0,1,\dots,n-1\}.
> $$
> For a set $X$, the set of $n$-tuples in $X$ is
> $$
> X^n=\{a:n\to X\}.
> $$
> If $a\in X^n$, its value at $i<n$ may be written $a_i$, and the tuple may be displayed as $(a_0,\dots,a_{n-1})$.

> [!notation] Notation 1.3: Underlying Carriers
> If $\mathbf A$ is an algebraic structure, its underlying set is denoted $|\mathbf A|$. If no ambiguity is possible, the carrier may be written $A$, but formal statements distinguish $\mathbf A$ from $|\mathbf A|$.

> [!notation] Notation 1.4: Images, Kernels, and Restrictions
> For a function $h:X\to Y$ and $S\subseteq X$, write
> $$
> h[S]=\{h(s):s\in S\}.
> $$
> If $E$ is an equivalence relation on $X$, the $E$-class of $x\in X$ is written $[x]_E$, and the quotient set is $X/E$. If $R\subseteq X\times X$, the diagonal relation on $X$ is
> $$
> \Delta_X=\{(x,x):x\in X\}.
> $$

> [!warning] Warning 1.5: Equality, Isomorphism, and Presentation
> Equality of sets, equality of algebras, isomorphism of algebras, and equivalence of presentations are distinct notions. A tree model of syntax, a tuple model of syntax, and a string model of syntax may be canonically isomorphic as $\Omega$-algebras without being literally equal as sets or as implemented data structures.

### 1.2. Global Algebraic Scope

> [!definition] Definition 1.6: One-Sorted Finitary Scope
> Unless explicitly extended, a signature is one-sorted and finitary: every operation symbol has arity $n$ for some $n\in\mathbb N$. Infinitary arities, partial operations, many-sorted carriers, relations, binding operators, and computability constraints are introduced only in sections where they are explicitly named.

> [!remark] Remark 1.7: Constants and Nullary Operations
> A nullary operation in an algebra $\mathbf A$ is a function $1\to |\mathbf A|$, where $1=\{\emptyset\}$ is a singleton. It is equivalently represented by its unique value in $|\mathbf A|$. A generator is not a nullary operation symbol unless the signature explicitly contains a corresponding nullary operation symbol.

> [!warning] Warning 1.8: Existence Versus Effective Recovery
> A structural theorem asserting unique decomposition, unique extension, or existence of a quotient does not by itself assert that an algorithm can recognize constructor ranges, compute decompositions, decide equality modulo a congruence, or parse strings. Computability requires additional data and decidability hypotheses.


## 2. Signatures and Algebras

### 2.1. Signatures

> [!definition] Definition 2.1: Finitary Signature
> A finitary one-sorted signature is a pair
> $$
> \Omega=(\Omega,\operatorname{ar})
> $$
> consisting of a set $\Omega$ of operation symbols and an arity function
> $$
> \operatorname{ar}:\Omega\to\mathbb N.
> $$
> For $n\in\mathbb N$, define
> $$
> \Omega_n=\{f\in\Omega:\operatorname{ar}(f)=n\}.
> $$
> The family $(\Omega_n)_{n\in\mathbb N}$ is the arity-indexed family of operation symbols.

> [!definition] Definition 2.2: Similarity Type
> Two signatures $\Omega$ and $\Omega'$ have the same similarity type when there is a bijection
> $$
> \alpha:\Omega\to\Omega'
> $$
> satisfying
> $$
> \operatorname{ar}'(\alpha(f))=\operatorname{ar}(f)
> $$
> for every $f\in\Omega$. An $\Omega$-algebra and an $\Omega'$-algebra may be compared as algebras of the same type only after such an arity-preserving identification has been specified.

> [!definition] Definition 2.3: Nullary Operation Symbol
> A symbol $c\in\Omega_0$ is a nullary operation symbol. In formal syntax it produces a term $c()$, often displayed as $c$. In an algebra $\mathbf A$, it is interpreted as an element $c^{\mathbf A}\in |\mathbf A|$.

> [!warning] Warning 2.4: Constants as Generators Versus Constants as Nullary Symbols
> Let $X$ be a set of generators and let $\Omega_0$ be the set of nullary operation symbols. The elements of $X$ are external parameters for freeness. The symbols in $\Omega_0$ are part of the similarity type. A term variable $x\in X$ and a nullary symbol $c\in\Omega_0$ have different formal origins even if a valuation sends $x$ to the same element of a target algebra as $c^{\mathbf A}$.

> [!definition] Definition 2.5: Many-Sorted Signature
> A many-sorted finitary signature consists of a set $S$ of sorts, a set $\Omega$ of operation symbols, and for each $f\in\Omega$ a finite input word
> $$
> (s_0,\dots,s_{n-1})\in S^n
> $$
> together with an output sort $s\in S$. The one-sorted case is recovered when $S$ is a singleton. Unless stated otherwise, subsequent sections use the one-sorted case.

### 2.2. $\Omega$-Algebras

> [!definition] Definition 2.6: $\Omega$-Algebra
> Let $\Omega$ be a finitary one-sorted signature. An $\Omega$-algebra is a pair
> $$
> \mathbf A=(|\mathbf A|,(f^{\mathbf A})_{f\in\Omega})
> $$
> where $|\mathbf A|$ is a set and, for every $f\in\Omega_n$, the interpretation of $f$ in $\mathbf A$ is a function
> $$
> f^{\mathbf A}:|\mathbf A|^n\to |\mathbf A|.
> $$
> If $n=0$, then $|\mathbf A|^0$ is a singleton and $f^{\mathbf A}$ is identified with its unique value.

> [!example] Example 2.7: Magmas, Monoids, Groups, Rings, Lattices, and Boolean Algebras
> A magma has one binary symbol $m$. A monoid has one binary symbol $m$ and one nullary symbol $e$. A group has $m$, a unary inverse symbol $i$, and $e$. A ring signature may contain binary $+$, binary $\cdot$, unary $-$, and nullary $0,1$. A lattice signature contains binary $\wedge$ and binary $\vee$. A Boolean algebra signature may contain $\wedge,\vee,\neg,0,1$ with arities $2,2,1,0,0$.

> [!definition] Definition 2.8: Reduct and Expansion
> Let $\Omega'\subseteq\Omega$ be a subsignature, meaning $\operatorname{ar}$ restricts to an arity function on $\Omega'$. If $\mathbf A$ is an $\Omega$-algebra, its $\Omega'$-reduct $\mathbf A|_{\Omega'}$ has the same carrier and only the operations indexed by $\Omega'$. Conversely, an expansion of an $\Omega'$-algebra to $\Omega$ is an $\Omega$-algebra whose $\Omega'$-reduct is the given algebra.

> [!warning] Warning 2.9: Structure Is Not Just Carrier
> Two algebras may have the same underlying set and different operation interpretations. Statements about homomorphisms, subalgebras, congruences, generated subalgebras, and quotients depend on the operations, not only on the carrier.

### 2.3. Homomorphisms

> [!definition] Definition 2.10: Homomorphism
> Let $\mathbf A$ and $\mathbf B$ be $\Omega$-algebras. A homomorphism
> $$
> h:\mathbf A\to\mathbf B
> $$
> is a function
> $$
> h:|\mathbf A|\to |\mathbf B|
> $$
> such that for every $f\in\Omega_n$ and every $(a_0,\dots,a_{n-1})\in |\mathbf A|^n$,
> $$
> h(f^{\mathbf A}(a_0,\dots,a_{n-1}))
> =
> f^{\mathbf B}(h(a_0),\dots,h(a_{n-1})).
> $$
> If $n=0$, this condition states
> $$
> h(f^{\mathbf A})=f^{\mathbf B}.
> $$

> [!proposition] Proposition 2.11: Identity and Composition
> For every $\Omega$-algebra $\mathbf A$, the identity function on $|\mathbf A|$ is a homomorphism $\mathbf A\to\mathbf A$. If $h:\mathbf A\to\mathbf B$ and $k:\mathbf B\to\mathbf C$ are homomorphisms, then $k\circ h:\mathbf A\to\mathbf C$ is a homomorphism.

> [!definition] Definition 2.12: Isomorphism, Embedding, Endomorphism, and Automorphism
> Let $h:\mathbf A\to\mathbf B$ be a homomorphism of $\Omega$-algebras.
> 
> 1. $h$ is an isomorphism if there is a homomorphism $h^{-1}:\mathbf B\to\mathbf A$ such that $h^{-1}\circ h=\operatorname{id}_{\mathbf A}$ and $h\circ h^{-1}=\operatorname{id}_{\mathbf B}$.
> 2. $h$ is an embedding if $h$ is injective as a function and identifies $\mathbf A$ with the induced subalgebra on $h[|\mathbf A|]$.
> 3. $h$ is an endomorphism if $\mathbf A=\mathbf B$.
> 4. $h$ is an automorphism if it is both an endomorphism and an isomorphism.

> [!proposition] Proposition 2.13: Bijective Homomorphism Criterion
> A homomorphism $h:\mathbf A\to\mathbf B$ is an isomorphism if and only if $h$ is bijective as a function. In that case the inverse function is a homomorphism.

### 2.4. Subalgebras and Generated Subalgebras

> [!definition] Definition 2.14: Closed Subset
> Let $\mathbf A$ be an $\Omega$-algebra. A subset $S\subseteq |\mathbf A|$ is closed under the $\Omega$-operations of $\mathbf A$ if, for every $f\in\Omega_n$ and every $(s_0,\dots,s_{n-1})\in S^n$,
> $$
> f^{\mathbf A}(s_0,\dots,s_{n-1})\in S.
> $$
> When $n=0$, this requires $f^{\mathbf A}\in S$ for every $f\in\Omega_0$.

> [!definition] Definition 2.15: Subalgebra
> A subalgebra $\mathbf S\leq\mathbf A$ is an $\Omega$-algebra with carrier $|\mathbf S|\subseteq |\mathbf A|$ such that $|\mathbf S|$ is closed under the operations of $\mathbf A$ and, for every $f\in\Omega_n$,
> $$
> f^{\mathbf S}=f^{\mathbf A}|_{|\mathbf S|^n}.
> $$

> [!proposition] Proposition 2.16: Subalgebra Criterion
> For an $\Omega$-algebra $\mathbf A$ and a subset $S\subseteq |\mathbf A|$, there exists a unique subalgebra of $\mathbf A$ with carrier $S$ if and only if $S$ is closed under all $\Omega$-operations of $\mathbf A$.

> [!proposition] Proposition 2.17: Intersections of Subalgebras
> Let $\mathcal S$ be a nonempty family of subalgebras of $\mathbf A$. Then
> $$
> \bigcap_{\mathbf S\in\mathcal S}|\mathbf S|
> $$
> is closed under all $\Omega$-operations of $\mathbf A$, hence carries a unique subalgebra structure induced from $\mathbf A$. If nullary operation symbols are present, every subalgebra carrier contains their interpretations, so the intersection condition includes nullary closure.

> [!construction] Construction 2.18: Generated Subalgebra
> Let $\mathbf A$ be an $\Omega$-algebra and let $S\subseteq |\mathbf A|$. The subalgebra generated by $S$ in $\mathbf A$ is the subalgebra whose carrier is
> $$
> \langle S\rangle_{\mathbf A}
> =
> \bigcap\{B\subseteq |\mathbf A|: S\subseteq B \text{ and } B \text{ is closed under all } \Omega\text{-operations of } \mathbf A\}.
> $$
> It is the least subalgebra carrier of $\mathbf A$ containing $S$.

> [!construction] Construction 2.19: Stage Construction for Generated Subalgebras
> Let $S\subseteq |\mathbf A|$. Define stages $G_m(S)\subseteq |\mathbf A|$ recursively by
> $$
> G_0(S)=S\cup\{c^{\mathbf A}:c\in\Omega_0\},
> $$
> and
> $$
> G_{m+1}(S)=G_m(S)\cup\{f^{\mathbf A}(a_0,\dots,a_{n-1}):f\in\Omega_n,\ (a_0,\dots,a_{n-1})\in G_m(S)^n,\ n\in\mathbb N\}.
> $$
> The stage-generated set is
> $$
> G_\omega(S)=\bigcup_{m<\omega}G_m(S).
> $$

> [!proposition] Proposition 2.20: Finite-Arity Stage Characterization
> If $\Omega$ is finitary, then
> $$
> \langle S\rangle_{\mathbf A}=G_\omega(S).
> $$
> Each element of $\langle S\rangle_{\mathbf A}$ is obtained by finitely many applications of operation interpretations to elements of $S$ and to nullary operation interpretations.

> [!remark] Remark 2.21: Generatedness as a Structural Property
> The statement $\langle S\rangle_{\mathbf A}=|\mathbf A|$ is a property of the pair $(\mathbf A,S)$. It does not specify unique expressions for elements of $|\mathbf A|$. Unique expression requires additional freeness or unique-readability hypotheses.

### 2.5. Products

> [!construction] Construction 2.22: Direct Product of $\Omega$-Algebras
> Let $(\mathbf A_i)_{i\in I}$ be a family of $\Omega$-algebras indexed by a set $I$. Their direct product is the $\Omega$-algebra
> $$
> \prod_{i\in I}\mathbf A_i
> $$
> with carrier
> $$
> \prod_{i\in I}|\mathbf A_i|
> $$
> and operations defined coordinatewise: for $f\in\Omega_n$ and $(a^0,\dots,a^{n-1})\in(\prod_i |\mathbf A_i|)^n$,
> $$
> f^{\prod_i\mathbf A_i}(a^0,\dots,a^{n-1})(i)
> =
> f^{\mathbf A_i}(a^0(i),\dots,a^{n-1}(i)).
> $$

> [!proposition] Proposition 2.23: Projection Homomorphisms
> For each $j\in I$, the coordinate projection
> $$
> \pi_j:\prod_{i\in I}\mathbf A_i\to\mathbf A_j
> $$
> is a homomorphism.

> [!theorem] Theorem 2.24: Product Universal Property
> Let $\mathbf B$ be an $\Omega$-algebra and let $(h_i:\mathbf B\to\mathbf A_i)_{i\in I}$ be a family of homomorphisms. There exists a unique homomorphism
> $$
> h:\mathbf B\to\prod_{i\in I}\mathbf A_i
> $$
> such that
> $$
> \pi_i\circ h=h_i
> $$
> for every $i\in I$.

> [!construction] Construction 2.25: Diagonal Homomorphism
> For an $\Omega$-algebra $\mathbf A$, the diagonal map
> $$
> \delta_{\mathbf A}:\mathbf A\to\mathbf A\times\mathbf A
> $$
> is the function $a\mapsto(a,a)$. It is a homomorphism.

> [!definition] Definition 2.26: Subdirect Product
> A subalgebra $\mathbf S\leq\prod_{i\in I}\mathbf A_i$ is subdirect if every restricted projection
> $$
> \pi_i|_{|\mathbf S|}:\mathbf S\to\mathbf A_i
> $$
> is surjective.

### 2.6. Congruences

> [!definition] Definition 2.27: Compatible Equivalence Relation
> Let $\mathbf A$ be an $\Omega$-algebra. An equivalence relation $\theta\subseteq |\mathbf A|\times |\mathbf A|$ is compatible with the $\Omega$-operations if, for every $f\in\Omega_n$ and all $a_i,b_i\in |\mathbf A|$ with $(a_i,b_i)\in\theta$ for each $i<n$,
> $$
> (f^{\mathbf A}(a_0,\dots,a_{n-1}),f^{\mathbf A}(b_0,\dots,b_{n-1}))\in\theta.
> $$
> For $n=0$, compatibility imposes no extra condition beyond equality of the same nullary value with itself.

> [!definition] Definition 2.28: Congruence
> A congruence on an $\Omega$-algebra $\mathbf A$ is an equivalence relation on $|\mathbf A|$ compatible with every $\Omega$-operation of $\mathbf A$. The set of congruences on $\mathbf A$ is denoted
> $$
> \operatorname{Con}(\mathbf A).
> $$

> [!proposition] Proposition 2.29: Congruences as Subalgebras of the Product
> Let $\theta$ be an equivalence relation on $|\mathbf A|$. Then $\theta$ is a congruence on $\mathbf A$ if and only if $\theta$, regarded as a subset of $|\mathbf A|\times |\mathbf A|$, is the carrier of a subalgebra of $\mathbf A\times\mathbf A$.

> [!construction] Construction 2.30: Congruence Generated by a Relation
> Let $R\subseteq |\mathbf A|\times |\mathbf A|$. The congruence generated by $R$ is
> $$
> \operatorname{Cg}_{\mathbf A}(R)
> =
> \bigcap\{\theta\in\operatorname{Con}(\mathbf A):R\subseteq\theta\}.
> $$
> It is the least congruence on $\mathbf A$ containing $R$.

> [!theorem] Theorem 2.31: Congruence Lattice
> For every $\Omega$-algebra $\mathbf A$, the poset $\operatorname{Con}(\mathbf A)$ ordered by inclusion is a complete lattice. Meets are intersections. Joins are given by generated congruences:
> $$
> \bigvee_{i\in I}\theta_i
> =
> \operatorname{Cg}_{\mathbf A}\left(\bigcup_{i\in I}\theta_i\right).
> $$

> [!definition] Definition 2.32: Kernel of a Homomorphism
> Let $h:\mathbf A\to\mathbf B$ be a homomorphism. The kernel of $h$ is the relation
> $$
> \ker h=\{(a,a')\in |\mathbf A|^2:h(a)=h(a')\}.
> $$

> [!proposition] Proposition 2.33: Kernels Are Congruences
> If $h:\mathbf A\to\mathbf B$ is a homomorphism of $\Omega$-algebras, then $\ker h\in\operatorname{Con}(\mathbf A)$.

### 2.7. Quotient Algebras

> [!construction] Construction 2.34: Quotient Algebra
> Let $\mathbf A$ be an $\Omega$-algebra and let $\theta\in\operatorname{Con}(\mathbf A)$. The quotient algebra $\mathbf A/\theta$ has carrier $|\mathbf A|/\theta$ and operations defined by
> $$
> f^{\mathbf A/\theta}([a_0]_\theta,\dots,[a_{n-1}]_\theta)
> =
> [f^{\mathbf A}(a_0,\dots,a_{n-1})]_\theta
> $$
> for $f\in\Omega_n$.

> [!warning] Warning 2.35: Compatibility Is the Well-Definedness Condition
> The formula defining $f^{\mathbf A/\theta}$ is well-defined precisely because $\theta$ is compatible with $f^{\mathbf A}$. If $\theta$ is only an equivalence relation and not a congruence, the displayed formula may depend on representatives.

> [!construction] Construction 2.36: Quotient Projection
> The quotient projection associated with $\theta\in\operatorname{Con}(\mathbf A)$ is the homomorphism
> $$
> q_\theta:\mathbf A\to\mathbf A/\theta,
> \qquad
> q_\theta(a)=[a]_\theta.
> $$
> It is surjective and satisfies
> $$
> \ker q_\theta=\theta.
> $$

> [!theorem] Theorem 2.37: Universal Property of Quotients
> Let $h:\mathbf A\to\mathbf B$ be a homomorphism and let $\theta\in\operatorname{Con}(\mathbf A)$. There exists a unique homomorphism
> $$
> \bar h:\mathbf A/\theta\to\mathbf B
> $$
> such that
> $$
> \bar h\circ q_\theta=h
> $$
> if and only if
> $$
> \theta\subseteq\ker h.
> $$
> In that case, $\bar h([a]_\theta)=h(a)$ for every $a\in |\mathbf A|$.

### 2.8. Kernels, Images, and Isomorphism

> [!definition] Definition 2.38: Image Subalgebra
> If $h:\mathbf A\to\mathbf B$ is a homomorphism, then
> $$
> \operatorname{im}(h)=h[|\mathbf A|]
> $$
> is closed under all operations of $\mathbf B$. The induced subalgebra of $\mathbf B$ with this carrier is also denoted $\operatorname{im}(h)$.

> [!theorem] Theorem 2.39: First Isomorphism Theorem for $\Omega$-Algebras
> Let $h:\mathbf A\to\mathbf B$ be a homomorphism of $\Omega$-algebras. Then $\ker h$ is a congruence on $\mathbf A$, $\operatorname{im}(h)$ is a subalgebra of $\mathbf B$, and there is a unique isomorphism
> $$
> \Phi_h:\mathbf A/\ker h\to\operatorname{im}(h)
> $$
> satisfying
> $$
> \Phi_h([a]_{\ker h})=h(a)
> $$
> for every $a\in |\mathbf A|$.

> [!corollary] Corollary 2.40: Injectivity and Trivial Kernel
> A homomorphism $h:\mathbf A\to\mathbf B$ is injective if and only if
> $$
> \ker h=\Delta_{|\mathbf A|}.
> $$

> [!corollary] Corollary 2.41: Surjectivity and Image Equality
> A homomorphism $h:\mathbf A\to\mathbf B$ is surjective if and only if
> $$
> \operatorname{im}(h)=\mathbf B
> $$
> as a subalgebra of $\mathbf B$.

> [!construction] Construction 2.42: Canonical Image Factorization
> Every homomorphism $h:\mathbf A\to\mathbf B$ factors as
> $$
> \mathbf A\xrightarrow{q_{\ker h}}\mathbf A/\ker h
> \xrightarrow{\Phi_h}\operatorname{im}(h)
> \xhookrightarrow{}\mathbf B,
> $$
> where $q_{\ker h}$ is surjective, $\Phi_h$ is an isomorphism, and the last map is the inclusion embedding of the image subalgebra.


## 3. Free Algebras and the Term Algebra

### 3.1. Free Algebras Abstractly

> [!definition] Definition 3.1: Generator Assignment
> Let $X$ be a set and let $\mathbf F$ be an $\Omega$-algebra. A generator assignment for $\mathbf F$ indexed by $X$ is a function
> $$
> \eta:X\to |\mathbf F|.
> $$
> The pair $(\mathbf F,\eta)$ is generated by $X$ when
> $$
> \langle \eta[X]\rangle_{\mathbf F}=|\mathbf F|.
> $$

> [!definition] Definition 3.2: Free $\Omega$-Algebra on a Set
> Let $X$ be a set. A free $\Omega$-algebra on $X$ is a pair $(\mathbf F_X,\eta_X)$ where $\mathbf F_X$ is an $\Omega$-algebra and $\eta_X:X\to |\mathbf F_X|$ is a function such that for every $\Omega$-algebra $\mathbf A$ and every function $g:X\to |\mathbf A|$, there exists a unique homomorphism
> $$
> \overline g:\mathbf F_X\to\mathbf A
> $$
> satisfying
> $$
> \overline g\circ\eta_X=g.
> $$

> [!remark] Remark 3.3: Freeness Relative to the Chosen Generator Map
> Freeness is a property of the pair $(\mathbf F_X,\eta_X)$, not of the carrier $|\mathbf F_X|$ alone. Replacing $\eta_X$ by another function $X\to |\mathbf F_X|$ may destroy or create the universal mapping property.

> [!theorem] Theorem 3.4: Generatedness of Free Algebras
> If $(\mathbf F_X,\eta_X)$ is free on $X$, then
> $$
> \langle \eta_X[X]\rangle_{\mathbf F_X}=|\mathbf F_X|.
> $$
> Thus every element of a finitary free algebra is obtained by a finite formal use of operation interpretations from the designated generators and nullary operation interpretations.

> [!theorem] Theorem 3.5: Uniqueness up to Unique Isomorphism
> Let $(\mathbf F_X,\eta_X)$ and $(\mathbf F'_X,\eta'_X)$ be free $\Omega$-algebras on the same set $X$. There exists a unique isomorphism
> $$
> \alpha:\mathbf F_X\to\mathbf F'_X
> $$
> satisfying
> $$
> \alpha\circ\eta_X=\eta'_X.
> $$
> Its inverse is the unique homomorphism $\mathbf F'_X\to\mathbf F_X$ extending $\eta_X$ along $\eta'_X$.

> [!warning] Warning 3.6: Free Object Before Representation
> The universal property defines an abstract algebraic object. It does not require choosing strings, trees, tuples, memory layouts, parsing algorithms, or pointer structures. Such choices are presentations or implementations of the free object.

### 3.2. Formal Terms

> [!construction] Construction 3.7: Set of Formal $\Omega$-Terms over $X$
> Let $\Omega$ be a finitary signature and let $X$ be a set disjoint from $\Omega$ after tagging if necessary. The set $T_{\Omega}(X)$ of formal $\Omega$-terms over $X$ is the least set equipped with:
> 
> 1. a variable constructor $\eta_X:X\to T_{\Omega}(X)$;
> 2. for each $f\in\Omega_n$, a formal constructor
> $$
> f^{T}:T_{\Omega}(X)^n\to T_{\Omega}(X)
> $$
> written
> $$
> f(t_0,\dots,t_{n-1});
> $$
> 3. no elements except those forced by iterating the preceding clauses finitely many times.

> [!definition] Definition 3.8: Variable Term and Nullary Term
> For $x\in X$, the element $\eta_X(x)\in T_{\Omega}(X)$ is a variable term. For $c\in\Omega_0$, the element $c()\in T_{\Omega}(X)$ is a nullary-symbol term. The former is controlled by the generator set; the latter is controlled by the signature.

> [!notation] Notation 3.9: Display of Formal Terms
> The term $\eta_X(x)$ is often displayed as $x$ after the injection $\eta_X$ has been fixed. The formal constructor value $f^{T}(t_0,\dots,t_{n-1})$ is displayed as
> $$
> f(t_0,\dots,t_{n-1}).
> $$
> This notation denotes syntax formation, not application of an interpreted operation in a target algebra.

> [!warning] Warning 3.10: Same Glyph, Different Layer
> The expression $f(t_0,\dots,t_{n-1})$ on the syntax side denotes a formal term constructor. The expression $f^{\mathbf A}(a_0,\dots,a_{n-1})$ denotes the value of an interpreted operation in an algebra $\mathbf A$. Suppressing the superscript is permissible only when the layer is fixed.

### 3.3. The Term Algebra

> [!construction] Construction 3.11: Term Algebra
> The term algebra over $X$ is the $\Omega$-algebra
> $$
> \mathbf T_{\Omega}(X)
> $$
> with carrier $T_{\Omega}(X)$ and interpretations
> $$
> f^{\mathbf T_{\Omega}(X)}(t_0,\dots,t_{n-1})
> =
> f(t_0,\dots,t_{n-1})
> $$
> for each $f\in\Omega_n$.

> [!proposition] Proposition 3.12: Generatedness of the Term Algebra
> The carrier of $\mathbf T_{\Omega}(X)$ is generated by the variable terms $\eta_X[X]$. Nullary operation symbols are included automatically as values of nullary operations of the algebra. Thus
> $$
> \langle \eta_X[X]\rangle_{\mathbf T_{\Omega}(X)}=T_{\Omega}(X).
> $$

> [!theorem] Theorem 3.13: Term Algebra Freeness
> For every set $X$, the pair
> $$
> (\mathbf T_{\Omega}(X),\eta_X)
> $$
> is a free $\Omega$-algebra on $X$. For every $\Omega$-algebra $\mathbf A$ and every function $g:X\to |\mathbf A|$, there exists a unique homomorphism
> $$
> \overline g:\mathbf T_{\Omega}(X)\to\mathbf A
> $$
> with $\overline g\circ\eta_X=g$.

> [!corollary] Corollary 3.14: Canonical Representation of Free Algebras
> If $(\mathbf F_X,\eta'_X)$ is any free $\Omega$-algebra on $X$, then there is a unique isomorphism
> $$
> \mathbf T_{\Omega}(X)\cong\mathbf F_X
> $$
> commuting with the generator maps from $X$.

### 3.4. Formal Application Versus Interpretation

> [!definition] Definition 3.15: Syntax-Side Equality
> Equality in $T_{\Omega}(X)$ is equality of formal terms. It is generated by the constructors used to build the terms and does not identify two distinct formal constructor expressions unless the construction of $T_{\Omega}(X)$ does so by definitional equality.

> [!definition] Definition 3.16: Algebra-Side Equality
> Equality in an $\Omega$-algebra $\mathbf A$ is equality in its carrier $|\mathbf A|$. Distinct formal terms may evaluate to the same element of $|\mathbf A|$ under a homomorphic extension.

> [!proposition] Proposition 3.17: Constructor Injectivity in the Term Algebra
> In $\mathbf T_{\Omega}(X)$, if
> $$
> f(t_0,\dots,t_{m-1})=g(s_0,\dots,s_{n-1}),
> $$
> where $f\in\Omega_m$ and $g\in\Omega_n$, then $f=g$, $m=n$, and $t_i=s_i$ for every $i<n$. Variable terms are not equal to formal constructor terms, after variables and operation-symbol constructors have been disjointly tagged.

> [!warning] Warning 3.18: Collapse Under Interpretation
> In a concrete algebra $\mathbf A$, it may happen that
> $$
> f^{\mathbf A}(a_0,\dots,a_{m-1})=g^{\mathbf A}(b_0,\dots,b_{n-1})
> $$
> even when $f\ne g$ or the input tuples differ. Such collapse is semantic identification, not equality of formal syntax.

### 3.5. Universal Mapping Property of the Term Algebra

> [!construction] Construction 3.19: Evaluation Extension from a Generator Assignment
> Let $\mathbf A$ be an $\Omega$-algebra and let $g:X\to |\mathbf A|$. The unique homomorphic extension
> $$
> \overline g:\mathbf T_{\Omega}(X)\to\mathbf A
> $$
> is determined by the recursive clauses
> $$
> \overline g(\eta_X(x))=g(x)
> $$
> for $x\in X$, and
> $$
> \overline g(f(t_0,\dots,t_{n-1}))
> =
> f^{\mathbf A}(\overline g(t_0),\dots,\overline g(t_{n-1}))
> $$
> for $f\in\Omega_n$.

> [!theorem] Theorem 3.20: Existence and Uniqueness of Evaluation
> The clauses in Construction 3.19 determine exactly one homomorphism $\overline g:\mathbf T_{\Omega}(X)\to\mathbf A$. Any homomorphism $h:\mathbf T_{\Omega}(X)\to\mathbf A$ satisfying $h\circ\eta_X=g$ is equal to $\overline g$.

> [!remark] Remark 3.21: Evaluation Is Representation-Independent
> If $\mathbf R$ is another presentation of the free algebra on $X$ with generator map $\rho_X:X\to |\mathbf R|$ and isomorphism $\alpha:\mathbf T_{\Omega}(X)\to\mathbf R$ satisfying $\alpha\circ\eta_X=\rho_X$, then evaluation from $\mathbf R$ into $\mathbf A$ is $\overline g\circ\alpha^{-1}$.

### 3.6. Equivalent Characterizations of Freeness

> [!theorem] Theorem 3.22: Equivalent Characterizations of a Free Syntax Algebra
> Let $\mathbf S$ be an $\Omega$-algebra and let $\eta:X\to |\mathbf S|$. Under the finitary term-formation hypotheses above, the following conditions are equivalent when the displayed constructor maps on $\mathbf S$ are the interpretations of the $\Omega$-operations and $\eta[X]$ is disjointly specified as the generator image:
> 
> 1. $(\mathbf S,\eta)$ is free on $X$.
> 2. For every $\Omega$-algebra $\mathbf A$ and function $g:X\to |\mathbf A|$, there exists a unique homomorphism $\mathbf S\to\mathbf A$ extending $g$.
> 3. The canonical comparison homomorphism
> $$
> \chi:\mathbf T_{\Omega}(X)\to\mathbf S
> $$
> extending $\eta$ is an isomorphism.
> 4. The kernel of $\chi$ is $\Delta_{T_{\Omega}(X)}$ and $\chi$ is surjective.
> 5. The constructor maps in $\mathbf S$, together with the generator map, give unique readability: every element of $|\mathbf S|$ is uniquely a generator occurrence or a formal operation-symbol application to uniquely determined immediate components.
> 6. Structural recursion on $\mathbf S$ is uniquely determined by generator clauses and operation-symbol clauses.

> [!warning] Warning 3.23: Generated Does Not Imply Free
> The condition $\langle\eta[X]\rangle_{\mathbf S}=|\mathbf S|$ gives surjectivity of the comparison map from the term algebra. Freeness further requires injectivity of that comparison map, equivalently trivial kernel, equivalently absence of nontrivial syntax identifications.


## 4. Representations of the Syntax Algebra

### 4.1. Representations and Transport of Structure

> [!definition] Definition 4.1: Concrete Presentation of a Free Algebra
> Let $(\mathbf F_X,\eta_X)$ be a free $\Omega$-algebra. A concrete presentation of it consists of an $\Omega$-algebra $\mathbf R$, a map $\rho_X:X\to |\mathbf R|$, and an isomorphism
> $$
> \alpha:\mathbf F_X\to\mathbf R
> $$
> satisfying
> $$
> \alpha\circ\eta_X=\rho_X.
> $$

> [!construction] Construction 4.2: Transport of Algebra Structure Along a Bijection
> Let $\mathbf A$ be an $\Omega$-algebra and let $\beta:|\mathbf A|\to R$ be a bijection. The transported $\Omega$-algebra $\beta_*(\mathbf A)$ has carrier $R$ and operations
> $$
> f^{\beta_*(\mathbf A)}(r_0,\dots,r_{n-1})
> =
> \beta\left(f^{\mathbf A}(\beta^{-1}(r_0),\dots,\beta^{-1}(r_{n-1}))\right).
> $$
> The map $\beta:\mathbf A\to\beta_*(\mathbf A)$ is an isomorphism.

> [!warning] Warning 4.3: Isomorphic Presentation Versus Literal Identity
> A tree presentation and a tuple presentation may be connected by a bijective homomorphism. This does not make the tree carrier literally equal to the tuple carrier. Any statement transferring operations, equality, recursion, or parsing across presentations must specify the relevant isomorphism or identification convention.

### 4.2. Tree Presentation

> [!definition] Definition 4.4: Address Space
> Let
> $$
> \mathbb N_{>0}^{<\omega}
> $$
> be the set of all finite sequences of positive natural numbers. The empty sequence is denoted $\epsilon$ and is called the root address. Concatenation of addresses $u$ and $v$ is denoted $uv$.

> [!definition] Definition 4.5: Prefix Order and Immediate Children
> For $u,v\in\mathbb N_{>0}^{<\omega}$, write $u\preceq v$ when there exists $w\in\mathbb N_{>0}^{<\omega}$ such that $v=uw$. If $v=ui$ for some $i\in\mathbb N_{>0}$, then $v$ is the $i$-th immediate child address of $u$.

> [!definition] Definition 4.6: Finite Tree Domain
> A finite tree domain is a finite subset $D\subseteq\mathbb N_{>0}^{<\omega}$ satisfying:
> 
> 1. $\epsilon\in D$;
> 2. if $v\in D$ and $u\preceq v$, then $u\in D$;
> 3. for each $u\in D$, the set
> $$
> \{i\in\mathbb N_{>0}:ui\in D\}
> $$
> is an initial segment $\{1,\dots,k\}$ for some $k\in\mathbb N$.

> [!definition] Definition 4.7: Arity-Correct Labelled Term Tree
> Let $\Omega$ be a finitary signature and let $X$ be a set. An arity-correct labelled term tree over $(\Omega,X)$ is a pair $(D,\ell)$ where $D$ is a finite tree domain and
> $$
> \ell:D\to X\amalg\Omega
> $$
> satisfies:
> 
> 1. if $\ell(u)\in X$, then $u$ has no children;
> 2. if $\ell(u)=f\in\Omega_n$, then the children of $u$ are exactly $u1,\dots,un$.

> [!construction] Construction 4.8: Tree Constructors
> For $x\in X$, define the leaf tree $\eta^{\mathrm{tr}}(x)$ with domain $\{\epsilon\}$ and label $x$ at $\epsilon$. For $f\in\Omega_n$ and trees $\tau_0,\dots,\tau_{n-1}$, define
> $$
> N_f(\tau_0,\dots,\tau_{n-1})
> $$
> to be the tree with root labelled $f$ and whose $i+1$-st immediate subtree is $\tau_i$.

> [!definition] Definition 4.9: Tree Algebra of Terms
> The tree algebra $\mathbf{Tr}_{\Omega}(X)$ has as carrier the set of all finite arity-correct labelled term trees over $(\Omega,X)$ and interprets each $f\in\Omega_n$ by the constructor map
> $$
> N_f:\operatorname{Tr}_{\Omega}(X)^n\to\operatorname{Tr}_{\Omega}(X).
> $$

> [!theorem] Theorem 4.10: Tree Presentation Theorem
> The algebra $\mathbf{Tr}_{\Omega}(X)$ with generator map $x\mapsto\eta^{\mathrm{tr}}(x)$ is a free $\Omega$-algebra on $X$. Hence there is a unique isomorphism
> $$
> \mathbf T_{\Omega}(X)\cong\mathbf{Tr}_{\Omega}(X)
> $$
> commuting with the generator maps.

### 4.3. Tuple and Tagged-Tuple Presentation

> [!definition] Definition 4.11: Tagged Recursive Tuple Carrier
> Let $X$ and $\Omega$ be disjoint after tagging. A tagged tuple term over $(\Omega,X)$ is generated by:
> 
> 1. variable codes $({\mathtt{var}},x)$ for $x\in X$;
> 2. operation codes $({\mathtt{op}},f,(r_0,\dots,r_{n-1}))$ for $f\in\Omega_n$ and previously generated tuple terms $r_i$.

> [!construction] Construction 4.12: Tuple Algebra
> The tuple algebra $\mathbf{Tu}_{\Omega}(X)$ has carrier the set of tagged tuple terms. For $f\in\Omega_n$, define
> $$
> f^{\mathbf{Tu}_{\Omega}(X)}(r_0,\dots,r_{n-1})=({\mathtt{op}},f,(r_0,\dots,r_{n-1})).
> $$
> The generator map sends $x$ to $({\mathtt{var}},x)$.

> [!proposition] Proposition 4.13: Constructor Injectivity by Tagging
> In the tuple algebra, variable codes and operation codes are disjoint. If
> $$
> ({\mathtt{op}},f,(r_0,\dots,r_{m-1}))=({\mathtt{op}},g,(s_0,\dots,s_{n-1})),
> $$
> then $f=g$, $m=n$, and $r_i=s_i$ for every $i<n$.

> [!theorem] Theorem 4.14: Tuple Presentation Theorem
> The tuple algebra $\mathbf{Tu}_{\Omega}(X)$ is a free $\Omega$-algebra on $X$. It is uniquely isomorphic to both $\mathbf T_{\Omega}(X)$ and $\mathbf{Tr}_{\Omega}(X)$ over $X$.

### 4.4. String Presentation

> [!definition] Definition 4.15: Formal String Alphabet
> A string presentation of $\Omega$-terms over $X$ uses an alphabet
> $$
> \Sigma=X\amalg\Omega\amalg D
> $$
> where $D$ is a set of delimiter symbols sufficient to encode arities and grouping, such as left parenthesis, right parenthesis, comma, and optional arity tags.

> [!definition] Definition 4.16: Well-Formed Fully Parenthesized Strings
> A fully parenthesized string representation is a subset
> $$
> W_{\Omega}(X)\subseteq\Sigma^{<\omega}
> $$
> generated by:
> 
> 1. each $x\in X$ is a well-formed string;
> 2. for $f\in\Omega_0$, the symbol string for $f$ is well-formed;
> 3. for $f\in\Omega_n$ with $n>0$ and well-formed strings $s_0,\dots,s_{n-1}$, the concatenation
> $$
> f(s_0,\dots,s_{n-1})
> $$
> with explicit delimiters is well-formed;
> 4. no other strings are well-formed.

> [!construction] Construction 4.17: String Algebra
> If the constructor operation
> $$
> C_f:W_{\Omega}(X)^n\to W_{\Omega}(X)
> $$
> sends $(s_0,\dots,s_{n-1})$ to the delimited string $f(s_0,\dots,s_{n-1})$, then $W_{\Omega}(X)$ becomes an $\Omega$-algebra by interpreting $f$ as $C_f$.

> [!warning] Warning 4.18: Ambiguity Risks in String Presentations
> A string notation fails to present the term algebra when different formal terms have the same string or when a well-formed string has more than one parse tree. Infix notation without sufficient precedence, associativity conventions, delimiters, or arity information may not be injective as a representation of formal syntax.

> [!theorem] Theorem 4.19: Parenthesized String Presentation
> If the alphabet, delimiter system, and formation rules make the constructor maps $C_f$ injective with pairwise disjoint ranges and make variables disjoint from constructor ranges, then the resulting string algebra is free on $X$ and is uniquely isomorphic to $\mathbf T_{\Omega}(X)$ over $X$.

### 4.5. Flattening and Parsing

> [!construction] Construction 4.20: Flattening Map
> Let $\mathbf{Tr}_{\Omega}(X)$ be the tree presentation and let $W_{\Omega}(X)$ be a chosen string presentation. A flattening map is a function
> $$
> \operatorname{flat}:\operatorname{Tr}_{\Omega}(X)\to W_{\Omega}(X)
> $$
> recursively defined by a chosen traversal and delimiter convention.

> [!definition] Definition 4.21: Parser
> A parser for $W_{\Omega}(X)$ relative to $\operatorname{flat}$ is a function
> $$
> \operatorname{parse}:W_{\Omega}(X)\to\operatorname{Tr}_{\Omega}(X)
> $$
> satisfying
> $$
> \operatorname{parse}\circ\operatorname{flat}=\operatorname{id}_{\operatorname{Tr}_{\Omega}(X)}
> $$
> and
> $$
> \operatorname{flat}\circ\operatorname{parse}=\operatorname{id}_{W_{\Omega}(X)}.
> $$

> [!theorem] Theorem 4.22: Bijective Tree-String Correspondence
> A flattening map is a bijective presentation of term trees by well-formed strings if and only if it is injective and its image is exactly $W_{\Omega}(X)$. In that case its inverse is a parser and transports the tree algebra structure to the string algebra.

> [!warning] Warning 4.23: Parsing Is Extra Structure
> Abstract freeness of the term algebra entails unique decomposition in the abstract algebraic presentation. It does not supply a machine procedure for parsing arbitrary strings unless the string presentation includes a decidable well-formedness condition and a computable inverse to flattening.

### 4.6. Unique Readability Across Presentations

> [!definition] Definition 4.24: Representation Map
> Let $\mathbf R$ be a concrete $\Omega$-algebra with generator map $\rho:X\to |\mathbf R|$. The representation map from syntax is the unique homomorphism
> $$
> \rho^\sharp:\mathbf T_{\Omega}(X)\to\mathbf R
> $$
> satisfying
> $$
> \rho^\sharp(\eta_X(x))=\rho(x)
> $$
> for every $x\in X$.

> [!definition] Definition 4.25: Representation-Dependent Unique Readability
> The representation $\mathbf R$ has unique readability over $X$ when $\rho^\sharp$ is an isomorphism. Equivalently, $\rho^\sharp$ is surjective, has trivial kernel, and preserves the constructor decomposition of terms without identifying distinct constructor expressions.

> [!proposition] Proposition 4.26: Presentation Criteria
> For a concrete $\Omega$-algebra $\mathbf R$ with generator map $\rho:X\to |\mathbf R|$, the following are equivalent:
> 
> 1. $\mathbf R$ is a presentation of the free syntax algebra on $X$.
> 2. The representation map $\rho^\sharp$ is an isomorphism.
> 3. $\rho^\sharp$ is surjective and $\ker(\rho^\sharp)=\Delta_{T_{\Omega}(X)}$.
> 4. Each element of $|\mathbf R|$ has exactly one finite constructor decomposition from $\rho[X]$ and the nullary operation interpretations.

> [!remark] Remark 4.27: Invariance and Dependence
> Unique readability of the abstract term algebra is representation-independent. Computable parsing, memory layout, equality testing, and textual ambiguity are representation-dependent.


## 5. Trees as the Structural Presentation of Terms

### 5.1. Addressed Trees

> [!definition] Definition 5.1: Addressed Tree
> An addressed tree is a pair $(D,\ell)$ where $D\subseteq\mathbb N_{>0}^{<\omega}$ is a finite tree domain and $\ell$ is a labelling function from $D$ into a specified label set. Equality of addressed trees is equality of domains and equality of labelling functions.

> [!definition] Definition 5.2: Sibling Order
> If $u1,\dots,uk$ are the children of an address $u$, their sibling order is the order induced by
> $$
> u1<_{\mathrm{sib}}u2<_{\mathrm{sib}}\cdots<_{\mathrm{sib}}uk.
> $$
> This order is part of the data of ordered trees.

> [!definition] Definition 5.3: Arity-Correct Domain Relative to Labels
> Let $\ell:D\to X\amalg\Omega$. The domain $D$ is arity-correct relative to $\ell$ if every node labelled by $x\in X$ has zero children and every node labelled by $f\in\Omega_n$ has exactly $n$ ordered children.

### 5.2. Ranked Trees

> [!definition] Definition 5.4: Ranked Alphabet
> A ranked alphabet is a set $L$ equipped with a rank function
> $$
> r:L\to\mathbb N.
> $$
> A ranked tree over $L$ is a finite addressed labelled tree $(D,\ell)$ such that the number of children of every $u\in D$ is $r(\ell(u))$.

> [!construction] Construction 5.5: Term Labels as Ranked Alphabet
> Given $\Omega$ and $X$, define a ranked alphabet
> $$
> L_{\Omega,X}=X\amalg\Omega
> $$
> by $r(x)=0$ for $x\in X$ and $r(f)=\operatorname{ar}(f)$ for $f\in\Omega$. The ranked trees over $L_{\Omega,X}$ are exactly the arity-correct term trees over $(\Omega,X)$.

> [!definition] Definition 5.6: Leaf and Internal Node
> In a ranked tree $(D,\ell)$, an address $u\in D$ is a leaf if it has no children. It is internal if it has at least one child. In term trees, variables and nullary operation symbols label leaves; operation symbols of positive arity label internal nodes.

### 5.3. Tree Constructors

> [!construction] Construction 5.7: Leaf Constructors and Nullary Node Constructors
> For $x\in X$, the variable leaf constructor is
> $$
> \eta_x=\eta^{\mathrm{tr}}(x).
> $$
> For $c\in\Omega_0$, the nullary node constructor is
> $$
> N_c()
> $$
> whose one-node tree has root label $c$. Variable leaves and nullary operation leaves are distinct when $X$ and $\Omega_0$ are disjointly tagged.

> [!construction] Construction 5.8: Positive-Arity Node Constructor
> For $f\in\Omega_n$ with $n>0$, define
> $$
> N_f:\operatorname{Tr}_{\Omega}(X)^n\to\operatorname{Tr}_{\Omega}(X)
> $$
> by adjoining a new root labelled $f$ and translating the domain of the $i$-th input tree below child address $i+1$.

> [!proposition] Proposition 5.9: Constructor Injectivity and Disjointness
> In the tree presentation:
> 
> 1. variable leaves are pairwise distinct;
> 2. variable leaves are not nullary operation nodes;
> 3. variable leaves are not positive-arity operation nodes;
> 4. if $f\ne g$, then the ranges of $N_f$ and $N_g$ are disjoint;
> 5. if $N_f(\tau_0,\dots,\tau_{n-1})=N_f(\sigma_0,\dots,\sigma_{n-1})$, then $\tau_i=\sigma_i$ for every $i<n$.

### 5.4. Unique Decomposition

> [!theorem] Theorem 5.10: Unique Decomposition of Term Trees
> Every tree $\tau\in\operatorname{Tr}_{\Omega}(X)$ is exactly one of the following:
> 
> 1. a variable leaf $\eta^{\mathrm{tr}}(x)$ for a unique $x\in X$;
> 2. a nullary operation node $N_c()$ for a unique $c\in\Omega_0$;
> 3. a positive-arity node $N_f(\tau_0,\dots,\tau_{n-1})$ for a unique $f\in\Omega_n$ with $n>0$ and a unique tuple $(\tau_0,\dots,\tau_{n-1})$ of immediate subtrees.

> [!corollary] Corollary 5.11: Tree Equality by Recursive Decomposition
> Two term trees are equal if and only if their unique decompositions have the same outer case, the same root variable or operation symbol, and recursively equal corresponding immediate subtrees.

> [!remark] Remark 5.12: Structural Equality
> Structural equality of addressed trees is not semantic equality after evaluation. It is equality of the syntactic tree object itself.

### 5.5. Subtree Operations

> [!definition] Definition 5.13: Subtree at an Address
> Let $\tau=(D,\ell)$ be an addressed tree and let $u\in D$. The subtree of $\tau$ at $u$, denoted $\tau|_u$, has domain
> $$
> D_u=\{v\in\mathbb N_{>0}^{<\omega}:uv\in D\}
> $$
> and labelling function
> $$
> \ell_u(v)=\ell(uv).
> $$

> [!definition] Definition 5.14: Immediate and Proper Subtrees
> If $u1,\dots,uk$ are the children of the root $\epsilon$, then the immediate subtrees of $\tau$ are $\tau|_1,\dots,\tau|_k$. A proper subtree is $\tau|_u$ for some non-root address $u\ne\epsilon$.

> [!construction] Construction 5.15: Replacement of a Subtree
> Let $\tau=(D,\ell)$ be an addressed tree, let $u\in D$, and let $\sigma$ be a tree whose root label has the same sort requirements when sorts are present. The replacement
> $$
> \tau[u:=\sigma]
> $$
> is obtained by deleting all nodes strictly below $u$ in $\tau$, retaining $u$, and inserting a translated copy of $\sigma$ at address $u$.

> [!construction] Construction 5.16: Contexts and Grafting
> A one-hole context over $(\Omega,X)$ is a term tree over the extended generator set $X\amalg\{\Box\}$ in which the distinguished symbol $\Box$ occurs exactly once as a leaf. If $C$ is a context and $\tau$ is a term tree, the grafting operation $C[\tau]$ replaces the unique $\Box$-leaf by $\tau$.

> [!proposition] Proposition 5.17: Context Closure of Term Equality
> Let $C$ be a one-hole context and let $\tau,\sigma\in\operatorname{Tr}_{\Omega}(X)$. If $\tau=\sigma$, then $C[\tau]=C[\sigma]$. For quotient syntax, if $\theta$ is a congruence and $(\tau,\sigma)\in\theta$, then $(C[\tau],C[\sigma])\in\theta$.

### 5.6. Height and Subtree Descent

> [!definition] Definition 5.18: Depth and Height
> Let $\tau=(D,\ell)$ be a finite addressed tree. The depth of an address $u=(i_1,\dots,i_k)$ is $k$. The height of $\tau$ is
> $$
> \operatorname{ht}(\tau)=\max\{\operatorname{depth}(u):u\in D\}.
> $$
> The height of a node $u\in D$ is the height of the subtree $\tau|_u$.

> [!definition] Definition 5.19: Immediate-Subtree and Proper-Subtree Relations
> On $\operatorname{Tr}_{\Omega}(X)$, define $\sigma\prec_1\tau$ when $\sigma$ is an immediate subtree of $\tau$. Define $\sigma\prec\tau$ when $\sigma$ is a proper subtree of $\tau$.

> [!theorem] Theorem 5.20: Well-Foundedness of Subtree Descent
> The relation $\prec$ of proper-subtree descent on finite term trees is well-founded. Equivalently, there is no infinite sequence
> $$
> \tau_0\succ\tau_1\succ\tau_2\succ\cdots
> $$
> where each $\tau_{i+1}$ is a proper subtree of $\tau_i$.

> [!corollary] Corollary 5.21: Induction on Height
> Let $P$ be a predicate on $\operatorname{Tr}_{\Omega}(X)$. If $P(\tau)$ follows whenever $P$ holds for all immediate subtrees of $\tau$, including the vacuous cases of variable leaves and nullary nodes, then $P$ holds for every term tree.

### 5.7. Structural Induction and Structural Recursion on Trees

> [!theorem] Theorem 5.22: Structural Induction on Trees
> Let $P$ be a predicate on $\operatorname{Tr}_{\Omega}(X)$. Suppose:
> 
> 1. $P(\eta^{\mathrm{tr}}(x))$ holds for every $x\in X$;
> 2. $P(N_c())$ holds for every $c\in\Omega_0$;
> 3. for every $f\in\Omega_n$ with $n>0$, if $P(\tau_i)$ holds for all $i<n$, then
> $$
> P(N_f(\tau_0,\dots,\tau_{n-1}))
> $$
> holds.
> 
> Then $P(\tau)$ holds for every $\tau\in\operatorname{Tr}_{\Omega}(X)$.

> [!theorem] Theorem 5.23: Structural Recursion on Trees
> Let $Y$ be a set. Suppose data are given by functions
> $$
> b_X:X\to Y,
> $$
> elements $b_c\in Y$ for every $c\in\Omega_0$, and functions
> $$
> R_f:Y^n\to Y
> $$
> for every $f\in\Omega_n$ with $n>0$. There exists a unique function
> $$
> H:\operatorname{Tr}_{\Omega}(X)\to Y
> $$
> satisfying
> $$
> H(\eta^{\mathrm{tr}}(x))=b_X(x),
> $$
> $$
> H(N_c())=b_c,
> $$
> and
> $$
> H(N_f(\tau_0,\dots,\tau_{n-1}))=R_f(H(\tau_0),\dots,H(\tau_{n-1})).
> $$

> [!remark] Remark 5.24: Tree Recursion as Concrete Free-Algebra Recursion
> Structural recursion on trees is the concrete tree-presentation form of the unique homomorphic extension property of the free algebra. When the codomain $Y$ carries an $\Omega$-algebra structure with $R_f=f^{\mathbf Y}$, the recursive map is a homomorphism.


## 6. Evaluation into Concrete Algebras

### 6.1. Concrete Target Algebras and Valuations

> [!definition] Definition 6.1: Concrete Target Algebra
> A concrete target algebra for syntax over $\Omega$ is an $\Omega$-algebra
> $$
> \mathbf B=(|\mathbf B|,(f^{\mathbf B})_{f\in\Omega}).
> $$
> Its carrier $|\mathbf B|$ is the semantic domain, and each $f^{\mathbf B}$ is an interpreted operation on that domain.

> [!definition] Definition 6.2: Valuation or Generator Assignment
> Let $X$ be the set of formal generators. A valuation of $X$ in $\mathbf B$ is a function
> $$
> g:X\to |\mathbf B|.
> $$
> It assigns semantic values to formal generators. It does not assign interpretations to operation symbols; those are already fixed by the algebra $\mathbf B$.

### 6.2. Evaluation Homomorphism

> [!construction] Construction 6.3: Evaluation Homomorphism
> Given $g:X\to |\mathbf B|$, define
> $$
> \operatorname{ev}_g:\mathbf T_{\Omega}(X)\to\mathbf B
> $$
> to be the unique homomorphism extending $g$. It satisfies
> $$
> \operatorname{ev}_g(\eta_X(x))=g(x)
> $$
> and
> $$
> \operatorname{ev}_g(f(t_0,\dots,t_{n-1}))
> =
> f^{\mathbf B}(\operatorname{ev}_g(t_0),\dots,\operatorname{ev}_g(t_{n-1})).
> $$

> [!proposition] Proposition 6.4: Evaluation on Representations
> Let $\mathbf R$ be a concrete presentation of $\mathbf T_{\Omega}(X)$ via an isomorphism $\alpha:\mathbf T_{\Omega}(X)\to\mathbf R$. Evaluation on $\mathbf R$ is the homomorphism
> $$
> \operatorname{ev}^{\mathbf R}_g=\operatorname{ev}_g\circ\alpha^{-1}:\mathbf R\to\mathbf B.
> $$
> Thus tree evaluation, tuple evaluation, and string evaluation agree after transport along the chosen presentation isomorphism.

> [!warning] Warning 6.5: Evaluation Is Not Parsing
> The homomorphism $\operatorname{ev}_g$ is defined on terms. If input data are strings, a parsing map into a term presentation is required before evaluation is applicable.

### 6.3. Image as Generated Subalgebra

> [!theorem] Theorem 6.6: Evaluation Image Theorem
> Let $\mathbf B$ be an $\Omega$-algebra and let $g:X\to |\mathbf B|$. Then
> $$
> \operatorname{im}(\operatorname{ev}_g)=\langle g[X]\rangle_{\mathbf B}
> $$
> as subalgebras of $\mathbf B$.

> [!corollary] Corollary 6.7: Concrete Generated Algebra as Homomorphic Image
> The subalgebra of $\mathbf B$ generated by $g[X]$ is a homomorphic image of the free algebra:
> $$
> \mathbf T_{\Omega}(X)\twoheadrightarrow\langle g[X]\rangle_{\mathbf B}.
> $$
> The displayed map is $\operatorname{ev}_g$ with codomain restricted to its image.

> [!remark] Remark 6.8: Generatedness as Surjectivity
> The statement that $\langle g[X]\rangle_{\mathbf B}$ is generated by $g[X]$ is exactly the statement that the restricted evaluation map
> $$
> \operatorname{ev}_g:\mathbf T_{\Omega}(X)\to\langle g[X]\rangle_{\mathbf B}
> $$
> is surjective.

### 6.4. Kernel of Evaluation

> [!definition] Definition 6.9: Semantic Kernel of a Valuation
> The kernel of evaluation under $g$ is
> $$
> \ker(\operatorname{ev}_g)=\{(s,t)\in T_{\Omega}(X)^2:\operatorname{ev}_g(s)=\operatorname{ev}_g(t)\}.
> $$
> It is a congruence on $\mathbf T_{\Omega}(X)$.

> [!definition] Definition 6.10: Semantic Equality Relative to a Valuation
> Two terms $s,t\in T_{\Omega}(X)$ are semantically equal relative to $g$ in $\mathbf B$ when
> $$
> (s,t)\in\ker(\operatorname{ev}_g).
> $$
> This relation depends on the target algebra and on the valuation.

> [!warning] Warning 6.11: Accidental Identifications
> A nontrivial kernel of evaluation records identifications imposed by the target algebra and valuation. Such identifications need not be visible from the syntax alone. If $s\ne t$ in $T_{\Omega}(X)$ but $\operatorname{ev}_g(s)=\operatorname{ev}_g(t)$, then evaluation has collapsed distinct syntax.

> [!proposition] Proposition 6.12: Trivial Kernel and Faithful Syntax
> The evaluation map $\operatorname{ev}_g:\mathbf T_{\Omega}(X)\to\langle g[X]\rangle_{\mathbf B}$ is injective if and only if
> $$
> \ker(\operatorname{ev}_g)=\Delta_{T_{\Omega}(X)}.
> $$
> In that case each element of $\langle g[X]\rangle_{\mathbf B}$ has a unique representing term.

### 6.5. Quotient Description of the Generated Subalgebra

> [!theorem] Theorem 6.13: Quotient Description of a Generated Subalgebra
> Let $\mathbf B$ be an $\Omega$-algebra and let $g:X\to |\mathbf B|$. Then there is a canonical isomorphism
> $$
> \mathbf T_{\Omega}(X)/\ker(\operatorname{ev}_g)
> \cong
> \langle g[X]\rangle_{\mathbf B}
> $$
> sending $[t]_{\ker(\operatorname{ev}_g)}$ to $\operatorname{ev}_g(t)$.

> [!construction] Construction 6.14: Canonical Evaluation Factorization
> The evaluation homomorphism factors as
> $$
> \mathbf T_{\Omega}(X)
> \xrightarrow{q_{\ker(\operatorname{ev}_g)}}
> \mathbf T_{\Omega}(X)/\ker(\operatorname{ev}_g)
> \xrightarrow{\cong}
> \langle g[X]\rangle_{\mathbf B}
> \xhookrightarrow{}
> \mathbf B.
> $$

> [!remark] Remark 6.15: Syntax Modulo Semantic Identification
> A concrete generated subalgebra may be understood as free syntax modulo exactly the equations that hold under evaluation. The quotient does not discard syntax arbitrarily; it identifies precisely those terms with equal semantic value.

### 6.6. Freeness of Concrete Generated Subalgebras

> [!definition] Definition 6.16: Concrete Freely Generated Subalgebra
> Let $g:X\to |\mathbf B|$ and let $\mathbf C=\langle g[X]\rangle_{\mathbf B}$. The subalgebra $\mathbf C$ is freely generated by $g[X]$ indexed by $X$ when the restricted evaluation map
> $$
> \operatorname{ev}_g:\mathbf T_{\Omega}(X)\to\mathbf C
> $$
> is an isomorphism.

> [!theorem] Theorem 6.17: Equivalent Criteria for Concrete Freeness
> With $\mathbf C=\langle g[X]\rangle_{\mathbf B}$, the following are equivalent:
> 
> 1. $\mathbf C$ is freely generated by $g[X]$ indexed by $X$.
> 2. The restricted evaluation map $\operatorname{ev}_g:\mathbf T_{\Omega}(X)\to\mathbf C$ is bijective.
> 3. The restricted evaluation map is injective.
> 4. $\ker(\operatorname{ev}_g)=\Delta_{T_{\Omega}(X)}$.
> 5. Every element of $|\mathbf C|$ has a unique representing term in $T_{\Omega}(X)$.
> 6. Internal recursive definitions on $\mathbf C$ are well-defined by term decomposition without quotient-compatibility checks.

> [!warning] Warning 6.18: Internal Decomposition May Fail
> Even when $\mathbf C$ is generated by $g[X]$, it may fail to have internal unique decomposition. For example, in a monoid generated by $a$, the words $x^2x$ and $xx^2$ may evaluate to the same element if the target monoid imposes an associativity or commutativity identification not present in the raw term algebra of a nonassociative signature.


## 7. Recursion and Induction from Freeness

### 7.1. Structural Induction on Terms

> [!theorem] Theorem 7.1: Structural Induction on Terms
> Let $P$ be a predicate on $T_{\Omega}(X)$. Suppose:
> 
> 1. $P(\eta_X(x))$ holds for every $x\in X$;
> 2. for every $c\in\Omega_0$, $P(c())$ holds;
> 3. for every $f\in\Omega_n$ with $n>0$, if $P(t_i)$ holds for each $i<n$, then
> $$
> P(f(t_0,\dots,t_{n-1}))
> $$
> holds.
> 
> Then $P(t)$ holds for every $t\in T_{\Omega}(X)$.

> [!proposition] Proposition 7.2: Transfer of Induction Across Presentations
> Let $\alpha:\mathbf T_{\Omega}(X)\to\mathbf R$ be an isomorphism of syntax presentations. An induction principle on $T_{\Omega}(X)$ transfers to $|\mathbf R|$ by replacing a predicate $P$ on $|\mathbf R|$ with $P\circ\alpha$ on $T_{\Omega}(X)$.

> [!warning] Warning 7.3: String Induction Requires Formation Data
> Induction on strings as syntax is valid only over the well-formed string set with a unique parse or constructor formation relation. Induction over all finite strings in the alphabet is not the same as induction over terms.

### 7.2. Structural Recursion on the Free Algebra

> [!definition] Definition 7.4: Recursion Data into a Set
> Let $Y$ be a set. Recursion data from $T_{\Omega}(X)$ into $Y$ consist of:
> 
> 1. a function $b:X\to Y$;
> 2. an element $r_c\in Y$ for each $c\in\Omega_0$;
> 3. for each $f\in\Omega_n$ with $n>0$, a function
> $$
> R_f:Y^n\to Y.
> $$

> [!theorem] Theorem 7.5: Set-Valued Structural Recursion
> Given recursion data into a set $Y$, there is a unique function
> $$
> H:T_{\Omega}(X)\to Y
> $$
> such that
> $$
> H(\eta_X(x))=b(x),
> $$
> $$
> H(c())=r_c,
> $$
> and
> $$
> H(f(t_0,\dots,t_{n-1}))=R_f(H(t_0),\dots,H(t_{n-1}))
> $$
> for every $f\in\Omega_n$ with $n>0$.

> [!definition] Definition 7.6: Recursion Data into an Algebra
> If $\mathbf A$ is an $\Omega$-algebra and $b:X\to |\mathbf A|$, the recursion data are algebraic when $r_c=c^{\mathbf A}$ for $c\in\Omega_0$ and $R_f=f^{\mathbf A}$ for every positive-arity $f$.

> [!corollary] Corollary 7.7: Algebra-Valued Recursion as Homomorphic Extension
> Algebraic recursion data determine the unique homomorphism
> $$
> \overline b:\mathbf T_{\Omega}(X)\to\mathbf A
> $$
> extending $b$.

> [!remark] Remark 7.8: Structural Recursion as Freeness
> The existence and uniqueness of structurally recursive definitions on the term algebra are equivalent to the free-algebra universal mapping property, after the codomain is equipped with the operations specified by the recursion clauses.

### 7.3. Recursion on Concrete Freely Generated Subalgebras

> [!definition] Definition 7.9: Internal Constructor Presentation
> Let $\mathbf C\leq\mathbf B$ be generated by $g[X]$. An internal constructor presentation of $\mathbf C$ consists of partial or total destructor data recovering, for each $c\in |\mathbf C|$, whether $c$ is a generator value, a nullary value, or an operation value with specified immediate predecessors.

> [!theorem] Theorem 7.10: Recursion on a Concrete Freely Generated Subalgebra
> If the restricted evaluation map
> $$
> \operatorname{ev}_g:\mathbf T_{\Omega}(X)\to\mathbf C
> $$
> is an isomorphism, then every structurally recursive definition on $T_{\Omega}(X)$ transports uniquely to a structurally recursive definition on $|\mathbf C|$ via
> $$
> H_{\mathbf C}=H\circ(\operatorname{ev}_g)^{-1}.
> $$

> [!warning] Warning 7.11: Failure of Recursion Under Nontrivial Kernel
> If $\ker(\operatorname{ev}_g)$ is nontrivial, a definition by term representatives descends to $\langle g[X]\rangle_{\mathbf B}$ only if it is constant on kernel classes. Without this invariance, the same concrete element may receive conflicting values.

### 7.4. Descent to Quotients

> [!definition] Definition 7.12: Quotient Syntax
> Let $\theta\in\operatorname{Con}(\mathbf T_{\Omega}(X))$. The quotient syntax algebra is
> $$
> \mathbf T_{\Omega}(X)/\theta.
> $$
> Its elements are equivalence classes of terms modulo $\theta$.

> [!theorem] Theorem 7.13: Descent Criterion for Functions on Representatives
> Let $H:T_{\Omega}(X)\to Y$ be a function into a set $Y$. There exists a unique function
> $$
> \widetilde H:T_{\Omega}(X)/\theta\to Y
> $$
> satisfying
> $$
> \widetilde H([t]_\theta)=H(t)
> $$
> for all $t$ if and only if
> $$
> \theta\subseteq\ker H,
> $$
> where
> $$
> \ker H=\{(s,t):H(s)=H(t)\}.
> $$

> [!theorem] Theorem 7.14: Descent Criterion for Homomorphisms
> Let $h:\mathbf T_{\Omega}(X)\to\mathbf A$ be a homomorphism and let $\theta\in\operatorname{Con}(\mathbf T_{\Omega}(X))$. There exists a unique homomorphism
> $$
> \widetilde h:\mathbf T_{\Omega}(X)/\theta\to\mathbf A
> $$
> with
> $$
> \widetilde h\circ q_\theta=h
> $$
> if and only if $\theta\subseteq\ker h$.

> [!definition] Definition 7.15: Recursion Modulo Equations
> Recursion modulo a congruence $\theta$ is a structurally recursive function on raw terms whose output is invariant under $\theta$. Equivalently, it is a function or homomorphism defined on the quotient algebra $\mathbf T_{\Omega}(X)/\theta$.

> [!warning] Warning 7.16: Free Recursion Versus Quotient Recursion
> Free recursion requires only clauses on constructors. Quotient recursion additionally requires compatibility with all equations imposed by the quotient congruence.


## 8. Presentations by Generators and Relations

### 8.1. Equational Presentations

> [!definition] Definition 8.1: Equation Over a Generator Set
> An equation over $X$ in signature $\Omega$ is an ordered pair
> $$
> (s,t)\in T_{\Omega}(X)^2.
> $$
> It is displayed as $s\approx t$. A set of equations is a subset
> $$
> E\subseteq T_{\Omega}(X)^2.
> $$

> [!construction] Construction 8.2: Congruence Generated by Equations
> Given $E\subseteq T_{\Omega}(X)^2$, define
> $$
> \theta_E=\operatorname{Cg}_{\mathbf T_{\Omega}(X)}(E).
> $$
> This is the least congruence on the term algebra containing every pair in $E$.

> [!definition] Definition 8.3: Presented Algebra
> The algebra presented by generators $X$ and equations $E$ is
> $$
> \langle X\mid E\rangle_{\Omega}
> =
> \mathbf T_{\Omega}(X)/\theta_E.
> $$
> If $E=\emptyset$, then
> $$
> \langle X\mid\emptyset\rangle_{\Omega}\cong\mathbf T_{\Omega}(X).
> $$

> [!theorem] Theorem 8.4: Universal Property of Presented Algebras
> Let $\mathbf A$ be an $\Omega$-algebra and let $g:X\to |\mathbf A|$. There exists a unique homomorphism
> $$
> \widehat g:\langle X\mid E\rangle_{\Omega}\to\mathbf A
> $$
> satisfying
> $$
> \widehat g([\eta_X(x)]_{\theta_E})=g(x)
> $$
> for every $x\in X$ if and only if
> $$
> E\subseteq\ker(\operatorname{ev}_g).
> $$
> Equivalently, every equation in $E$ holds under the valuation $g$ in $\mathbf A$.

### 8.2. Congruence Generation and Equational Closure

> [!definition] Definition 8.5: Contextual Closure
> Let $R\subseteq T_{\Omega}(X)^2$. Its contextual closure consists of all pairs
> $$
> (C[s],C[t])
> $$
> where $(s,t)\in R$ and $C$ is a one-hole term context over $X$.

> [!definition] Definition 8.6: Substitution Closure
> A relation $R\subseteq T_{\Omega}(X)^2$ is substitution-closed if for every substitution homomorphism
> $$
> \overline\sigma:\mathbf T_{\Omega}(X)\to\mathbf T_{\Omega}(X)
> $$
> and every $(s,t)\in R$, one has
> $$
> (\overline\sigma(s),\overline\sigma(t))\in R.
> $$

> [!proposition] Proposition 8.7: Congruence Generation Criteria
> The congruence $\theta_E$ is the least relation on $T_{\Omega}(X)$ containing $E$ that is reflexive, symmetric, transitive, and closed under all term constructors. Equivalently, it is the least equivalence relation containing $E$ and closed under all one-hole contexts.

> [!definition] Definition 8.8: Equality Modulo a Presentation
> Terms $s,t\in T_{\Omega}(X)$ are equal modulo the presentation $\langle X\mid E\rangle_{\Omega}$ when
> $$
> (s,t)\in\theta_E.
> $$
> The corresponding element of the presented algebra is the class $[s]_{\theta_E}=[t]_{\theta_E}$.

### 8.3. Concrete Algebras as Presented Algebras

> [!construction] Construction 8.9: Presentation Induced by a Valuation
> Let $g:X\to |\mathbf B|$ and let
> $$
> \mathbf C=\langle g[X]\rangle_{\mathbf B}.
> $$
> The complete defining congruence of $\mathbf C$ relative to $g$ is
> $$
> \ker(\operatorname{ev}_g).
> $$
> The induced complete presentation is
> $$
> \mathbf C\cong\mathbf T_{\Omega}(X)/\ker(\operatorname{ev}_g).
> $$

> [!definition] Definition 8.10: Complete and Incomplete Presentations
> Let $E\subseteq T_{\Omega}(X)^2$ and let $g:X\to |\mathbf B|$. The presentation $\langle X\mid E\rangle$ is sound for $g$ if $E\subseteq\ker(\operatorname{ev}_g)$. It is complete for the generated subalgebra if
> $$
> \theta_E=\ker(\operatorname{ev}_g).
> $$
> It is incomplete when $\theta_E\subsetneq\ker(\operatorname{ev}_g)$.

> [!warning] Warning 8.11: Abstract Quotient Versus Computable Quotient
> The quotient $\mathbf T_{\Omega}(X)/\theta_E$ exists as a set-theoretic algebra whenever $\theta_E$ is a congruence. This does not imply that membership in $\theta_E$ is decidable, that normal forms exist, or that equality of quotient classes can be effectively tested.

### 8.4. Rewriting and Normal Forms

> [!definition] Definition 8.12: Term Rewrite System
> A term rewrite system over $T_{\Omega}(X)$ is a binary relation
> $$
> \to_R\subseteq T_{\Omega}(X)\times T_{\Omega}(X)
> $$
> usually generated from a set of oriented equations $l\to r$ by closure under contexts and substitutions.

> [!definition] Definition 8.13: Termination
> A rewrite relation $\to_R$ is terminating if there is no infinite sequence
> $$
> t_0\to_R t_1\to_R t_2\to_R\cdots.
> $$

> [!definition] Definition 8.14: Confluence
> A rewrite relation $\to_R$ is confluent if whenever
> $$
> s\to_R^* t
> $$
> and
> $$
> s\to_R^* u,
> $$
> there exists $v$ such that
> $$
> t\to_R^* v
> $$
> and
> $$
> u\to_R^* v.
> $$

> [!definition] Definition 8.15: Normal Form
> A term $t$ is in normal form with respect to $\to_R$ if there is no $u$ such that $t\to_R u$. A normal form of $s$ is a normal term $n$ with $s\to_R^* n$.

> [!theorem] Theorem 8.16: Unique Normal Forms from Termination and Confluence
> If $\to_R$ is terminating and confluent, then every term has a unique normal form. If the symmetric, transitive, reflexive closure of $\to_R$ is $\theta_E$, then two terms are equal modulo $E$ if and only if their normal forms are equal.

> [!warning] Warning 8.17: Limitations of Rewriting
> A set of equations need not admit a terminating confluent orientation. Even when an abstract congruence is well-defined, its word problem may be undecidable.


## 9. Substitution, Evaluation, and Naturality

### 9.1. Valuations

> [!definition] Definition 9.1: Valuation in an Algebra
> For an $\Omega$-algebra $\mathbf A$ and a set $X$, a valuation is a function
> $$
> v:X\to |\mathbf A|.
> $$
> Its evaluation extension is the unique homomorphism
> $$
> \overline v:\mathbf T_{\Omega}(X)\to\mathbf A
> $$
> satisfying $\overline v\circ\eta_X=v$.

> [!proposition] Proposition 9.2: Agreement on Generators
> If $h,k:\mathbf T_{\Omega}(X)\to\mathbf A$ are homomorphisms and
> $$
> h\circ\eta_X=k\circ\eta_X,
> $$
> then $h=k$.

### 9.2. Substitutions

> [!definition] Definition 9.3: Substitution Assignment
> Let $X$ and $Y$ be sets. A substitution assignment from $X$ to terms over $Y$ is a function
> $$
> \sigma:X\to T_{\Omega}(Y).
> $$

> [!construction] Construction 9.4: Induced Substitution Homomorphism
> A substitution assignment $\sigma:X\to T_{\Omega}(Y)$ induces a unique homomorphism
> $$
> \overline\sigma:\mathbf T_{\Omega}(X)\to\mathbf T_{\Omega}(Y)
> $$
> satisfying
> $$
> \overline\sigma(\eta_X(x))=\sigma(x).
> $$
> This is substitution as evaluation into the syntax algebra $\mathbf T_{\Omega}(Y)$.

> [!definition] Definition 9.5: Variable and Term Substitution
> Variable substitution is the special case in which $\sigma(x)\in\eta_Y[Y]$ for every $x\in X$. Term substitution allows arbitrary $\sigma(x)\in T_{\Omega}(Y)$.

> [!proposition] Proposition 9.6: Composition of Substitutions
> If $\sigma:X\to T_{\Omega}(Y)$ and $\tau:Y\to T_{\Omega}(Z)$ are substitution assignments, their composite substitution is
> $$
> \tau\star\sigma:X\to T_{\Omega}(Z),
> \qquad
> (\tau\star\sigma)(x)=\overline\tau(\sigma(x)).
> $$
> The induced homomorphism satisfies
> $$
> \overline{\tau\star\sigma}=\overline\tau\circ\overline\sigma.
> $$

### 9.3. Evaluation-Substitution Identity

> [!theorem] Theorem 9.7: Evaluation-Substitution Identity
> Let $\sigma:X\to T_{\Omega}(Y)$ be a substitution assignment, let $\mathbf A$ be an $\Omega$-algebra, and let $w:Y\to |\mathbf A|$ be a valuation. Define the pulled-back valuation
> $$
> w\sigma:X\to |\mathbf A|,
> \qquad
> (w\sigma)(x)=\overline w(\sigma(x)).
> $$
> Then
> $$
> \overline w\circ\overline\sigma
> =
> \overline{w\sigma}.
> $$
> Equivalently, for every $t\in T_{\Omega}(X)$,
> $$
> \operatorname{ev}_w(\overline\sigma(t))
> =
> \operatorname{ev}_{w\sigma}(t).
> $$

> [!remark] Remark 9.8: Naturality of Evaluation
> The identity in Theorem 9.7 states that evaluating after syntactic substitution equals evaluating the original term under the valuation obtained by evaluating the substituted terms.

### 9.4. Equations Under Substitution

> [!definition] Definition 9.9: Valid Equation in an Algebra
> An equation $s\approx t$ over variables $X$ is valid in an $\Omega$-algebra $\mathbf A$ if for every valuation $v:X\to |\mathbf A|$,
> $$
> \overline v(s)=\overline v(t).
> $$

> [!definition] Definition 9.10: Equational Theory
> The equational theory of $\mathbf A$ on variables $X$ is
> $$
> \operatorname{Eq}_X(\mathbf A)=\{(s,t)\in T_{\Omega}(X)^2: \mathbf A\models s\approx t\}.
> $$

> [!definition] Definition 9.11: Fully Invariant Congruence
> A congruence $\theta\in\operatorname{Con}(\mathbf T_{\Omega}(X))$ is fully invariant if for every endomorphism
> $$
> e:\mathbf T_{\Omega}(X)\to\mathbf T_{\Omega}(X)
> $$
> and every $(s,t)\in\theta$, one has $(e(s),e(t))\in\theta$.

> [!proposition] Proposition 9.12: Substitution Stability of Valid Equations
> If an equation is valid in $\mathbf A$, then every substitution instance of it is valid in $\mathbf A$. Consequently $\operatorname{Eq}_X(\mathbf A)$ is a fully invariant congruence on $\mathbf T_{\Omega}(X)$.

> [!definition] Definition 9.13: Equational Consequence
> For a set $E\subseteq T_{\Omega}(X)^2$, an equation $s\approx t$ is an equational consequence of $E$ in a class $\mathcal K$ of $\Omega$-algebras if every $\mathbf A\in\mathcal K$ and every valuation satisfying all equations in $E$ also satisfy $s\approx t$.

### 9.5. Term Monad Repackaging

> [!construction] Construction 9.14: Term Functor on Sets
> The term assignment extends to a functor
> $$
> T_{\Omega}:\mathbf{Set}\to\mathbf{Set}
> $$
> sending a set $X$ to $T_{\Omega}(X)$ and a function $u:X\to Y$ to the substitution homomorphism induced by
> $$
> \eta_Y\circ u:X\to T_{\Omega}(Y).
> $$

> [!construction] Construction 9.15: Unit and Multiplication
> The unit at $X$ is the generator map
> $$
> \eta_X:X\to T_{\Omega}(X).
> $$
> The multiplication at $X$ is the flattening substitution homomorphism
> $$
> \mu_X:T_{\Omega}(T_{\Omega}(X))\to T_{\Omega}(X)
> $$
> extending the identity map on $T_{\Omega}(X)$.

> [!theorem] Theorem 9.16: Monad Laws for Terms
> The triple $(T_{\Omega},\eta,\mu)$ is a monad on $\mathbf{Set}$. Substitution is the corresponding Kleisli extension operation. The monad associativity law is the associativity of iterated substitution, and the unit laws state that substituting variables for themselves changes no term.

> [!remark] Remark 9.17: Repackaging Status
> The monad formulation repackages the substitution and evaluation machinery. It is not needed to define terms, free algebras, evaluation homomorphisms, or generated subalgebras.


## 10. Relational Generation

### 10.1. From Operations to Relations

> [!definition] Definition 10.1: Relational Constructor
> Let $B$ be a set. An $n$-ary relational constructor on $B$ is a relation
> $$
> R\subseteq B^n\times B.
> $$
> If $((b_0,\dots,b_{n-1}),c)\in R$, then $c$ is an allowed output from the inputs $(b_0,\dots,b_{n-1})$.

> [!definition] Definition 10.2: Functional, Partial, Total, and Multivalued Constructors
> A relational constructor $R\subseteq B^n\times B$ is:
> 
> 1. functional if each input tuple has at most one output;
> 2. total if each input tuple has at least one output;
> 3. partial functional if it is functional but not necessarily total;
> 4. multivalued if some input tuple has more than one output.

> [!remark] Remark 10.3: Operations as Relations
> A function $F:B^n\to B$ determines the relational constructor
> $$
> R_F=\{((b_0,\dots,b_{n-1}),F(b_0,\dots,b_{n-1})): (b_0,\dots,b_{n-1})\in B^n\}.
> $$
> Thus ordinary algebraic generation is a special case of relational generation.

### 10.2. Relationally Generated Closures

> [!definition] Definition 10.4: Relational Signature on a Set
> A relational constructor system on a set $B$ consists of a family
> $$
> \mathcal R=(R_j\subseteq B^{n_j}\times B)_{j\in J}
> $$
> indexed by a set $J$, with arity $n_j\in\mathbb N$ for each $j$.

> [!definition] Definition 10.5: Relation-Closed Set
> Let $S\subseteq B$. A subset $C\subseteq B$ is closed under $\mathcal R$ if for every $j\in J$, every input tuple $\vec b\in C^{n_j}$, and every $c\in B$ with $(\vec b,c)\in R_j$, one has $c\in C$.

> [!construction] Construction 10.6: Relationally Generated Closure
> The closure of $S$ under $\mathcal R$ is
> $$
> \operatorname{Cl}_{\mathcal R}(S)=\bigcap\{C\subseteq B:S\subseteq C\text{ and }C\text{ is closed under }\mathcal R\}.
> $$

> [!construction] Construction 10.7: Stage Construction for Relational Generation
> Define
> $$
> C_0=S
> $$
> and
> $$
> C_{m+1}=C_m\cup\{c\in B: \exists j\in J,\ \exists\vec b\in C_m^{n_j},\ (\vec b,c)\in R_j\}.
> $$
> For finitary relational constructors, set
> $$
> C_\omega=\bigcup_{m<\omega}C_m.
> $$

> [!proposition] Proposition 10.8: Least Relational Closure
> For finitary relational constructor systems,
> $$
> \operatorname{Cl}_{\mathcal R}(S)=C_\omega.
> $$
> The set $\operatorname{Cl}_{\mathcal R}(S)$ is the least subset of $B$ containing $S$ and closed under every relation in $\mathcal R$.

> [!theorem] Theorem 10.9: Induction Principle for Relational Closure
> Let $P$ be a predicate on $\operatorname{Cl}_{\mathcal R}(S)$. If $P(s)$ holds for every $s\in S$, and whenever $(\vec b,c)\in R_j$ with all entries of $\vec b$ satisfying $P$, the element $c$ satisfies $P$, then $P$ holds for every element of $\operatorname{Cl}_{\mathcal R}(S)$.

### 10.3. Formal Relational Syntax and Derivation Witnesses

> [!definition] Definition 10.10: Formal Relational Constructor Event
> For a relational constructor $R_j\subseteq B^{n_j}\times B$, a formal constructor event is a tuple
> $$
> (j,w_0,\dots,w_{n_j-1},c)
> $$
> where $w_i$ are derivation witnesses and $c\in B$ is asserted to satisfy
> $$
> ((\operatorname{val}(w_0),\dots,\operatorname{val}(w_{n_j-1})),c)\in R_j.
> $$

> [!construction] Construction 10.11: Relational Derivation Trees
> Given base set $S\subseteq B$, relational derivation trees are generated by:
> 
> 1. base witnesses $\operatorname{base}(s)$ for $s\in S$ with value $s$;
> 2. constructor witnesses $\operatorname{step}_j(w_0,\dots,w_{n_j-1};c)$ whenever the relation $R_j$ permits $c$ from the values of the input witnesses.

> [!definition] Definition 10.12: Witnessed Versus Unwitnessed Generation
> The unwitnessed generated closure is a subset of $B$. The witnessed relational syntax is a set of derivation objects equipped with a value map
> $$
> \operatorname{val}:\operatorname{Der}_{\mathcal R}(S)\to\operatorname{Cl}_{\mathcal R}(S).
> $$
> Multiple witnesses may have the same value.

### 10.4. Comparison Map and Relational Freeness

> [!theorem] Theorem 10.13: Surjectivity of the Relational Value Map
> The value map
> $$
> \operatorname{val}:\operatorname{Der}_{\mathcal R}(S)\to\operatorname{Cl}_{\mathcal R}(S)
> $$
> is surjective. Every generated element has at least one finite derivation witness.

> [!definition] Definition 10.14: Relational Kernel
> The relational kernel of the value map is
> $$
> \ker(\operatorname{val})=\{(d,e):\operatorname{val}(d)=\operatorname{val}(e)\}.
> $$
> It measures non-uniqueness of derivations representing the same generated value.

> [!definition] Definition 10.15: Unique Relational Readability
> A relationally generated closure has unique relational readability relative to the chosen witness system if the value map
> $$
> \operatorname{val}:\operatorname{Der}_{\mathcal R}(S)\to\operatorname{Cl}_{\mathcal R}(S)
> $$
> is bijective.

> [!theorem] Theorem 10.16: Equivalent Criteria for Relational Freeness
> Under a fixed finitary relational witness system, the following are equivalent:
> 
> 1. the value map from derivation witnesses to generated elements is bijective;
> 2. the value map is surjective and has trivial kernel;
> 3. every generated element has a unique derivation witness;
> 4. every relational constructor event has recoverable inputs and label from its output, except for base elements according to the specified base tagging;
> 5. recursive definitions on generated elements may be specified by clauses on base witnesses and constructor events without a representative-invariance condition.

> [!warning] Warning 10.17: Relational Generation Need Not Be Algebraic Generation
> If a relational constructor is partial, multivalued, or nondeterministic, the generated closure need not be the subalgebra generated by $S$ for any total operation system on $B$ unless additional choices, restrictions, or functionalization data are supplied.


## 11. Logic as a Specialization

### 11.1. Propositional Syntax

> [!definition] Definition 11.1: Propositional Signature
> Let $P$ be a set of propositional variables. A propositional connective signature may contain symbols such as
> $$
> \neg\in\Omega_1,
> \qquad
> \wedge,\vee,\to\in\Omega_2,
> \qquad
> \bot,\top\in\Omega_0.
> $$
> The formula algebra over $P$ is the term algebra
> $$
> \mathbf{Form}_{\Omega}(P)=\mathbf T_{\Omega}(P).
> $$

> [!definition] Definition 11.2: Propositional Formula
> A propositional formula is an element of $T_{\Omega}(P)$, or equivalently an element of any chosen concrete presentation of that free algebra, such as formula trees or well-formed strings with a specified parser.

> [!theorem] Theorem 11.3: Structural Induction for Propositional Formulas
> Every predicate on propositional formulas that holds for propositional variables and nullary connective formulas, and is preserved by each connective constructor, holds for all propositional formulas.

### 11.2. Propositional Semantics

> [!construction] Construction 11.4: Boolean Truth-Value Algebra
> Let
> $$
> \mathbf 2=(\{0,1\},\neg^{\mathbf 2},\wedge^{\mathbf 2},\vee^{\mathbf 2},\to^{\mathbf 2},\bot^{\mathbf 2},\top^{\mathbf 2})
> $$
> be the $\Omega$-algebra of truth values with the intended truth-functions.

> [!definition] Definition 11.5: Propositional Valuation and Evaluation
> A propositional valuation is a function
> $$
> v:P\to\{0,1\}.
> $$
> Its evaluation extension is the unique homomorphism
> $$
> \overline v:\mathbf{Form}_{\Omega}(P)\to\mathbf 2.
> $$

> [!definition] Definition 11.6: Tautology and Logical Equivalence
> A formula $\varphi\in T_{\Omega}(P)$ is a tautology if
> $$
> \overline v(\varphi)=1
> $$
> for every valuation $v:P\to\{0,1\}$. Two formulas $\varphi,\psi$ are truth-functionally equivalent if
> $$
> \overline v(\varphi)=\overline v(\psi)
> $$
> for every valuation $v$.

> [!definition] Definition 11.7: Semantic Kernel of Propositional Logic
> The truth-functional semantic equivalence relation on formulas is
> $$
> \equiv_{\mathbf 2}=\bigcap_{v:P\to\{0,1\}}\ker(\overline v).
> $$
> It is a congruence on the formula algebra.

> [!definition] Definition 11.8: Tautological Consequence
> For $\Gamma\subseteq T_{\Omega}(P)$ and $\varphi\in T_{\Omega}(P)$, write
> $$
> \Gamma\models_{\mathrm{taut}}\varphi
> $$
> when every valuation $v:P\to\{0,1\}$ satisfying $\overline v(\gamma)=1$ for all $\gamma\in\Gamma$ also satisfies $\overline v(\varphi)=1$.

### 11.3. First-Order Terms

> [!definition] Definition 11.9: First-Order Function Signature
> A first-order function signature consists of a set $\mathcal F$ of function symbols with arity function
> $$
> \operatorname{ar}:\mathcal F\to\mathbb N.
> $$
> Constants are nullary function symbols in $\mathcal F_0$.

> [!definition] Definition 11.10: First-Order Term Algebra
> For a set $V$ of variables, the first-order term algebra is
> $$
> \mathbf T_{\mathcal F}(V).
> $$
> Its elements are first-order terms built from variables in $V$ and function symbols in $\mathcal F$.

> [!definition] Definition 11.11: Structure and Term Evaluation
> A first-order $\mathcal L$-structure $\mathcal M$ interprets each function symbol $f\in\mathcal F_n$ as a function
> $$
> f^{\mathcal M}:M^n\to M.
> $$
> An assignment $s:V\to M$ induces the unique term-evaluation homomorphism
> $$
> \overline s:\mathbf T_{\mathcal F}(V)\to\mathbf M_{\mathcal F},
> $$
> where $\mathbf M_{\mathcal F}$ is the $\mathcal F$-algebra reduct of $\mathcal M$.

### 11.4. First-Order Formulas and Binding

> [!definition] Definition 11.12: First-Order Relational Signature
> A first-order language contains function symbols $\mathcal F$, relation symbols $\mathcal R$ with arities, and optionally an equality symbol. Relation symbols are not operation symbols of the term algebra; they are used to form atomic formulas.

> [!definition] Definition 11.13: Atomic Formula
> If $R\in\mathcal R_n$ and $t_0,\dots,t_{n-1}\in T_{\mathcal F}(V)$, then
> $$
> R(t_0,\dots,t_{n-1})
> $$
> is an atomic formula. If equality is present and $s,t\in T_{\mathcal F}(V)$, then
> $$
> s=t
> $$
> is an atomic formula.

> [!construction] Construction 11.14: Formula Formation
> First-order formulas are generated from atomic formulas by connective constructors and quantifier constructors
> $$
> \forall x,
> \qquad
> \exists x
> $$
> for variables $x\in V$. A formula presentation must include binding information distinguishing free occurrences from bound occurrences.

> [!definition] Definition 11.15: Free and Bound Variables
> The set of free variables $\operatorname{FV}(\varphi)$ of a formula $\varphi$ is defined recursively by:
> 
> 1. variables occurring in atomic formulas are free;
> 2. Boolean connectives take unions of free-variable sets of immediate subformulas;
> 3. $\operatorname{FV}(\forall x\,\varphi)=\operatorname{FV}(\varphi)\setminus\{x\}$;
> 4. $\operatorname{FV}(\exists x\,\varphi)=\operatorname{FV}(\varphi)\setminus\{x\}$.

> [!warning] Warning 11.16: Binding Is Not Ordinary One-Sorted Term Algebra Alone
> Quantifiers bind variables. A formula algebra with binding requires occurrence-sensitive syntax, alpha-equivalence conventions or named-variable hygiene, and capture-avoiding substitution. Treating quantifiers as ordinary unary operation symbols loses binding semantics unless additional binding data are included.

> [!definition] Definition 11.17: Capture-Avoiding Substitution
> A substitution of terms for variables in a formula is capture-avoiding if no free variable of a substituted term becomes bound after substitution. Formal definitions require either renaming of bound variables, de Bruijn indices, nominal syntax, or another explicit binding representation.

### 11.5. Limits of Truth-Functional Abstraction

> [!definition] Definition 11.18: Tautological Skeleton
> The tautological skeleton of a first-order formula is obtained by replacing atomic subformulas by propositional variables while retaining Boolean connective structure. It forgets predicate structure, term structure, equality structure, and quantifier-binding structure.

> [!warning] Warning 11.19: Universal Instantiation Is Not Merely Tautological Consequence
> Principles involving quantifiers, such as passing from $\forall x\,\varphi(x)$ to $\varphi(t)$ under admissibility conditions on $t$, depend on first-order binding and substitution structure. They are not consequences of the truth-functional skeleton alone.

> [!remark] Remark 11.20: Logic as Syntax Plus Semantic Machinery
> Propositional logic is closely modelled by a formula algebra and evaluation homomorphisms into a Boolean algebra. First-order logic additionally requires term evaluation, relation interpretation, assignments, binding, variable hygiene, and satisfaction relations.


## 12. Derivation Trees and Build Certificates

### 12.1. Derivation Trees for Generated Objects

> [!definition] Definition 12.1: Derivation Tree
> Let a closure or algebraic generation process be specified by base objects and constructor rules. A derivation tree is a finite rooted labelled tree whose leaves are labelled by base events and whose internal nodes are labelled by constructor events whose children witness the required inputs.

> [!definition] Definition 12.2: Object Tree Versus Derivation Tree
> An object tree is a syntactic object whose nodes are the constructors of that object. A derivation tree is a witness that some object belongs to a generated set. These coincide only when the generated object is itself freely and uniquely constructed by the same constructors.

> [!definition] Definition 12.3: Occurrence-Aware Position
> In a derivation tree, an occurrence-aware position is an address in the tree domain. Two positions may carry equal values while remaining distinct occurrences. Occurrence identity is address identity, not value identity.

> [!warning] Warning 12.4: Value and Occurrence Are Distinct
> If the same generated element appears twice in a derivation tree, the two appearances may be distinct occurrences with identical values. Quotienting occurrences by equality of values can destroy dependency information.

### 12.2. Staged Build Certificates

> [!definition] Definition 12.5: Rank-Level Position Sets
> Let $D$ be the domain of a finite derivation tree. The rank level $L_k(D)$ is the set of addresses at height $k$ from the leaves or, under a root-oriented convention, at depth $k$ from the root. A certificate must specify which convention is used.

> [!definition] Definition 12.6: Staged Build Certificate
> A staged build certificate for an object produced by a finite generation process is a finite sequence
> $$
> (B_0,B_1,\dots,B_m)
> $$
> where each $B_k$ is an ordered list or finite set of constructor events whose inputs occur in earlier stages and whose outputs become available at stage $k$.

> [!definition] Definition 12.7: Validity of a Build Certificate
> A certificate is valid relative to a constructor system when:
> 
> 1. every base event in $B_0$ is permitted;
> 2. every constructor event in $B_{k+1}$ has all required inputs among outputs of earlier stages;
> 3. every declared output is the output specified by the event;
> 4. the final stage contains or names the intended generated object;
> 5. all side conditions of the constructor system are satisfied.

> [!construction] Construction 12.8: Reconstruction from a Certificate
> Given a valid certificate and a fixed within-stage ordering convention, reconstruct the derivation object by recursively attaching each event to the earlier events named as its inputs. If sharing is permitted, reconstruction yields a directed acyclic dependency graph; if occurrences are duplicated, reconstruction yields a tree.

> [!theorem] Theorem 12.9: Uniqueness Under Freeness and Fixed Ordering
> If the constructor system is free, every generated object has unique derivation, and a fixed ordering convention is imposed within stages, then the canonical staged build certificate associated with the object is unique.

> [!warning] Warning 12.10: Linear Histories Are Not Canonical Certificates
> A linear construction history may depend on arbitrary choices of order among independent subbuilds. A staged certificate records dependency rank and may be canonical only after a convention for ordering incomparable events is specified.


## 13. Effective Parsing and Computability

### 13.1. Abstract Freeness Versus Effective Parsing

> [!definition] Definition 13.1: Abstract Destructor
> In a free constructor presentation, an abstract destructor is a set-theoretic function that recovers the unique outer constructor case and immediate subcomponents of a non-generator element. Its existence follows from the disjointness and injectivity of constructor ranges in the presentation.

> [!definition] Definition 13.2: Computable Destructor
> A computable destructor is an algorithm which, for a chosen data representation, terminates and returns the outer constructor case and immediate subcomponents when the input belongs to a constructor range, and reports non-membership when it does not.

> [!warning] Warning 13.3: Abstract Unique Decomposition Does Not Give an Effective Parser
> A set-theoretic unique-decomposition theorem does not provide a computable procedure for recognizing constructor ranges, splitting strings, checking well-formedness, or deciding equality. Effective parsing requires an explicit representation with decidable formation conditions and computable destructors.

> [!definition] Definition 13.4: Recognizer
> A recognizer for a representation carrier $R$ inside a larger data set $D$ is a decidable predicate
> $$
> \operatorname{WF}:D\to\{0,1\}
> $$
> such that
> $$
> \operatorname{WF}(d)=1
> $$
> if and only if $d\in R$.

### 13.2. Parsing Tree and String Presentations

> [!definition] Definition 13.5: Prefix Notation with Fixed Arities
> In prefix notation, a term with root $f\in\Omega_n$ is displayed by writing $f$ followed by the encodings of its $n$ immediate subterms. Fixed arities allow a parser to determine how many subterms to read after seeing $f$.

> [!definition] Definition 13.6: Fully Parenthesized Notation
> In fully parenthesized notation, a positive-arity term is displayed with a root symbol, opening delimiter, delimited encodings of immediate subterms, and closing delimiter. Nullary symbols and variables must be disjointly recognizable.

> [!theorem] Theorem 13.7: Parser Correctness, Completeness, and Uniqueness
> Let $W_{\Omega}(X)$ be a decidable well-formed string representation with a parser
> $$
> \operatorname{parse}:W_{\Omega}(X)\to\operatorname{Tr}_{\Omega}(X)
> $$
> and flattening map $\operatorname{flat}$. The parser is correct, complete, and unique when
> $$
> \operatorname{parse}(\operatorname{flat}(\tau))=\tau
> $$
> for all trees $\tau$, every well-formed string is in the image of $\operatorname{flat}$, and the inverse image of each well-formed string contains exactly one tree.

> [!remark] Remark 13.8: Complexity Measures for Parsing
> Recursive parsing procedures require a well-founded complexity measure, such as string length, number of symbols, number of unmatched delimiters, or tree height after partial parsing. Each recursive call must strictly decrease the chosen measure.

### 13.3. Structural Equality and Sharing

> [!definition] Definition 13.9: Recursive Structural Equality Algorithm
> For a constructor presentation with decidable outer cases and computable destructors, structural equality is decidable by recursively comparing outer constructor labels and corresponding immediate subcomponents.

> [!definition] Definition 13.10: Hash-Consing
> Hash-consing is an implementation representation in which structurally equal nodes are stored once and reused by pointer. It changes sharing structure but not the abstract term represented, provided node construction preserves the invariant that equal constructor labels and equal child pointers determine a unique stored node.

> [!definition] Definition 13.11: DAG Representation of Terms
> A directed acyclic graph representation of a term permits sharing of identical subterms. Its unfolding is a tree. It represents the same free syntax only when unfolding is well-founded and every node has a constructor label with arity matching its outgoing ordered children.

> [!warning] Warning 13.12: Structural Equality Versus Equality Modulo Quotient
> Structural equality compares raw syntax. Equality modulo a congruence $\theta$ asks whether $(s,t)\in\theta$. A structural equality algorithm does not decide quotient equality unless $\theta$ is the diagonal congruence or unless additional quotient-decision machinery is supplied.

### 13.4. Word Problems

> [!definition] Definition 13.13: Word Problem of a Presentation
> For a presentation
> $$
> \langle X\mid E\rangle_{\Omega},
> $$
> the word problem is the decision problem: given $s,t\in T_{\Omega}(X)$, determine whether
> $$
> (s,t)\in\theta_E.
> $$

> [!definition] Definition 13.14: Decidable Word Problem
> A presentation has decidable word problem if there is an algorithm that terminates on every pair of terms and returns whether they are equal modulo $\theta_E$.

> [!proposition] Proposition 13.15: Normal Forms as Computable Representatives
> If a terminating and confluent rewrite system presents $\theta_E$, then the word problem for $\langle X\mid E\rangle$ is decidable by computing normal forms and comparing them structurally.

> [!warning] Warning 13.16: Limits of Effective Quotienting
> A quotient algebra may exist even when no algorithm decides equality of representatives. Effective implementation of quotient syntax requires a decision procedure, canonical representatives, normalization, or a controlled incomplete approximation.


## 14. Categorical Repackaging

### 14.1. Category of $\Omega$-Algebras

> [!definition] Definition 14.1: Category of $\Omega$-Algebras
> The category $\mathbf{Alg}_{\Omega}$ has $\Omega$-algebras as objects and $\Omega$-homomorphisms as morphisms. Identities and composition are the identity functions and composition of functions, which preserve all operations.

> [!construction] Construction 14.2: Forgetful Functor
> The forgetful functor
> $$
> U:\mathbf{Alg}_{\Omega}\to\mathbf{Set}
> $$
> sends $\mathbf A$ to $|\mathbf A|$ and sends a homomorphism to its underlying function.

> [!construction] Construction 14.3: Free Algebra Functor
> The free algebra construction defines a functor
> $$
> F:\mathbf{Set}\to\mathbf{Alg}_{\Omega}
> $$
> by
> $$
> F(X)=\mathbf T_{\Omega}(X).
> $$
> For a function $u:X\to Y$, the homomorphism $F(u)$ is the unique extension of
> $$
> \eta_Y\circ u:X\to T_{\Omega}(Y).
> $$

> [!theorem] Theorem 14.4: Free-Forgetful Adjunction
> The free algebra functor is left adjoint to the forgetful functor:
> $$
> F\dashv U.
> $$
> For every set $X$ and $\Omega$-algebra $\mathbf A$, there is a bijection
> $$
> \mathbf{Alg}_{\Omega}(F(X),\mathbf A)
> \cong
> \mathbf{Set}(X,U\mathbf A)
> $$
> sending a homomorphism $h$ to $h\circ\eta_X$.

### 14.2. Universal Properties Revisited

> [!proposition] Proposition 14.5: Products as Categorical Products
> The direct product of $\Omega$-algebras, with its projection homomorphisms, is the categorical product in $\mathbf{Alg}_{\Omega}$.

> [!proposition] Proposition 14.6: Quotients as Coequalizer-Type Objects
> If $\theta\in\operatorname{Con}(\mathbf A)$ and $r_1,r_2:\mathbf R\rightrightarrows\mathbf A$ are homomorphisms whose generated image relation has congruence closure $\theta$, then the quotient projection
> $$
> q_\theta:\mathbf A\to\mathbf A/\theta
> $$
> coequalizes $r_1$ and $r_2$ and is universal among homomorphisms coequalizing them.

> [!definition] Definition 14.7: Kernel Pair
> For a homomorphism $h:\mathbf A\to\mathbf B$, the kernel pair is the pullback of $h$ along itself. Its carrier is
> $$
> \{(a,a')\in |\mathbf A|^2:h(a)=h(a')\},
> $$
> which is the kernel congruence as a subalgebra of $\mathbf A\times\mathbf A$.

> [!remark] Remark 14.8: Unique up to Unique Isomorphism
> A universal object is unique up to a unique isomorphism compatible with the universal structure. This is stronger than merely saying that any two such objects are isomorphic, because the compatibility condition determines the isomorphism.

### 14.3. Polynomial Functors and Initial Algebras

> [!construction] Construction 14.9: Signature Polynomial Functor
> For a finitary signature $\Omega$, define the polynomial functor
> $$
> P_{\Omega}(Y)=\coprod_{n<\omega}\Omega_n\times Y^n.
> $$
> An element is a pair consisting of an $n$-ary operation symbol and an $n$-tuple of $Y$-inputs.

> [!definition] Definition 14.10: $P_{\Omega}$-Algebra
> A $P_{\Omega}$-algebra is a set $A$ equipped with a structure map
> $$
> \alpha:P_{\Omega}(A)\to A.
> $$
> It is equivalent to an $\Omega$-algebra by taking
> $$
> f^{\mathbf A}(a_0,\dots,a_{n-1})=\alpha(f,(a_0,\dots,a_{n-1})).
> $$

> [!construction] Construction 14.11: Free Terms as an Initial Algebra with Variables
> For a set $X$, define
> $$
> H_X(Y)=X\amalg P_{\Omega}(Y).
> $$
> The term set $T_{\Omega}(X)$ carries an $H_X$-algebra structure whose components are the variable injection and the operation-symbol constructors.

> [!theorem] Theorem 14.12: Initial Algebra Characterization of Terms
> The set $T_{\Omega}(X)$ with its variable and constructor structure is an initial $H_X$-algebra. Structural recursion is the unique homomorphism from this initial algebra into any other $H_X$-algebra.

> [!corollary] Corollary 14.13: Trees as Initial Algebras
> The tree presentation $\operatorname{Tr}_{\Omega}(X)$ is an initial $H_X$-algebra and hence is isomorphic to $T_{\Omega}(X)$ as the carrier of the free syntax algebra.

### 14.4. Term Monad Categorically

> [!theorem] Theorem 14.14: Term Monad from the Adjunction
> The free-forgetful adjunction
> $$
> F\dashv U
> $$
> induces the monad
> $$
> T_{\Omega}=UF
> $$
> on $\mathbf{Set}$ with unit $\eta$ and multiplication induced by counit evaluation
> $$
> FUF(X)\to F(X).
> $$

> [!proposition] Proposition 14.15: Kleisli Morphisms as Substitutions
> A Kleisli morphism
> $$
> X\to T_{\Omega}(Y)
> $$
> is exactly a substitution assignment from variables in $X$ to terms over $Y$. Kleisli composition is substitution composition.

> [!remark] Remark 14.16: Categorical Summary
> The categorical language packages the same constructions: free algebras as left adjoints, products by universal cones, quotients by universal coequalizing maps, terms as initial algebras, and substitutions as Kleisli morphisms of the term monad.


## 15. Synthesis

### 15.1. Main Objects

| Object | Data | Governing Condition | Failure Mode |
|---|---|---|---|
| Signature $\Omega$ | operation symbols and arities | arity function $\Omega\to\mathbb N$ | ambiguous arities or untagged symbol collisions |
| $\Omega$-algebra $\mathbf A$ | carrier and operations | $f^{\mathbf A}:|\mathbf A|^n\to|\mathbf A|$ | confusing formal symbols with interpretations |
| Subalgebra | subset with induced operations | closure under all operations, including nullary ones | subset not closed |
| Congruence | equivalence relation | compatibility with every operation | quotient operations not well-defined |
| Quotient algebra | classes modulo congruence | operations defined on classes | representative dependence |
| Free algebra | algebra plus generator map | unique homomorphic extension | generated but not injective from syntax |
| Term algebra | formal terms as carrier | constructors interpret operation symbols | mistaking representation for syntax itself |
| Tree presentation | addressed arity-correct trees | constructor injectivity and disjoint ranges | parsing or coding not supplied |
| Evaluation | homomorphic extension of valuation | universal property of term algebra | semantic collapse of distinct terms |
| Presentation | generators and equations | quotient by generated congruence | undecidable word problem |
| Substitution | map from variables to terms | induced homomorphism | capture in binding contexts |
| Relational closure | base set and constructor relations | least relation-closed set | nondeterminism and nonunique witnesses |

### 15.2. Central Factorizations

> [!theorem] Theorem 15.1: Master Evaluation Factorization
> Let $\Omega$ be a finitary signature, $X$ a set, $\mathbf B$ an $\Omega$-algebra, and $g:X\to |\mathbf B|$ a valuation. Then evaluation factors as
> $$
> \mathbf T_{\Omega}(X)
> \twoheadrightarrow
> \mathbf T_{\Omega}(X)/\ker(\operatorname{ev}_g)
> \cong
> \langle g[X]\rangle_{\mathbf B}
> \hookrightarrow
> \mathbf B.
> $$
> Generatedness is the surjectivity onto $\langle g[X]\rangle_{\mathbf B}$. Freeness of the generated subalgebra is the injectivity of the restricted evaluation map. Semantic identification is the kernel congruence.

> [!theorem] Theorem 15.2: Master Presentation Factorization
> Let $E\subseteq T_{\Omega}(X)^2$ and let $\theta_E=\operatorname{Cg}(E)$. For any valuation $g:X\to |\mathbf A|$, the extension $\operatorname{ev}_g$ factors through
> $$
> \mathbf T_{\Omega}(X)/\theta_E
> $$
> if and only if
> $$
> E\subseteq\ker(\operatorname{ev}_g).
> $$
> Thus a presentation imposes exactly the congruence generated by its equations, while a concrete valuation may impose a larger kernel unless the presentation is complete for that valuation.

### 15.3. Main Equivalences

> [!proposition] Proposition 15.3: Free Syntax Equivalence Package
> For a concrete syntax presentation $\mathbf R$ with generator map $\rho:X\to |\mathbf R|$, the following are equivalent:
> 
> 1. $\mathbf R$ is the free $\Omega$-algebra on $X$.
> 2. The canonical map $\mathbf T_{\Omega}(X)\to\mathbf R$ is an isomorphism.
> 3. The canonical map is surjective and has trivial kernel.
> 4. Constructors in $\mathbf R$ have disjoint ranges and are injective, with generator values separated from constructor values.
> 5. Every element has unique readability.
> 6. Structural recursion on $\mathbf R$ requires no representative-compatibility condition.

> [!proposition] Proposition 15.4: Concrete Generation Equivalence Package
> For $\mathbf C=\langle g[X]\rangle_{\mathbf B}$, the following are equivalent:
> 
> 1. $\mathbf C$ is freely generated by $g[X]$ indexed by $X$.
> 2. $\operatorname{ev}_g:\mathbf T_{\Omega}(X)\to\mathbf C$ is an isomorphism.
> 3. $\ker(\operatorname{ev}_g)=\Delta_{T_{\Omega}(X)}$.
> 4. Each element of $\mathbf C$ has a unique formal term representative.
> 5. The quotient description of $\mathbf C$ has diagonal quotient congruence.

> [!proposition] Proposition 15.5: Quotient Recursion Equivalence Package
> For $\theta\in\operatorname{Con}(\mathbf T_{\Omega}(X))$ and a structurally recursive map $H:T_{\Omega}(X)\to Y$, the following are equivalent:
> 
> 1. $H$ descends to $T_{\Omega}(X)/\theta$.
> 2. $\theta\subseteq\ker H$.
> 3. $H(s)=H(t)$ for every equation forced by $\theta$.
> 4. The value assigned by $H$ is independent of the chosen representative of a quotient class.

### 15.4. Key Failure Modes

> [!warning] Warning 15.6: Common Failure Modes
> The following failures are structurally distinct:
> 
> 1. A subset fails to be a subalgebra because it is not closed under operations.
> 2. An equivalence relation fails to define a quotient algebra because it is not a congruence.
> 3. A generated algebra fails to be free because the evaluation kernel is nontrivial.
> 4. A string notation fails to present syntax because parsing is ambiguous or not total on the intended well-formed set.
> 5. A quotient presentation exists abstractly but has undecidable equality of representatives.
> 6. A recursive definition on raw terms fails to descend to a quotient because it is not invariant under the quotient congruence.
> 7. A derivation witness system fails to give unique derivability because multiple derivation trees have the same generated value.
> 8. A first-order formula representation fails to respect logic because binding and capture-avoidance have not been represented.

### 15.5. Conceptual Spine

The technical spine is the following chain.

1. A signature fixes operation symbols and arities.
2. An algebra interprets those symbols as operations on a carrier.
3. The term algebra is the free algebra on a generator set.
4. Trees, tuples, and strings are concrete presentations of the same free algebra when their constructor maps are injective, disjoint, and arity-correct.
5. A valuation into a concrete algebra extends uniquely to an evaluation homomorphism.
6. The image of evaluation is the generated subalgebra of the target.
7. The kernel of evaluation records exactly the semantic identifications among terms.
8. The first isomorphism theorem identifies the generated subalgebra with syntax modulo the evaluation kernel.
9. A presentation by generators and relations is a quotient of the term algebra by a generated congruence.
10. Structural induction and recursion are consequences of freeness; quotient recursion requires compatibility with the quotient congruence.
11. Substitution is evaluation into another term algebra and is governed by the evaluation-substitution identity.
12. Relational generation generalizes operation-based generation by replacing functions with relations and by separating values from derivation witnesses.
13. Logic instantiates the syntax/evaluation pattern but first-order logic adds binding, assignments, relation interpretation, and capture-avoidance.
14. Effective parsing and word-problem decidability require computability assumptions not supplied by abstract freeness alone.
15. Categorical language repackages the same facts as adjunctions, initial algebras, coequalizers, kernel pairs, polynomial functors, and monads.

> [!remark] Remark 15.7: Final Structural Summary
> The free term algebra is the initial syntax object. Concrete generated structures are its homomorphic images. Congruences measure identifications. Quotients impose equations. Presentations specify generators and relations. Representations encode the free object in concrete carriers. Evaluation maps syntax into semantics. Recursion and induction are available freely only before quotienting or after verifying descent conditions. Effective parsing and equality algorithms require representation-specific computability data.


## 16. Expanded Reference on Closure, Generation, and Rank

### 16.1. Closure Operators Induced by Algebraic Structure

> [!definition] Definition 16.1: Closure Operator on a Powerset
> Let $B$ be a set. A closure operator on $B$ is a function
> $$
> \operatorname{cl}:\mathcal P(B)\to\mathcal P(B)
> $$
> satisfying, for all $S,T\subseteq B$:
> 
> 1. extensivity:
> $$
> S\subseteq\operatorname{cl}(S);
> $$
> 2. monotonicity:
> $$
> S\subseteq T\Rightarrow\operatorname{cl}(S)\subseteq\operatorname{cl}(T);
> $$
> 3. idempotence:
> $$
> \operatorname{cl}(\operatorname{cl}(S))=\operatorname{cl}(S).
> $$

> [!definition] Definition 16.2: Closed Set of a Closure Operator
> A subset $C\subseteq B$ is closed for $\operatorname{cl}$ if
> $$
> \operatorname{cl}(C)=C.
> $$
> The collection of closed subsets is denoted
> $$
> \operatorname{Closed}(B,\operatorname{cl}).
> $$

> [!proposition] Proposition 16.3: Generated Subalgebra Closure Operator
> For an $\Omega$-algebra $\mathbf A$, the assignment
> $$
> S\mapsto\langle S\rangle_{\mathbf A}
> $$
> is a closure operator on $|\mathbf A|$. Its closed subsets are exactly the carriers of subalgebras of $\mathbf A$.

> [!proposition] Proposition 16.4: Relational Closure Operator
> For a relational constructor system $\mathcal R$ on $B$, the assignment
> $$
> S\mapsto\operatorname{Cl}_{\mathcal R}(S)
> $$
> is a closure operator on $B$. Its closed subsets are exactly the relation-closed subsets of $B$.

> [!definition] Definition 16.5: Algebraic Closure Operator
> A closure operator $\operatorname{cl}$ on $B$ is algebraic if for every $S\subseteq B$,
> $$
> \operatorname{cl}(S)=\bigcup\{\operatorname{cl}(F):F\subseteq S\text{ and }F\text{ is finite}\}.
> $$

> [!proposition] Proposition 16.6: Finitary Operations Induce Algebraic Closure
> If $\mathbf A$ is a finitary $\Omega$-algebra, then $S\mapsto\langle S\rangle_{\mathbf A}$ is an algebraic closure operator. If $\mathcal R$ is a finitary relational constructor system, then $S\mapsto\operatorname{Cl}_{\mathcal R}(S)$ is algebraic.

> [!warning] Warning 16.7: Infinitary Arities Change the Closure Law
> If operations of infinite arity are admitted, an element generated from $S$ may depend on infinitely many elements of $S$. The finite-subset formula for algebraic closure may fail unless explicit compactness or finitary-dependence hypotheses are imposed.

### 16.2. Rank and Stage of Generated Elements

> [!definition] Definition 16.8: Algebraic Construction Rank
> Let $\mathbf A$ be a finitary $\Omega$-algebra and $S\subseteq |\mathbf A|$. With stages $G_m(S)$ as in Construction 2.19, the construction rank of $a\in\langle S\rangle_{\mathbf A}$ relative to $S$ is
> $$
> \operatorname{rk}_S(a)=\min\{m<\omega:a\in G_m(S)\}.
> $$

> [!proposition] Proposition 16.9: Rank Bound for Operation Values
> Let $f\in\Omega_n$ and let $a_i\in\langle S\rangle_{\mathbf A}$ for $i<n$. Then
> $$
> \operatorname{rk}_S(f^{\mathbf A}(a_0,\dots,a_{n-1}))
> \leq
> 1+\max_{i<n}\operatorname{rk}_S(a_i).
> $$
> Equality need not hold in nonfree algebras because the operation value may also have a shorter representation.

> [!definition] Definition 16.10: Minimal Term Rank Under Evaluation
> Let $g:X\to |\mathbf B|$ and $b\in\langle g[X]\rangle_{\mathbf B}$. The minimal term rank of $b$ relative to $g$ is
> $$
> \operatorname{mtr}_g(b)=\min\{\operatorname{ht}(t):t\in T_{\Omega}(X),\ \operatorname{ev}_g(t)=b\}.
> $$

> [!warning] Warning 16.11: Rank Is Not Intrinsic Without a Generating Set
> The rank $\operatorname{rk}_S(a)$ depends on the chosen generator set $S$ and on the operation signature. Expanding the signature or enlarging $S$ may lower ranks.

> [!proposition] Proposition 16.12: Rank in a Free Term Algebra
> In $\mathbf T_{\Omega}(X)$, the construction rank of a term relative to $\eta_X[X]$ agrees with its height, up to the convention that variables and nullary symbols have rank $0$.

### 16.3. Closure Systems and Lattice Structure

> [!definition] Definition 16.13: Closure System
> A closure system on a set $B$ is a family $\mathcal C\subseteq\mathcal P(B)$ closed under arbitrary intersections and containing $B$.

> [!theorem] Theorem 16.14: Closure Operators and Closure Systems
> Closure operators on $B$ are in bijective correspondence with closure systems on $B$. The closed sets of a closure operator form a closure system, and a closure system $\mathcal C$ determines the closure operator
> $$
> \operatorname{cl}_{\mathcal C}(S)=\bigcap\{C\in\mathcal C:S\subseteq C\}.
> $$

> [!proposition] Proposition 16.15: Subalgebra Lattice
> The set $\operatorname{Sub}(\mathbf A)$ of subalgebras of an $\Omega$-algebra $\mathbf A$, ordered by inclusion of carriers, is a complete lattice. Meets are intersections of carriers. Joins are generated subalgebras of unions:
> $$
> \bigvee_{i\in I}\mathbf A_i
> =
> \left\langle\bigcup_{i\in I}|\mathbf A_i|\right\rangle_{\mathbf A}.
> $$

> [!remark] Remark 16.16: Two Lattices Associated with an Algebra
> An algebra $\mathbf A$ has at least two central complete lattices: its subalgebra lattice $\operatorname{Sub}(\mathbf A)$ and its congruence lattice $\operatorname{Con}(\mathbf A)$. The former organizes closed subsets of the carrier; the latter organizes compatible identifications of the carrier.

### 16.4. Compact Generation and Finite Witnesses

> [!definition] Definition 16.17: Compact Element in a Complete Lattice
> Let $L$ be a complete lattice. An element $c\in L$ is compact if whenever
> $$
> c\leq\bigvee D
> $$
> for a directed subset $D\subseteq L$, there exists $d\in D$ such that $c\leq d$.

> [!definition] Definition 16.18: Directed Family
> A subset $D$ of a poset $P$ is directed if it is nonempty and for all $d_0,d_1\in D$ there exists $d_2\in D$ such that
> $$
> d_0\leq d_2
> \qquad\text{and}\qquad
> d_1\leq d_2.
> $$

> [!proposition] Proposition 16.19: Finite Generation and Compactness of Subalgebras
> In the subalgebra lattice of a finitary algebra, every finitely generated subalgebra is compact. Conversely, under the usual finitary algebraic-closure setting, compact closed sets are generated by finite subsets.

> [!remark] Remark 16.20: Compactness as Finite Dependency
> Compactness in a lattice of generated objects formalizes finite dependency: membership or containment can be witnessed at a finite stage or by a finitely generated subobject.

## 17. Expanded Reference on Congruences and Quotients

### 17.1. Compatibility Tests

> [!lemma] Lemma 17.1: Operationwise Congruence Test
> Let $\theta$ be an equivalence relation on $|\mathbf A|$. Then $\theta$ is a congruence if and only if for every $f\in\Omega_n$ and every pair of tuples $\vec a,\vec b\in |\mathbf A|^n$ satisfying $(a_i,b_i)\in\theta$ for all $i<n$, one has
> $$
> (f^{\mathbf A}(\vec a),f^{\mathbf A}(\vec b))\in\theta.
> $$

> [!lemma] Lemma 17.2: Unary Translation Test
> Let $\Omega$ be finitary. For each $f\in\Omega_n$, each coordinate $i<n$, and each fixed tuple of parameters $\vec p\in |\mathbf A|^{n-1}$, define the unary translation
> $$
> \lambda_{f,i,\vec p}(x)=f^{\mathbf A}(p_0,\dots,p_{i-1},x,p_i,\dots,p_{n-2}).
> $$
> An equivalence relation $\theta$ is a congruence if and only if it is preserved by every such unary translation.

> [!definition] Definition 17.3: Term Operation
> Let $\mathbf A$ be an $\Omega$-algebra. Each term $t\in T_{\Omega}(\{x_0,\dots,x_{m-1}\})$ induces a term operation
> $$
> t^{\mathbf A}:|\mathbf A|^m\to |\mathbf A|
> $$
> by evaluation under valuations of the variables.

> [!proposition] Proposition 17.4: Term-Operation Compatibility
> A congruence $\theta$ on $\mathbf A$ is compatible with every term operation of $\mathbf A$. Conversely, if an equivalence relation is compatible with the basic operation interpretations, then it is compatible with every term operation.

> [!warning] Warning 17.5: Equivalence Relation Alone Is Insufficient
> Reflexivity, symmetry, and transitivity do not control operation values. Quotients of algebras require operation compatibility, not merely partitioning of the carrier.

### 17.2. Generated Congruences in Term Algebras

> [!definition] Definition 17.6: Elementary Equational Step
> Let $E\subseteq T_{\Omega}(X)^2$. An elementary equational step from $s$ to $t$ relative to $E$ is a pair obtained by replacing an occurrence of one side of an equation in $E$ by the other side inside a one-hole context, possibly after applying a substitution.

> [!definition] Definition 17.7: Equational Chain
> An equational chain from $s$ to $t$ relative to $E$ is a finite sequence
> $$
> s=t_0,t_1,\dots,t_m=t
> $$
> such that each adjacent pair is an elementary equational step in either direction.

> [!proposition] Proposition 17.8: Equational Chains and Generated Congruence
> For $s,t\in T_{\Omega}(X)$,
> $$
> (s,t)\in\theta_E
> $$
> if and only if there exists an equational chain from $s$ to $t$ relative to $E$, using closure under contexts, substitutions when the equation scheme is intended schematically, and equivalence closure.

> [!warning] Warning 17.9: Object-Level Equations Versus Equation Schemes
> A subset $E\subseteq T_{\Omega}(X)^2$ over a fixed variable set gives object-level equations. If the intended equations are schemes closed under arbitrary substitutions, substitution closure must be stated explicitly or obtained by passing to the fully invariant congruence generated by $E$.

### 17.3. Quotient Maps and Factorization Patterns

> [!proposition] Proposition 17.10: Kernel Inclusion Factorization Pattern
> For homomorphisms $h:\mathbf A\to\mathbf B$ and $q_\theta:\mathbf A\to\mathbf A/\theta$, the following data are equivalent:
> 
> 1. a homomorphism $\bar h:\mathbf A/\theta\to\mathbf B$ with $\bar h\circ q_\theta=h$;
> 2. the inclusion $\theta\subseteq\ker h$;
> 3. constancy of $h$ on every $\theta$-equivalence class;
> 4. well-definedness of the formula $\bar h([a]_\theta)=h(a)$.

> [!proposition] Proposition 17.11: Quotient Comparison Map
> If $\theta\subseteq\psi$ are congruences on $\mathbf A$, there exists a unique surjective homomorphism
> $$
> \pi_{\theta,\psi}:\mathbf A/\theta\to\mathbf A/\psi
> $$
> satisfying
> $$
> \pi_{\theta,\psi}([a]_\theta)=[a]_\psi.
> $$

> [!theorem] Theorem 17.12: Correspondence Theorem for Congruences
> Let $\theta\in\operatorname{Con}(\mathbf A)$. Congruences on $\mathbf A/\theta$ are in inclusion-preserving bijection with congruences $\psi\in\operatorname{Con}(\mathbf A)$ satisfying $\theta\subseteq\psi$. The correspondence sends $\psi$ to
> $$
> \psi/\theta=\{([a]_\theta,[b]_\theta):(a,b)\in\psi\}.
> $$

> [!corollary] Corollary 17.13: Iterated Quotients
> If $\theta\subseteq\psi$ are congruences on $\mathbf A$, then
> $$
> (\mathbf A/\theta)/(\psi/\theta)\cong\mathbf A/\psi.
> $$

### 17.4. Kernels and Images as Structural Diagnostics

> [!definition] Definition 17.14: Faithful Homomorphism
> A homomorphism $h:\mathbf A\to\mathbf B$ is faithful when its kernel is diagonal. In algebraic contexts this is equivalent to injectivity of the underlying function.

> [!definition] Definition 17.15: Full Image Relative to a Chosen Subalgebra
> Let $\mathbf C\leq\mathbf B$. A homomorphism $h:\mathbf A\to\mathbf B$ is full onto $\mathbf C$ if
> $$
> \operatorname{im}(h)=\mathbf C.
> $$

> [!proposition] Proposition 17.16: Diagnostic Table for a Homomorphism
> Let $h:\mathbf A\to\mathbf B$ be a homomorphism. Then:
> 
> 1. $h$ is injective exactly when $\ker h=\Delta_{|\mathbf A|}$;
> 2. $h$ is surjective onto $\mathbf B$ exactly when $\operatorname{im}(h)=\mathbf B$;
> 3. $h$ is an isomorphism exactly when both conditions hold;
> 4. $h$ identifies precisely the pairs in $\ker h$;
> 5. $h$ reaches precisely the subalgebra $\operatorname{im}(h)$.

## 18. Expanded Reference on Free Algebras

### 18.1. Initiality Formulation

> [!definition] Definition 18.1: Category of Algebras Under a Set of Generators
> Fix a set $X$. The comma category $X\downarrow U$ has objects $(\mathbf A,g)$ where $\mathbf A$ is an $\Omega$-algebra and $g:X\to |\mathbf A|$. A morphism
> $$
> h:(\mathbf A,g)\to(\mathbf B,k)
> $$
> is a homomorphism $h:\mathbf A\to\mathbf B$ satisfying
> $$
> h\circ g=k.
> $$

> [!proposition] Proposition 18.2: Free Algebra as Initial Object Under $X$
> A pair $(\mathbf F_X,\eta_X)$ is a free $\Omega$-algebra on $X$ if and only if it is an initial object of $X\downarrow U$.

> [!remark] Remark 18.3: Meaning of Unique Extension
> The phrase unique homomorphic extension is the assertion that for each object $(\mathbf A,g)$ of $X\downarrow U$, there is exactly one morphism from $(\mathbf F_X,\eta_X)$ to $(\mathbf A,g)$.

### 18.2. Comparison Maps

> [!construction] Construction 18.4: Canonical Comparison Map from Terms
> Let $\mathbf A$ be an $\Omega$-algebra and let $g:X\to |\mathbf A|$. The canonical comparison map from syntax to $\mathbf A$ is
> $$
> \chi_g=\operatorname{ev}_g:\mathbf T_{\Omega}(X)\to\mathbf A.
> $$
> If $\mathbf A$ is generated by $g[X]$, then the codomain may be restricted to $\langle g[X]\rangle_{\mathbf A}$ and $\chi_g$ is surjective.

> [!definition] Definition 18.5: Presentation Kernel
> The presentation kernel of a generated pair $(\mathbf A,g)$ is
> $$
> \kappa_g=\ker(\chi_g).
> $$
> It is the complete set of term identifications holding in $\mathbf A$ under the named generators.

> [!proposition] Proposition 18.6: Comparison Map Classification
> Let $\mathbf A$ be generated by $g[X]$. Then:
> 
> 1. $\chi_g$ is surjective;
> 2. $\chi_g$ is injective if and only if $(\mathbf A,g)$ is free on $X$;
> 3. $\mathbf A\cong\mathbf T_{\Omega}(X)/\kappa_g$;
> 4. two generated pairs $(\mathbf A,g)$ and $(\mathbf B,h)$ have isomorphic presentations over $X$ when $\kappa_g=\kappa_h$.

### 18.3. Basis-Like Language and Its Limits

> [!definition] Definition 18.7: Algebraic Basis in a Free Algebra
> A subset $B\subseteq |\mathbf F|$ is a free basis for an $\Omega$-algebra $\mathbf F$ when the inclusion $B\hookrightarrow |\mathbf F|$ makes $\mathbf F$ free on $B$.

> [!warning] Warning 18.8: Generating Set Need Not Be a Free Basis
> A generating set $S$ for $\mathbf A$ need not be a free basis. A relation among terms in $S$ gives a nontrivial kernel of the evaluation map from $\mathbf T_{\Omega}(S)$ to $\mathbf A$.

> [!example] Example 18.9: Free Monoid Versus Cyclic Group
> Let $\Omega$ be the monoid signature with binary multiplication and nullary identity. The free monoid on one generator has carrier the words $x^n$ for $n\in\mathbb N$. The cyclic group of order two is generated as a monoid by an element $a$ satisfying
> $$
> a^2=e.
> $$
> The evaluation map from the free monoid on $x$ to this cyclic group has nontrivial kernel identifying $x^{n+2}$ with $x^n$ for every $n$.

> [!example] Example 18.10: Free Magma Versus Semigroup Quotient
> In the binary magma signature, the terms
> $$
> m(m(x,y),z)
> $$
> and
> $$
> m(x,m(y,z))
> $$
> are distinct. In a semigroup presented as a magma satisfying associativity, their classes are equal in the quotient by the associativity congruence.

### 18.4. Nullary Operations in Free Algebras

> [!proposition] Proposition 18.11: Nullary Symbols Are Always Present in Subalgebras
> If $c\in\Omega_0$ and $\mathbf S\leq\mathbf A$, then
> $$
> c^{\mathbf A}=c^{\mathbf S}\in |\mathbf S|.
> $$
> Hence a subalgebra carrier must contain every distinguished nullary value.

> [!remark] Remark 18.12: Free Algebra on the Empty Set
> The free algebra $\mathbf T_{\Omega}(\emptyset)$ consists of closed terms. If $\Omega_0=\emptyset$ and all operation symbols have positive arity, then $T_{\Omega}(\emptyset)$ may be empty. If empty carriers are not permitted for algebras in the chosen convention, this case requires an explicit nonemptiness convention or a restriction on signatures.

> [!warning] Warning 18.13: Empty Carrier Conventions
> Universal algebra texts differ on whether empty algebras are allowed. If nullary operation symbols exist, every $\Omega$-algebra carrier is nonempty. If no nullary symbols exist, empty carriers may or may not be admitted depending on convention. The existence of $\mathbf T_{\Omega}(\emptyset)$ as an algebra with empty carrier depends on that convention.

## 19. Expanded Reference on Representations and Encodings

### 19.1. Explicit Isomorphisms Among Presentations

> [!construction] Construction 19.1: Term-to-Tree Map
> Define
> $$
> \alpha_{T,Tr}:T_{\Omega}(X)\to\operatorname{Tr}_{\Omega}(X)
> $$
> recursively by
> $$
> \alpha_{T,Tr}(\eta_X(x))=\eta^{\mathrm{tr}}(x)
> $$
> and
> $$
> \alpha_{T,Tr}(f(t_0,\dots,t_{n-1}))
> =
> N_f(\alpha_{T,Tr}(t_0),\dots,\alpha_{T,Tr}(t_{n-1})).
> $$

> [!construction] Construction 19.2: Tree-to-Term Map
> Define
> $$
> \alpha_{Tr,T}:\operatorname{Tr}_{\Omega}(X)\to T_{\Omega}(X)
> $$
> by structural recursion on the unique tree decomposition:
> $$
> \alpha_{Tr,T}(\eta^{\mathrm{tr}}(x))=\eta_X(x),
> $$
> and
> $$
> \alpha_{Tr,T}(N_f(\tau_0,\dots,\tau_{n-1}))
> =
> f(\alpha_{Tr,T}(\tau_0),\dots,\alpha_{Tr,T}(\tau_{n-1})).
> $$

> [!theorem] Theorem 19.3: Term-Tree Inverse Isomorphisms
> The maps $\alpha_{T,Tr}$ and $\alpha_{Tr,T}$ are inverse $\Omega$-algebra isomorphisms commuting with the generator maps.

> [!construction] Construction 19.4: Term-to-Tuple Map
> Define
> $$
> \alpha_{T,Tu}:T_{\Omega}(X)\to |\mathbf{Tu}_{\Omega}(X)|
> $$
> by
> $$
> \alpha_{T,Tu}(\eta_X(x))=({\mathtt{var}},x)
> $$
> and
> $$
> \alpha_{T,Tu}(f(t_0,\dots,t_{n-1}))
> =({\mathtt{op}},f,(\alpha_{T,Tu}(t_0),\dots,\alpha_{T,Tu}(t_{n-1}))).
> $$

> [!theorem] Theorem 19.5: Tuple Isomorphism
> The term-to-tuple map is an $\Omega$-algebra isomorphism from $\mathbf T_{\Omega}(X)$ to $\mathbf{Tu}_{\Omega}(X)$.

### 19.2. Coding Discipline

> [!definition] Definition 19.6: Tagging Scheme
> A tagging scheme for syntax over $X$ and $\Omega$ is a family of injective maps into a common universe whose ranges are pairwise disjoint:
> $$
> X\to U,
> \qquad
> \Omega\to U,
> \qquad
> D\to U,
> $$
> together with constructor tags distinguishing variables, operation applications, delimiters, holes, and auxiliary metadata.

> [!warning] Warning 19.7: Untagged Encodings Can Identify Distinct Syntax
> If the same raw object is used to code a variable, an operation symbol, and a delimiter, then syntactically distinct objects may receive the same code. A representation theorem requires injective, disjoint coding or an explicit quotient explaining the intended identifications.

> [!definition] Definition 19.8: Faithful Encoding
> A representation map
> $$
> e:T_{\Omega}(X)\to C
> $$
> into a coding set $C$ is faithful if it is injective. It is complete relative to a well-formed subset $W\subseteq C$ if $e[T_{\Omega}(X)]=W$.

> [!proposition] Proposition 19.9: Representation by Codes
> A coding system presents the term algebra exactly when its encoding map is faithful and complete and when the transported constructor operations on the well-formed codes are computable or set-theoretically defined according to the intended level of analysis.

### 19.3. Parentheses, Precedence, and Associativity as Quotient or Parsing Data

> [!definition] Definition 19.10: Parsing Convention
> A parsing convention is additional data assigning a unique parse tree to each string in a notation class. Examples include precedence levels, associativity declarations, reserved delimiters, fixed arity tables, and lexical tokenization rules.

> [!warning] Warning 19.11: Associativity Convention Is Not Associativity Equation
> A convention that the string $x+y+z$ parses as $(x+y)+z$ is a parsing convention. An equation asserting
> $$
> (x+y)+z=x+(y+z)
> $$
> is an algebraic identification. The former selects a syntax tree; the latter imposes a congruence.

> [!warning] Warning 19.12: Precedence Does Not Remove Semantic Questions
> Precedence determines parse structure for unparenthesized strings. It does not assert identities in the target algebra and does not decide equality modulo a presentation.

> [!example] Example 19.13: Infix Arithmetic Notation
> In a signature with binary $+$ and binary $\cdot$, the string
> $$
> x+y\cdot z
> $$
> may parse as
> $$
> +(x,\cdot(y,z))
> $$
> under multiplication-before-addition convention. This is a syntax convention. It does not assert distributivity, associativity, or commutativity.

### 19.4. Representation Comparison Table

| Presentation | Carrier Form | Constructor Mechanism | Main Risk | Faithfulness Condition |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Abstract terms | inductively generated formal objects | formal operation application | mistaking notation for implementation | built into term formation |
| Addressed trees | finite prefix-closed labelled domains | new labelled root with translated subtrees | address bookkeeping | unique decomposition of labelled domains |
| Tagged tuples | nested tagged records | tuple tag plus symbol plus child tuple | untagged collision | disjoint tags and tuple equality |

| Hash-consed terms | canonical stored nodes | unique allocation per constructor tuple | implementation invariant failure | pointer equality agrees with structural equality |

## 20. Expanded Reference on Contexts, Substitution, and Equations

### 20.1. Contexts with Multiple Holes

> [!definition] Definition 20.1: Multi-Hole Context
> Let $H=\{\Box_0,\dots,\Box_{m-1}\}$ be a set of distinguished hole symbols disjoint from $X$ and $\Omega$. An $m$-hole context is a term
> $$
> C\in T_{\Omega}(X\amalg H)
> $$
> in which each hole symbol occurs at least once or exactly once, according to the specified convention.

> [!construction] Construction 20.2: Filling a Multi-Hole Context
> Given an $m$-hole context $C$ and terms $t_0,\dots,t_{m-1}\in T_{\Omega}(X)$, the filled term
> $$
> C[t_0,\dots,t_{m-1}]
> $$
> is obtained by the substitution homomorphism extending $\Box_i\mapsto t_i$ and fixing every $x\in X$.

> [!proposition] Proposition 20.3: Contexts as Derived Operations
> An $m$-hole context $C\in T_{\Omega}(X\amalg H)$ determines an $m$-ary derived operation on $T_{\Omega}(X)$ by
> $$
> (t_0,\dots,t_{m-1})\mapsto C[t_0,\dots,t_{m-1}].
> $$
> If $\theta$ is a congruence, then this derived operation preserves $\theta$ in each coordinate.

### 20.2. Substitution Laws

> [!proposition] Proposition 20.4: Identity Substitution
> Let $\iota_X:X\to T_{\Omega}(X)$ be $\eta_X$. The induced substitution homomorphism
> $$
> \overline{\iota_X}:\mathbf T_{\Omega}(X)\to\mathbf T_{\Omega}(X)
> $$
> is the identity homomorphism.

> [!proposition] Proposition 20.5: Associativity of Substitution
> For substitutions
> $$
> \sigma:X\to T_{\Omega}(Y),
> \qquad
> \tau:Y\to T_{\Omega}(Z),
> \qquad
> \upsilon:Z\to T_{\Omega}(W),
> $$
> one has
> $$
> \upsilon\star(\tau\star\sigma)=(\upsilon\star\tau)\star\sigma.
> $$

> [!proposition] Proposition 20.6: Substitution and Context Filling
> If $C$ is an $m$-hole context and $\sigma:X\to T_{\Omega}(Y)$ is a substitution on ordinary variables, then applying $\sigma$ after filling $C$ agrees with filling the substituted context by the substituted terms:
> $$
> \overline\sigma(C[t_0,\dots,t_{m-1}])
> =
> (\overline\sigma C)[\overline\sigma(t_0),\dots,\overline\sigma(t_{m-1})].
> $$

### 20.3. Varieties and Equational Classes

> [!definition] Definition 20.7: Class Satisfying Equations
> Let $E$ be a set of equations over a sufficiently large variable set. A class $\mathcal K$ of $\Omega$-algebras satisfies $E$ if every $\mathbf A\in\mathcal K$ satisfies every equation in $E$ under every valuation.

> [!definition] Definition 20.8: Variety
> A variety of $\Omega$-algebras is a class of $\Omega$-algebras axiomatized by a set of equations. Equivalently, it is a class of the form
> $$
> \operatorname{Mod}_{\Omega}(E)=\{\mathbf A:\mathbf A\models E\}
> $$
> for some equation set $E$.

> [!definition] Definition 20.9: Free Algebra in a Variety
> Let $\mathcal V$ be a variety of $\Omega$-algebras. A free algebra on $X$ in $\mathcal V$ is a pair $(\mathbf F^{\mathcal V}_X,\eta_X)$ with $\mathbf F^{\mathcal V}_X\in\mathcal V$ such that every map $X\to |\mathbf A|$ into an algebra $\mathbf A\in\mathcal V$ extends uniquely to a homomorphism $\mathbf F^{\mathcal V}_X\to\mathbf A$.

> [!theorem] Theorem 20.10: Free Algebra in an Equational Variety
> If $\mathcal V=\operatorname{Mod}_{\Omega}(E)$, then the free algebra in $\mathcal V$ on $X$ is
> $$
> \mathbf T_{\Omega}(X)/\theta_{\mathcal V,X},
> $$
> where $\theta_{\mathcal V,X}$ is the congruence of all equations over $X$ valid in every algebra of $\mathcal V$.

> [!warning] Warning 20.11: Free in All Algebras Versus Free in a Variety
> The raw term algebra is free in the category of all $\Omega$-algebras. A free group, free monoid, free ring, or free lattice is usually free in a narrower category axiomatized by equations, hence is a quotient of raw syntax by the congruence enforcing those equations.

## 21. Expanded Reference on Algebraic Examples

### 21.1. Magmas and Semigroups

> [!example] Example 21.1: Free Magma
> Let $\Omega$ contain one binary symbol $m$. The free magma on $X$ has terms generated by
> $$
> x\in X
> $$
> and
> $$
> m(s,t).
> $$
> Its tree presentation consists of finite full binary trees with leaves labelled by elements of $X$.

> [!example] Example 21.2: Semigroup as Quotient of Free Magma
> The variety of semigroups is obtained from the magma signature by imposing associativity:
> $$
> m(m(x,y),z)\approx m(x,m(y,z)).
> $$
> The free semigroup on $X$ is the quotient of the free magma by the congruence generated by all substitution instances of associativity.

> [!remark] Remark 21.3: Parenthesization as Syntax
> In the free magma, different parenthesizations are generally different terms. In the free semigroup, parenthesizations of the same nonempty word are identified by the associativity congruence.

### 21.2. Monoids and Groups

> [!example] Example 21.4: Free Monoid
> In the monoid variety, the free monoid on $X$ has carrier $X^{<\omega}$, the set of finite words in $X$, with concatenation as multiplication and the empty word as identity. It is a quotient of raw binary-operation syntax by associativity and identity equations.

> [!example] Example 21.5: Free Group
> In the group signature with multiplication, inverse, and identity, the free group on $X$ may be represented by reduced words in the alphabet $X\amalg X^{-1}$. The reduction procedure gives canonical representatives for the quotient of raw group terms by the group equations.

> [!warning] Warning 21.6: Reduced Words Are Normal Forms, Not Raw Terms
> A reduced word in a free group is a normal-form representative of an equivalence class of group terms. It is not the same object as an arbitrary raw term in the group signature.

### 21.3. Rings and Modules

> [!example] Example 21.7: Polynomial Algebra as Free Commutative Algebra
> For a commutative ring $R$ and set $X$, the polynomial ring $R[X]$ is the free commutative $R$-algebra on $X$. It is not the raw term algebra of the ring signature; it is a quotient by ring axioms, commutativity of multiplication, and scalar-compatibility equations.

> [!example] Example 21.8: Free Module
> For a ring $R$ and set $X$, the free left $R$-module on $X$ has carrier the set of finitely supported functions
> $$
> X\to R.
> $$
> It is generated by the basis vectors $e_x$ and satisfies the universal property for maps from $X$ into the underlying set of any left $R$-module.

> [!remark] Remark 21.9: Linear Combination Normal Forms
> Finite-support functions provide canonical normal forms for formal finite $R$-linear combinations after quotienting by the module axioms.

### 21.4. Lattices and Boolean Algebras

> [!example] Example 21.10: Lattice Terms
> In the lattice signature $(\wedge,\vee)$, raw terms distinguish expressions such as
> $$
> x\wedge(y\vee z)
> $$
> and
> $$
> (x\wedge y)\vee(x\wedge z).
> $$
> These become equal in distributive lattices but not in all lattices.

> [!example] Example 21.11: Boolean Algebra Semantics
> In Boolean algebras, formula-like terms modulo Boolean algebra equations yield equivalence classes corresponding to Boolean functions when the variable set is finite. For infinite variable sets, each term depends on finitely many variables.

> [!warning] Warning 21.12: Semantic Equivalence Depends on the Class of Algebras
> Two lattice terms may be equal in all Boolean algebras, all distributive lattices, a specific lattice, or only under a specific valuation. These are different equivalence relations on the term algebra.

## 22. Expanded Reference on Binding-Sensitive Syntax

### 22.1. Named Variables and Alpha-Equivalence

> [!definition] Definition 22.1: Binder Constructor with Named Variables
> A binder constructor is a syntax-forming operation whose data include a variable name and a body, such as
> $$
> \forall x\,\varphi.
> $$
> Its formal behavior is not captured by an ordinary unary operation on formulas unless the binding occurrence of $x$ and the scope of the binder are included in the syntax object.

> [!definition] Definition 22.2: Alpha-Equivalence
> Alpha-equivalence is the equivalence relation on binding syntax generated by renaming bound variables in a way that avoids capture. For example,
> $$
> \forall x\,R(x)
> $$
> and
> $$
> \forall y\,R(y)
> $$
> are alpha-equivalent when $y$ is not captured by the renaming.

> [!warning] Warning 22.3: Literal Equality and Alpha-Equivalence Differ
> With named variables, two formulas may be alpha-equivalent but not literally equal as strings or trees. A quotient by alpha-equivalence or a nameless representation must be specified if bound-variable names are to be considered immaterial.

### 22.2. De Bruijn Indices

> [!definition] Definition 22.4: De Bruijn Representation
> In a de Bruijn representation, a bound variable occurrence is encoded by a natural number recording the number of binders between the occurrence and its binder. Free variables are represented by a separate environment or by named free-variable tags.

> [!remark] Remark 22.5: Binding Without Alpha-Equivalence Quotient
> De Bruijn syntax replaces alpha-equivalence of named binders by literal equality of nameless indices, provided shifting and substitution operations are defined with the correct index arithmetic.

> [!warning] Warning 22.6: Substitution Requires Shifting
> In de Bruijn syntax, substituting a term under a binder requires shifting free indices to prevent accidental capture. The operation is not ordinary term substitution unless the shifting discipline is part of the formal definition.

### 22.3. Formula Evaluation and Satisfaction

> [!definition] Definition 22.7: Assignment Update
> Let $\mathcal M$ be a first-order structure with carrier $M$, let $s:V\to M$ be an assignment, let $x\in V$, and let $a\in M$. The updated assignment $s[x:=a]$ is defined by
> $$
> s[x:=a](y)=
> \begin{cases}
> a, & y=x,\\
> s(y), & y\ne x.
> \end{cases}
> $$

> [!definition] Definition 22.8: Satisfaction for Quantifiers
> Satisfaction of quantified formulas is defined by
> $$
> \mathcal M,s\models\forall x\,\varphi
> $$
> if and only if for every $a\in M$,
> $$
> \mathcal M,s[x:=a]\models\varphi.
> $$
> Similarly,
> $$
> \mathcal M,s\models\exists x\,\varphi
> $$
> if and only if there exists $a\in M$ such that
> $$
> \mathcal M,s[x:=a]\models\varphi.
> $$

> [!warning] Warning 22.9: Formula Semantics Is Not an $\Omega$-Algebra Homomorphism Alone
> The semantic clauses for quantifiers quantify over updated assignments. They are not merely truth-functions on the truth values of immediate subformulas under a fixed assignment.

## 23. Expanded Reference on Effective Algorithms

### 23.1. Recognizers and Destructors for Concrete Syntax

> [!definition] Definition 23.1: Decidable Constructor Range
> In a representation carrier $R\subseteq D$, the range of a constructor $C_f:R^n\to R$ is decidable in $D$ if there is an algorithm which, given $d\in D$, determines whether $d=C_f(r_0,\dots,r_{n-1})$ for some $r_i\in R$.

> [!definition] Definition 23.2: Effective Unique Readability
> A concrete syntax representation has effective unique readability if it has unique readability and there are algorithms which, for every well-formed object, identify its outer case and return its immediate components.

> [!proposition] Proposition 23.3: Effective Structural Recursion
> If a representation has effective unique readability and the recursive clauses are computable, then the induced structural recursion function is computable on well-formed inputs.

> [!warning] Warning 23.4: Set-Theoretic Recursion May Be Noncomputable
> A function defined by structural recursion exists set-theoretically once the recursion data are functions. It is computable only when the representation and all recursion data are computable.

### 23.2. Parsing Correctness Conditions

> [!definition] Definition 23.5: Sound Parser
> A parser $p:D\rightharpoonup T_{\Omega}(X)$ is sound for a string language $W\subseteq D$ if whenever $p(d)$ is defined, $d\in W$ and the flattening of $p(d)$ is $d$.

> [!definition] Definition 23.6: Complete Parser
> A parser $p:D\rightharpoonup T_{\Omega}(X)$ is complete for $W$ if $p(d)$ is defined for every $d\in W$.

> [!definition] Definition 23.7: Unambiguous Parser
> A parsing specification is unambiguous if every $d\in W$ has exactly one parse tree.

> [!theorem] Theorem 23.8: Parser Adequacy
> A parser is adequate for a string representation of terms when it is sound, complete, unambiguous, and inverse to the chosen flattening map on the well-formed language.

### 23.3. Equality Algorithms

> [!definition] Definition 23.9: Structural Equality Decision Procedure
> A structural equality decision procedure for a representation $R$ is an algorithm deciding whether two elements of $R$ are equal as represented syntax objects.

> [!definition] Definition 23.10: Semantic Equality Decision Procedure
> Given a valuation $g:X\to |\mathbf B|$, a semantic equality procedure decides whether
> $$
> \operatorname{ev}_g(s)=\operatorname{ev}_g(t)
> $$
> for input terms $s,t$.

> [!definition] Definition 23.11: Quotient Equality Decision Procedure
> Given a presentation $\langle X\mid E\rangle$, a quotient equality procedure decides whether
> $$
> [s]_{\theta_E}=[t]_{\theta_E}.
> $$

> [!warning] Warning 23.12: Three Equality Problems
> Structural equality, semantic equality in a fixed target, and quotient equality modulo a presentation are different decision problems. Decidability of one does not imply decidability of the others without additional hypotheses.

## 25. Many-Sorted Variant

### 25.1. Sorted Sets and Sorted Functions

> [!definition] Definition 25.1: $S$-Sorted Set
> Let $S$ be a set of sorts. An $S$-sorted set is a family
> $$
> A=(A_s)_{s\in S}
> $$
> of sets indexed by $S$. The component $A_s$ is the carrier of sort $s$.

> [!definition] Definition 25.2: $S$-Sorted Function
> If $A=(A_s)_{s\in S}$ and $B=(B_s)_{s\in S}$ are $S$-sorted sets, an $S$-sorted function
> $$
> h:A\to B
> $$
> is a family of functions
> $$
> h_s:A_s\to B_s
> $$
> indexed by $s\in S$.

> [!definition] Definition 25.3: Sorted Product of Inputs
> For a finite word of sorts
> $$
> \vec s=(s_0,\dots,s_{n-1})\in S^n,
> $$
> and an $S$-sorted set $A$, define
> $$
> A_{\vec s}=A_{s_0}\times\cdots\times A_{s_{n-1}}.
> $$
> If $n=0$, this product is a singleton.

### 25.2. Many-Sorted Algebras

> [!definition] Definition 25.4: Many-Sorted Signature
> A many-sorted signature consists of:
> 
> 1. a set $S$ of sorts;
> 2. a set $\Omega$ of operation symbols;
> 3. for each $f\in\Omega$, an input sort word $\operatorname{in}(f)=(s_0,\dots,s_{n-1})\in S^n$;
> 4. for each $f\in\Omega$, an output sort $\operatorname{out}(f)\in S$.

> [!definition] Definition 25.5: Many-Sorted Algebra
> A many-sorted $\Omega$-algebra $\mathbf A$ consists of an $S$-sorted set $(A_s)_{s\in S}$ and, for each $f\in\Omega$ with input word $\vec s$ and output sort $t$, a function
> $$
> f^{\mathbf A}:A_{\vec s}\to A_t.
> $$

> [!definition] Definition 25.6: Many-Sorted Homomorphism
> A homomorphism $h:\mathbf A\to\mathbf B$ of many-sorted $\Omega$-algebras is an $S$-sorted function $h=(h_s)_{s\in S}$ such that for each $f\in\Omega$ with input word $(s_0,\dots,s_{n-1})$ and output sort $t$,
> $$
> h_t(f^{\mathbf A}(a_0,\dots,a_{n-1}))
> =
> f^{\mathbf B}(h_{s_0}(a_0),\dots,h_{s_{n-1}}(a_{n-1})).
> $$

> [!definition] Definition 25.7: Many-Sorted Subalgebra
> A many-sorted subalgebra $\mathbf C\leq\mathbf A$ is a family $C_s\subseteq A_s$ closed under each operation in the sense that
> $$
> (a_0,\dots,a_{n-1})\in C_{s_0}\times\cdots\times C_{s_{n-1}}
> $$
> implies
> $$
> f^{\mathbf A}(a_0,\dots,a_{n-1})\in C_t.
> $$

### 25.3. Many-Sorted Terms

> [!definition] Definition 25.8: Sorted Generator Family
> A sorted generator family is an $S$-sorted set
> $$
> X=(X_s)_{s\in S}.
> $$
> A generator $x\in X_s$ has sort $s$.

> [!construction] Construction 25.9: Many-Sorted Term Family
> The many-sorted term family
> $$
> T_{\Omega}(X)=(T_{\Omega}(X)_s)_{s\in S}
> $$
> is generated by:
> 
> 1. for each $x\in X_s$, a variable term $\eta_s(x)\in T_{\Omega}(X)_s$;
> 2. for each $f\in\Omega$ with input sorts $(s_0,\dots,s_{n-1})$ and output sort $t$, a constructor
> $$
> f:T_{\Omega}(X)_{s_0}\times\cdots\times T_{\Omega}(X)_{s_{n-1}}\to T_{\Omega}(X)_t.
> $$

> [!theorem] Theorem 25.10: Many-Sorted Term Algebra Freeness
> The many-sorted term algebra $\mathbf T_{\Omega}(X)$ is free on the sorted generator family $X$: for every many-sorted $\Omega$-algebra $\mathbf A$ and every sorted function $g:X\to |\mathbf A|$, there exists a unique many-sorted homomorphism
> $$
> \overline g:\mathbf T_{\Omega}(X)\to\mathbf A
> $$
> extending $g$ sort by sort.

> [!warning] Warning 25.11: Ill-Sorted Constructor Applications
> In many-sorted syntax, an expression $f(t_0,\dots,t_{n-1})$ is well-formed only when the sort of each $t_i$ matches the $i$-th input sort of $f$. Ill-sorted expressions are not terms.

### 25.4. Sorted Quotients and Congruences

> [!definition] Definition 25.12: Many-Sorted Congruence
> A congruence on a many-sorted algebra $\mathbf A$ is a family of equivalence relations
> $$
> \theta=(\theta_s\subseteq A_s\times A_s)_{s\in S}
> $$
> compatible with every operation: if $f$ has input sorts $(s_0,\dots,s_{n-1})$ and output sort $t$, and $(a_i,b_i)\in\theta_{s_i}$ for all $i<n$, then
> $$
> (f^{\mathbf A}(a_0,\dots,a_{n-1}),f^{\mathbf A}(b_0,\dots,b_{n-1}))\in\theta_t.
> $$

> [!construction] Construction 25.13: Many-Sorted Quotient
> If $\theta$ is a many-sorted congruence on $\mathbf A$, then $\mathbf A/\theta$ has sort carrier
> $$
> (A_s/\theta_s)_{s\in S}
> $$
> and operations defined on equivalence classes by the same representative formula as in the one-sorted case.

## 26. Term Operations, Clones, and Derived Algebraic Structure

### 26.1. Term Operations

> [!definition] Definition 26.1: $n$-Ary Term Over Variables
> Fix variables $x_0,\dots,x_{n-1}$. An $n$-ary term is an element of
> $$
> T_{\Omega}(\{x_0,\dots,x_{n-1}\}).
> $$

> [!construction] Construction 26.2: Induced Term Operation
> Let $\mathbf A$ be an $\Omega$-algebra and let $t$ be an $n$-ary term. The induced operation
> $$
> t^{\mathbf A}:|\mathbf A|^n\to |\mathbf A|
> $$
> is defined by evaluating $t$ under the valuation $x_i\mapsto a_i$:
> $$
> t^{\mathbf A}(a_0,\dots,a_{n-1})=\overline v(t).
> $$

> [!proposition] Proposition 26.3: Basic Operations as Term Operations
> Every basic operation $f^{\mathbf A}$ is a term operation, induced by the term
> $$
> f(x_0,\dots,x_{n-1}).
> $$
> Every projection operation $\pi_i^n(a_0,\dots,a_{n-1})=a_i$ is induced by the variable term $x_i$.

> [!proposition] Proposition 26.4: Closure Under Composition
> The set of all finitary term operations of $\mathbf A$ contains all projections and is closed under composition: if $t$ is $m$-ary and $s_0,\dots,s_{m-1}$ are $n$-ary terms, then
> $$
> t^{\mathbf A}(s_0^{\mathbf A},\dots,s_{m-1}^{\mathbf A})
> $$
> is induced by the substituted term $t[s_0/x_0,\dots,s_{m-1}/x_{m-1}]$.

### 26.2. Clone of an Algebra

> [!definition] Definition 26.5: Clone
> A clone on a set $A$ is a family $\mathcal C=(\mathcal C_n)_{n\geq 1}$ where $\mathcal C_n\subseteq A^{A^n}$ contains all projections and is closed under finitary composition.

> [!definition] Definition 26.6: Term Clone
> The term clone of an algebra $\mathbf A$ is the clone whose $n$-ary part consists of all $n$-ary term operations on $|\mathbf A|$.

> [!remark] Remark 26.7: What the Term Clone Forgets
> The term clone records the operations definable by terms in $\mathbf A$. It forgets the syntactic terms that induce the same operation. The map from terms to term operations can have a nontrivial kernel.

> [!definition] Definition 26.8: Polynomial Operation with Parameters
> A polynomial operation on $\mathbf A$ is obtained by evaluating a term in variables and additional parameters from $|\mathbf A|$. Formally, an $n$-ary polynomial operation is induced by a term over variables $x_0,\dots,x_{n-1}$ and constants naming selected elements of $|\mathbf A|$.

> [!warning] Warning 26.9: Term Operation Versus Polynomial Operation
> A term operation uses only the operations and variables of the signature. A polynomial operation may use fixed parameters from the algebra. They coincide only under additional naming or definability assumptions.

### 26.3. Compatibility with Derived Operations

> [!proposition] Proposition 26.10: Congruences Preserve Term Operations
> If $\theta\in\operatorname{Con}(\mathbf A)$ and $t$ is an $n$-ary term, then
> $$
> (a_i,b_i)\in\theta\text{ for all }i<n
> $$
> implies
> $$
> (t^{\mathbf A}(a_0,\dots,a_{n-1}),t^{\mathbf A}(b_0,\dots,b_{n-1}))\in\theta.
> $$

> [!proposition] Proposition 26.11: Homomorphisms Preserve Term Operations
> If $h:\mathbf A\to\mathbf B$ is a homomorphism and $t$ is an $n$-ary term, then
> $$
> h(t^{\mathbf A}(a_0,\dots,a_{n-1}))
> =
> t^{\mathbf B}(h(a_0),\dots,h(a_{n-1})).
> $$

> [!corollary] Corollary 26.12: Equations as Equality of Term Operations
> An equation $s\approx t$ in variables $x_0,\dots,x_{n-1}$ is valid in $\mathbf A$ if and only if
> $$
> s^{\mathbf A}=t^{\mathbf A}
> $$
> as functions $|\mathbf A|^n\to |\mathbf A|$.

## 27. Equational Theories, Varieties, and Free Quotients

### 27.1. Identities and Theories

> [!definition] Definition 27.1: Identity
> An identity of type $\Omega$ is a universally quantified equation between terms, written
> $$
> s\approx t,
> $$
> with the convention that all variables occurring in $s$ or $t$ are universally quantified over every $\Omega$-algebra under consideration.

> [!definition] Definition 27.2: Equational Theory of a Class
> For a class $\mathcal K$ of $\Omega$-algebras, its equational theory is
> $$
> \operatorname{Th}_{\mathrm{eq}}(\mathcal K)=\{s\approx t: \mathbf A\models s\approx t\text{ for every }\mathbf A\in\mathcal K\}.
> $$

> [!definition] Definition 27.3: Theory Congruence on a Term Algebra
> For a class $\mathcal K$ and variable set $X$, define
> $$
> \theta_{\mathcal K,X}=\{(s,t)\in T_{\Omega}(X)^2: \mathcal K\models s\approx t\}.
> $$
> This is a fully invariant congruence on $\mathbf T_{\Omega}(X)$.

> [!proposition] Proposition 27.4: Free Algebra of an Equational Class
> If $\mathcal V$ is a variety, then
> $$
> \mathbf F_{\mathcal V}(X)=\mathbf T_{\Omega}(X)/\theta_{\mathcal V,X}
> $$
> is free on $X$ inside $\mathcal V$.

### 27.2. Closure Properties of Varieties

> [!theorem] Theorem 27.5: HSP Closure of Equational Classes
> Every equational class of $\Omega$-algebras is closed under homomorphic images, subalgebras, and direct products. Thus if $\mathcal V=\operatorname{Mod}_{\Omega}(E)$, then $\mathcal V$ is closed under $H$, $S$, and $P$.

> [!theorem] Theorem 27.6: Birkhoff Variety Theorem
> A class of $\Omega$-algebras is axiomatizable by equations if and only if it is closed under homomorphic images, subalgebras, and direct products, subject to the chosen set/class size convention for classes of algebras.

> [!remark] Remark 27.7: Relevance to Presentations
> Presentations by generators and relations produce particular algebras. Varieties axiomatized by identities produce entire classes of algebras and determine corresponding free quotients of the raw term algebra.

> [!warning] Warning 27.8: Equations Versus Quasi-Equations
> A quasi-equation has implications between equations, such as
> $$
> s_0\approx t_0\wedge\cdots\wedge s_{k-1}\approx t_{k-1}\Rightarrow s\approx t.
> $$
> Such conditions are not equations and define classes with different closure properties.

### 27.3. Fully Invariant Congruences and Varieties

> [!definition] Definition 27.9: Fully Invariant Congruence on a Free Algebra
> A congruence $\theta$ on $\mathbf T_{\Omega}(X)$ is fully invariant when every endomorphism of $\mathbf T_{\Omega}(X)$ preserves $\theta$.

> [!proposition] Proposition 27.10: Identities Determine Fully Invariant Congruences
> If $E$ is a set of identities over $X$, the congruence generated by all substitution instances of $E$ is fully invariant.

> [!proposition] Proposition 27.11: Fully Invariant Congruences Determine Equational Quotients
> If $\theta$ is a fully invariant congruence on a sufficiently generated free term algebra, then the quotient by $\theta$ encodes a theory of identities stable under substitution.

> [!warning] Warning 27.12: Fixed-Generator Presentations Need Not Be Fully Invariant
> A congruence generated by equations over a specific generator set may not be fully invariant if the equations are intended as relations among named generators rather than universal laws.

## 28. Expanded Reference on Relational and Partial Constructors

### 28.1. Partial Algebraic Structures

> [!definition] Definition 28.1: Partial Operation
> A partial $n$-ary operation on a set $B$ is a function
> $$
> p:D\to B
> $$
> with domain $D\subseteq B^n$.

> [!definition] Definition 28.2: Partial Algebra
> A partial $\Omega$-algebra consists of a carrier $B$ and, for each $f\in\Omega_n$, a partial operation
> $$
> f^{\mathbf B}:D_f\to B
> $$
> with $D_f\subseteq B^n$.

> [!definition] Definition 28.3: Closed Set for Partial Operations
> A subset $C\subseteq B$ is closed under a partial operation $p:D\to B$ if whenever $\vec c\in C^n\cap D$, one has $p(\vec c)\in C$.

> [!warning] Warning 28.4: Undefined Inputs Are Not Failures of Closure
> For partial operations, closure only applies to input tuples on which the operation is defined. Requiring closure for undefined inputs is not meaningful unless a totalization convention is supplied.

### 28.2. Multivalued Generation

> [!definition] Definition 28.5: Multivalued Operation
> A multivalued $n$-ary operation on $B$ is a function
> $$
> M:B^n\to\mathcal P(B).
> $$
> It is equivalently a relation $R_M\subseteq B^n\times B$.

> [!definition] Definition 28.6: Closure Under a Multivalued Operation
> A subset $C\subseteq B$ is closed under $M$ if
> $$
> M(c_0,\dots,c_{n-1})\subseteq C
> $$
> for every $(c_0,\dots,c_{n-1})\in C^n$.

> [!proposition] Proposition 28.7: Relational Closure Includes Multivalued Closure
> The closure operator induced by multivalued operations is the relational closure operator for their graph relations.

> [!warning] Warning 28.8: Choice Functions Change the Theory
> Replacing a multivalued constructor by a chosen single-valued selection function changes the generated witnesses and may change the generated closure unless every possible output is selected at some stage or unless a specific choice semantics is intended.

### 28.3. Witnessed Generation and Ambiguity

> [!definition] Definition 28.9: Ambiguous Generation
> A relational generation process is ambiguous if there exist distinct derivation witnesses $d\ne e$ such that
> $$
> \operatorname{val}(d)=\operatorname{val}(e).
> $$

> [!definition] Definition 28.10: Dead Constructor Event
> A constructor event is dead relative to a base set $S$ if it is permitted by the global relation but its inputs are never simultaneously generated from $S$.

> [!definition] Definition 28.11: Redundant Constructor Event
> A constructor event is redundant relative to $S$ if its output is already generated by other witnesses before or at the same stage.

> [!proposition] Proposition 28.12: Sources of Nonunique Derivation
> Nonunique derivation can arise from multivalued relations, overlapping constructor ranges, redundant rules, cycles in non-well-founded systems, or equality of values not reflected in witness syntax.

## 29. Extended Reference on Derivation Certificates

### 29.1. Trees, DAGs, and Linearizations

> [!definition] Definition 29.1: Dependency DAG
> A dependency DAG for a generated object is a finite directed acyclic graph whose nodes are constructor or base events and whose edges point from an event to events that use its output as input.

> [!definition] Definition 29.2: Tree Unfolding of a Dependency DAG
> The tree unfolding of a dependency DAG duplicates shared dependencies along each path from the final output to a base event. It yields a derivation tree that records occurrences rather than shared nodes.

> [!definition] Definition 29.3: Linear Extension of a Build Dependency Order
> Given a finite dependency partial order on events, a linear extension is a total order of events such that every event occurs after its dependencies.

> [!warning] Warning 29.4: Linear Extension Is Not Unique
> Independent events may be ordered arbitrarily. A linear build history therefore contains scheduling information not intrinsic to the generated object.

### 29.2. Certificate Validity Predicates

> [!definition] Definition 29.5: Certificate Signature
> A certificate signature specifies the types of base events, constructor events, arities of event inputs, output type of each event, and side predicates required for validity.

> [!definition] Definition 29.6: Local Validity
> A certificate is locally valid if every event is individually well-typed, every constructor event names the correct number of input events, and each side predicate holds for the values of those inputs.

> [!definition] Definition 29.7: Global Validity
> A certificate is globally valid if it is locally valid and its dependency graph is acyclic, all referenced input events occur, all declared outputs are consistent, and the final output is designated.

> [!proposition] Proposition 29.8: Certificate Reconstruction Criterion
> A globally valid certificate reconstructs a generated object if each constructor event has a defined output from its input values and the designated final event is reachable from all events intended to contribute to the certificate.

### 29.3. Canonicality Conditions

> [!definition] Definition 29.9: Canonical Certificate Scheme
> A certificate scheme is canonical for a generation process if every generated object has exactly one valid certificate under the scheme.

> [!proposition] Proposition 29.10: Requirements for Canonical Certificates
> Canonicality of certificates generally requires unique derivability, a fixed representation of occurrences or sharing, a fixed ordering of incomparable dependencies, and a fixed normalization of redundant construction paths.

> [!warning] Warning 29.11: Freeness Alone Does Not Fix Scheduling
> Freeness can give unique decomposition of an object into constructors. It does not determine the order in which independent subobjects are constructed unless a scheduling convention is added.

## 30. Extended Categorical Reference

### 30.1. Monomorphisms, Epimorphisms, and Concrete Maps

> [!definition] Definition 30.1: Monomorphism
> A morphism $m:A\to B$ in a category is a monomorphism if for all objects $C$ and morphisms $u,v:C\to A$,
> $$
> m\circ u=m\circ v\Rightarrow u=v.
> $$

> [!definition] Definition 30.2: Epimorphism
> A morphism $e:A\to B$ in a category is an epimorphism if for all objects $C$ and morphisms $u,v:B\to C$,
> $$
> u\circ e=v\circ e\Rightarrow u=v.
> $$

> [!proposition] Proposition 30.3: Monomorphisms in $\mathbf{Alg}_{\Omega}$
> In $\mathbf{Alg}_{\Omega}$, every injective homomorphism is a monomorphism. Conversely, every monomorphism has injective underlying function.

> [!proposition] Proposition 30.4: Surjective Homomorphisms as Epimorphisms
> Every surjective homomorphism in $\mathbf{Alg}_{\Omega}$ is an epimorphism. In many familiar finitary algebraic categories, epimorphisms may require separate analysis if surjectivity is not assumed as part of the statement.

> [!warning] Warning 30.5: Categorical Epi Need Not Mean Surjective Everywhere
> In some algebraic or algebra-adjacent categories, epimorphisms need not be surjective on underlying sets. Statements using epimorphism should not be silently translated into surjectivity unless the category has that property.

### 30.2. Regular Epis and Quotients

> [!definition] Definition 30.6: Regular Epimorphism
> A morphism $e:A\to B$ is a regular epimorphism if it is the coequalizer of some parallel pair of morphisms.

> [!proposition] Proposition 30.7: Quotient Projections as Regular Epimorphisms
> In $\mathbf{Alg}_{\Omega}$, quotient projections
> $$
> q_\theta:\mathbf A\to\mathbf A/\theta
> $$
> are regular epimorphisms.

> [!definition] Definition 30.8: Effective Equivalence Relation
> An internal equivalence relation is effective if it is the kernel pair of its coequalizer. Congruences of ordinary algebras are effective in this sense under the usual quotient construction.

### 30.3. Algebraic Theories

> [!definition] Definition 30.9: Lawvere Theory of a Finitary Algebraic Theory
> A Lawvere theory is a small category with finite products whose objects are finite powers of a distinguished object. Models of the theory in $\mathbf{Set}$ are finite-product-preserving functors into $\mathbf{Set}$.

> [!remark] Remark 30.10: Relation to Signatures and Equations
> A finitary signature with equations determines a Lawvere theory whose operations are terms modulo provable equality. Conversely, a Lawvere theory packages all derived operations and their equations without choosing a particular syntactic presentation.

> [!warning] Warning 30.11: Algebraic Theory Versus Presented Algebra
> An algebraic theory specifies a category of models. A presented algebra specifies one object generated by named generators subject to named relations. These are related but not interchangeable.

## 31. Formal Glossary of Core Distinctions

> [!definition] Definition 31.1: Syntax
> Syntax is the formal algebra of expressions generated by operation symbols and variables. In this treatise, raw syntax is represented by $\mathbf T_{\Omega}(X)$ or by a concrete presentation isomorphic to it.

> [!definition] Definition 31.2: Semantics
> Semantics is an interpretation of syntax in a target algebra via a valuation and its induced evaluation homomorphism.

> [!definition] Definition 31.3: Representation
> A representation is a concrete carrier equipped with constructor operations and an isomorphism from the abstract syntax algebra, or a coding map satisfying faithfulness and completeness conditions.

> [!definition] Definition 31.4: Presentation
> A presentation by generators and relations is a quotient of a free algebra by the congruence generated by specified equations.

> [!definition] Definition 31.5: Generatedness
> Generatedness is the property that a carrier is the least substructure containing a specified subset. It yields surjectivity of the comparison map from a free object.

> [!definition] Definition 31.6: Freeness
> Freeness is a universal mapping property. For generated concrete structures, it amounts to generatedness plus no nontrivial identifications among formal terms.

> [!definition] Definition 31.7: Unique Readability
> Unique readability is the recoverability of the outer constructor and immediate components of a syntax object. In a free constructor presentation, it corresponds to constructor injectivity and disjointness of constructor ranges.

> [!definition] Definition 31.8: Parsing
> Parsing is the recovery of structured syntax from a concrete representation such as a string. It is an effective or set-theoretic inverse to a flattening or encoding map on a well-formed language.

> [!definition] Definition 31.9: Normalization
> Normalization is the assignment of canonical representatives to equivalence classes modulo a rewrite or quotient relation.

> [!definition] Definition 31.10: Word Problem
> The word problem is the decision problem for equality in a quotient presentation.

## 32. Final Consolidated Theorem Index

> [!theorem] Theorem 32.1: Algebraic Toolkit Theorem
> For every finitary signature $\Omega$, the category of $\Omega$-algebras has direct products, subalgebras defined by closed subsets, congruence quotients, homomorphic images, kernels, generated subalgebras, and first-isomorphism factorizations as developed above.

> [!theorem] Theorem 32.2: Free Syntax Theorem
> For every set $X$, the term algebra $\mathbf T_{\Omega}(X)$ is the free $\Omega$-algebra on $X$. Every other free $\Omega$-algebra on $X$ is uniquely isomorphic to it over $X$.

> [!theorem] Theorem 32.3: Representation Theorem
> A concrete syntax carrier with generator codes and constructor codes represents the free syntax algebra exactly when the canonical homomorphism from $\mathbf T_{\Omega}(X)$ is an isomorphism. Tree, tagged-tuple, and suitably delimited string presentations satisfy this condition when their constructors are arity-correct, injective, disjoint, and complete.

> [!theorem] Theorem 32.4: Evaluation-Image-Kernel Theorem
> For a valuation $g:X\to |\mathbf B|$, the evaluation homomorphism $\operatorname{ev}_g$ has image $\langle g[X]\rangle_{\mathbf B}$ and kernel equal to the semantic equality relation among terms under $g$. The generated subalgebra is canonically isomorphic to the quotient by this kernel.

> [!theorem] Theorem 32.5: Recursion and Descent Theorem
> Structural recursion on raw syntax is uniquely determined by clauses on generators and constructors. Such a recursively defined function descends to quotient syntax exactly when it is invariant under the quotient congruence.

> [!theorem] Theorem 32.6: Substitution-Naturality Theorem
> Substitution is the unique homomorphic extension of a map from variables to terms. Evaluation after substitution equals evaluation under the pulled-back valuation:
> $$
> \overline w\circ\overline\sigma=\overline{w\sigma}.
> $$

> [!theorem] Theorem 32.7: Presentation Theorem
> A generator-relation presentation $\langle X\mid E\rangle$ is the quotient of $\mathbf T_{\Omega}(X)$ by the congruence generated by $E$. Homomorphisms out of this quotient correspond exactly to valuations of $X$ satisfying the equations $E$.

> [!theorem] Theorem 32.8: Relational Generation Theorem
> Relational constructor systems generate least relation-closed sets by finite stages under finitary hypotheses. Derivation witnesses map surjectively onto the generated closure; unique relational readability is exactly bijectivity of this value map.

> [!theorem] Theorem 32.9: Effective Syntax Theorem
> Effective parsing, equality, and quotient computation require representation-level decidability and algorithmic data beyond abstract freeness. Normal forms decide quotient equality when supplied by a terminating confluent rewrite system presenting the quotient congruence.

> [!theorem] Theorem 32.10: Categorical Packaging Theorem
> The algebraic development is equivalently expressible by the free-forgetful adjunction, term monad, polynomial-functor initial algebra, categorical products, coequalizer quotients, and kernel pairs, provided the relevant categorical constructions are interpreted in $\mathbf{Alg}_{\Omega}$.

## 33. Compression Map of Dependencies

The dependency order of the theory is as follows.

1. Signatures are required before algebras.
2. Algebras are required before homomorphisms, subalgebras, congruences, and quotients.
3. Homomorphisms and generated subalgebras are required before free algebras can be used as comparison objects.
4. The term algebra realizes the free algebra.
5. Concrete representations are validated by isomorphism to the term algebra.
6. Trees become structural presentations after the free syntax algebra exists.
7. Evaluation is the unique homomorphic extension of a valuation.
8. Images and kernels of evaluation explain generated concrete algebras.
9. Quotients explain semantic identifications and presentations by relations.
10. Structural recursion follows from freeness; quotient recursion follows from descent.
11. Substitution is evaluation into a term algebra.
12. Relational generation generalizes operation-generated closure but requires witnesses to recover derivational structure.
13. Logic specializes syntax and evaluation, with extra binding machinery for first-order formulas.
14. Effective parsing and computation require representation-specific algorithms.
15. Category theory repackages the preceding structure after the concrete algebraic facts are established.

> [!remark] Remark 33.1: Ordering Principle
> The abstract syntax algebra should be separated from its presentations, and semantic evaluation should be separated from both. This prevents the common error of trying to extract syntactic decomposition from an arbitrary concrete algebra without first proving freeness or injectivity of the evaluation map.

> [!warning] Warning 33.2: The Core Obstruction
> The recurring obstruction is nontrivial kernel. Nontrivial kernels appear as semantic collapse, quotient equations, ambiguous representations, nonunique derivations, or failed descent of recursive definitions. Identifying the relevant map and its kernel is the primary diagnostic move.


## 34. Least Fixed Point Construction of Syntax

### 34.1. Constructor Operators on Powersets

> [!definition] Definition 34.1: Raw Constructor Operator
> Let $U$ be a universe of possible codes containing disjoint codes for variables and constructor applications. A raw constructor operator for $(\Omega,X)$ is a monotone function
> $$
> \Gamma_{\Omega,X}:\mathcal P(U)\to\mathcal P(U)
> $$
> defined by
> $$
> \Gamma_{\Omega,X}(S)=\eta_X[X]\cup\{\operatorname{code}(f,s_0,\dots,s_{n-1}):f\in\Omega_n,\ s_i\in S\}.
> $$

> [!definition] Definition 34.2: Pre-Fixed and Fixed Points
> A subset $S\subseteq U$ is pre-fixed for $\Gamma_{\Omega,X}$ if
> $$
> \Gamma_{\Omega,X}(S)\subseteq S.
> $$
> It is fixed if
> $$
> \Gamma_{\Omega,X}(S)=S.
> $$

> [!construction] Construction 34.3: Least Fixed Point of Term Formation
> The term carrier may be constructed as
> $$
> T_{\Omega}(X)=\bigcap\{S\subseteq U:\Gamma_{\Omega,X}(S)\subseteq S\}.
> $$
> Under the disjoint coding assumptions, this least pre-fixed point is a fixed point and is the least set closed under the variable and operation constructors.

> [!proposition] Proposition 34.4: Stage Approximation of the Least Fixed Point
> Define
> $$
> T_0=\emptyset,
> \qquad
> T_{m+1}=\Gamma_{\Omega,X}(T_m).
> $$
> For finitary signatures,
> $$
> T_{\Omega}(X)=\bigcup_{m<\omega}T_m.
> $$
> The stage $T_m$ contains exactly the terms of height less than $m$ under the convention that variables and nullary terms occur at stage $1$.

> [!warning] Warning 34.5: Least Fixed Point Depends on the Coding Operator
> The least fixed point construction yields the intended term set only when constructor codes are injective and disjoint from variable codes. If coding identifies constructor values, the fixed point may encode a quotient or a defective representation rather than raw syntax.

### 34.2. Induction from Leastness

> [!definition] Definition 34.6: Constructor-Closed Predicate Extension
> Given a predicate $P$ on $T_{\Omega}(X)$, let
> $$
> S_P=\{t\in T_{\Omega}(X):P(t)\}.
> $$
> The predicate $P$ is constructor-closed if $\eta_X[X]\subseteq S_P$ and $f(t_0,\dots,t_{n-1})\in S_P$ whenever every $t_i\in S_P$.

> [!proposition] Proposition 34.7: Induction as Leastness
> The structural induction principle for $T_{\Omega}(X)$ is equivalent to the statement that $T_{\Omega}(X)$ is the least constructor-closed subset of the ambient code universe.

> [!definition] Definition 34.8: Primitive Recursion Algebra for a Codomain
> Given a set $Y$ and recursion data $b:X\to Y$, $r_c\in Y$, and $R_f:Y^n\to Y$, define an algebra over the constructor functor $H_X(Y)=X\amalg P_{\Omega}(Y)$ by the structure map sending variables and constructor events to their assigned outputs.

> [!proposition] Proposition 34.9: Recursion as Unique Fold
> The structurally recursive map $H:T_{\Omega}(X)\to Y$ is the unique fold from the initial constructor algebra $T_{\Omega}(X)$ to the codomain constructor algebra determined by the recursion data.

### 34.3. Height, Size, and Support

> [!definition] Definition 34.10: Size of a Term
> The size of a term $t\in T_{\Omega}(X)$ is the number of nodes in its tree presentation. It is recursively specified by
> $$
> |\eta_X(x)|=1,
> $$
> and
> $$
> |f(t_0,\dots,t_{n-1})|=1+\sum_{i<n}|t_i|.
> $$

> [!definition] Definition 34.11: Variable Support of a Term
> The variable support of $t\in T_{\Omega}(X)$ is the finite subset
> $$
> \operatorname{supp}(t)\subseteq X
> $$
> recursively defined by
> $$
> \operatorname{supp}(\eta_X(x))=\{x\},
> $$
> and
> $$
> \operatorname{supp}(f(t_0,\dots,t_{n-1}))=\bigcup_{i<n}\operatorname{supp}(t_i).
> $$

> [!proposition] Proposition 34.12: Finite Support
> Every term in a finitary term algebra has finite support. If $u:X\to Y$ and $v:X\to Y$ agree on $\operatorname{supp}(t)$, then the induced variable-renaming homomorphisms send $t$ to the same term.

> [!corollary] Corollary 34.13: Evaluation Depends on Finite Support
> If $v,w:X\to |\mathbf A|$ are valuations into an $\Omega$-algebra and $v$ and $w$ agree on $\operatorname{supp}(t)$, then
> $$
> \overline v(t)=\overline w(t).
> $$

> [!warning] Warning 34.14: Infinite Generator Set Does Not Mean Infinite Terms
> Even when $X$ is infinite, each finitary term uses only finitely many variables. Infinitary syntax requires additional arity and formation rules.

## 35. Detailed Tree Calculus

### 35.1. Address Arithmetic

> [!definition] Definition 35.1: Address Concatenation
> For addresses $u=(u_1,\dots,u_m)$ and $v=(v_1,\dots,v_n)$ in $\mathbb N_{>0}^{<\omega}$, their concatenation is
> $$
> uv=(u_1,\dots,u_m,v_1,\dots,v_n).
> $$
> The empty address $\epsilon$ is the identity for concatenation.

> [!definition] Definition 35.2: Address Translation
> For an address $u$, the translation map below $u$ is
> $$
> \lambda_u:\mathbb N_{>0}^{<\omega}\to\mathbb N_{>0}^{<\omega},
> \qquad
> \lambda_u(v)=uv.
> $$

> [!proposition] Proposition 35.3: Prefix Compatibility with Translation
> If $v\preceq w$, then $uv\preceq uw$. Conversely, if $uv\preceq uw$, then $v\preceq w$.

> [!definition] Definition 35.4: Address Difference
> If $u\preceq v$, the address difference $u^{-1}v$ is the unique address $w$ such that
> $$
> v=uw.
> $$

### 35.2. Tree Replacement Calculus

> [!definition] Definition 35.5: Occurrence of a Subtree
> An occurrence of a subtree $\sigma$ in a tree $\tau$ is an address $u\in\operatorname{dom}(\tau)$ such that
> $$
> \tau|_u=\sigma.
> $$
> Distinct addresses may determine equal subtrees as values.

> [!construction] Construction 35.6: Simultaneous Replacement
> Let $\tau$ be a tree and let $U$ be a finite prefix-incomparable subset of $\operatorname{dom}(\tau)$. For each $u\in U$, let $\sigma_u$ be a replacement tree of the appropriate syntactic sort. The simultaneous replacement
> $$
> \tau[u:=\sigma_u]_{u\in U}
> $$
> replaces the subtrees at all addresses in $U$ simultaneously.

> [!proposition] Proposition 35.7: Independence of Prefix-Incomparable Replacements
> If $U$ is prefix-incomparable, simultaneous replacement at $U$ is independent of the order in which the individual replacements are performed, up to literal equality of addressed trees under the fixed address-translation convention.

> [!warning] Warning 35.8: Nested Replacement Requires Order
> If one replacement address is below another, the result depends on whether replacement is performed at the ancestor or descendant first. A simultaneous nested replacement must specify whether descendant addresses are interpreted in the original tree or after earlier replacements.

### 35.3. Context Composition

> [!definition] Definition 35.9: One-Hole Context Composition
> If $C$ and $D$ are one-hole contexts, their composition is
> $$
> C\circ D=C[D],
> $$
> the context obtained by filling the hole of $C$ with $D$ while retaining the hole of $D$.

> [!proposition] Proposition 35.10: Monoid of One-Hole Contexts
> One-hole contexts over $T_{\Omega}(X)$ form a monoid under context composition. The identity element is the one-hole context $\Box$.

> [!definition] Definition 35.11: Context Action on Terms
> The action of the context monoid on terms is
> $$
> (C,t)\mapsto C[t].
> $$
> It satisfies
> $$
> (C\circ D)[t]=C[D[t]]
> $$
> and
> $$
> \Box[t]=t.
> $$

> [!proposition] Proposition 35.12: Congruence Closure by Context Action
> A relation $\theta$ on $T_{\Omega}(X)$ is a congruence if and only if it is an equivalence relation stable under the action of every one-hole context.

### 35.4. Positions, Paths, and Occurrence Counts

> [!definition] Definition 35.13: Occurrence Count of a Variable
> For $x\in X$ and $t\in T_{\Omega}(X)$, the occurrence count $\operatorname{occ}_x(t)$ is recursively defined by
> $$
> \operatorname{occ}_x(\eta_X(y))=
> \begin{cases}
> 1,&y=x,\\
> 0,&y\ne x,
> \end{cases}
> $$
> and
> $$
> \operatorname{occ}_x(f(t_0,\dots,t_{n-1}))=\sum_{i<n}\operatorname{occ}_x(t_i).
> $$

> [!definition] Definition 35.14: Path Word
> A path from the root to an address $u=(i_1,\dots,i_k)$ is the finite word of child indices $i_1\dots i_k$. It identifies the sequence of choices made while descending the tree.

> [!proposition] Proposition 35.15: Support by Occurrences
> For a term $t$,
> $$
> \operatorname{supp}(t)=\{x\in X:\operatorname{occ}_x(t)>0\}.
> $$

## 36. Evaluation as a Functorial Construction

### 36.1. Functoriality in Variables

> [!construction] Construction 36.1: Renaming Homomorphism
> A function $u:X\to Y$ induces a homomorphism
> $$
> T_{\Omega}(u):\mathbf T_{\Omega}(X)\to\mathbf T_{\Omega}(Y)
> $$
> satisfying
> $$
> T_{\Omega}(u)(\eta_X(x))=\eta_Y(u(x)).
> $$

> [!proposition] Proposition 36.2: Identity and Composition for Renaming
> For every set $X$,
> $$
> T_{\Omega}(\operatorname{id}_X)=\operatorname{id}_{T_{\Omega}(X)}.
> $$
> For functions $u:X\to Y$ and $v:Y\to Z$,
> $$
> T_{\Omega}(v\circ u)=T_{\Omega}(v)\circ T_{\Omega}(u).
> $$

> [!proposition] Proposition 36.3: Evaluation After Renaming
> Let $u:X\to Y$ and let $w:Y\to |\mathbf A|$. Then
> $$
> \overline w\circ T_{\Omega}(u)=\overline{w\circ u}.
> $$

### 36.2. Functoriality in Target Algebras

> [!proposition] Proposition 36.4: Homomorphism After Evaluation
> Let $h:\mathbf A\to\mathbf B$ be a homomorphism and let $g:X\to |\mathbf A|$. Then
> $$
> h\circ\operatorname{ev}_g=\operatorname{ev}_{h\circ g}.
> $$

> [!corollary] Corollary 36.5: Images Under Homomorphisms of Generated Subalgebras
> If $h:\mathbf A\to\mathbf B$ is a homomorphism and $S\subseteq |\mathbf A|$, then
> $$
> h[\langle S\rangle_{\mathbf A}]\subseteq\langle h[S]\rangle_{\mathbf B}.
> $$
> If the codomain is restricted to $\operatorname{im}(h)$, equality holds inside that image for the generated subalgebra of $h[S]$.

> [!proposition] Proposition 36.6: Kernel Pullback Under Target Homomorphism
> With $h$ and $g$ as above,
> $$
> \ker(\operatorname{ev}_g)\subseteq\ker(\operatorname{ev}_{h\circ g}).
> $$
> Equality holds if $h$ is injective on $\langle g[X]\rangle_{\mathbf A}$.

### 36.3. Product Evaluation

> [!proposition] Proposition 36.7: Evaluation into Products
> Let $(\mathbf A_i)_{i\in I}$ be a family of $\Omega$-algebras and let
> $$
> g:X\to\prod_{i\in I}|\mathbf A_i|.
> $$
> Write $g_i=\pi_i\circ g$. Then for every term $t$,
> $$
> \operatorname{ev}_g(t)(i)=\operatorname{ev}_{g_i}(t).
> $$

> [!corollary] Corollary 36.8: Product Kernel Intersection
> In the situation of Proposition 36.7,
> $$
> \ker(\operatorname{ev}_g)=\bigcap_{i\in I}\ker(\operatorname{ev}_{g_i}).
> $$

> [!remark] Remark 36.9: Equational Validity via Products
> An equation holds under evaluation into a product precisely when it holds under evaluation into each coordinate algebra.

## 37. Detailed Presentation Examples and Kernels

### 37.1. Associativity and Identity

> [!example] Example 37.1: Monoid Presentation from Raw Binary Syntax
> Let $\Omega$ contain a binary symbol $m$ and a nullary symbol $e$. The monoid equations are
> $$
> m(m(x,y),z)\approx m(x,m(y,z)),
> $$
> $$
> m(e,x)\approx x,
> $$
> and
> $$
> m(x,e)\approx x.
> $$
> The free monoid on $X$ is the quotient of $\mathbf T_{\Omega}(X)$ by the fully invariant congruence generated by these equations.

> [!example] Example 37.2: Commutative Monoid
> Adding the equation
> $$
> m(x,y)\approx m(y,x)
> $$
> yields the free commutative monoid on $X$, represented by finitely supported functions $X\to\mathbb N$.

> [!remark] Remark 37.3: Normal Form for Free Commutative Monoids
> A finitely supported function $\alpha:X\to\mathbb N$ represents the product in which each generator $x$ occurs $\alpha(x)$ times. The order and parenthesization of occurrences have been quotiented away by associativity, identity, and commutativity.

### 37.2. Groups

> [!example] Example 37.4: Group Equations
> In a signature with binary $m$, unary $i$, and nullary $e$, the group equations include associativity, identity equations, and inverse equations
> $$
> m(i(x),x)\approx e,
> $$
> $$
> m(x,i(x))\approx e.
> $$

> [!example] Example 37.5: Kernel of the Map to a Presented Group
> If a group $G$ is generated by $g[X]$, the kernel of
> $$
> \operatorname{ev}_g:\mathbf T_{\Omega}(X)\to G
> $$
> contains the group-equation congruence and may contain additional relations specific to $G$, such as $x^n\approx e$ for a generator of finite order.

> [!warning] Warning 37.6: Raw Group-Signature Terms Are Not Reduced Words
> Reduced words are representatives after quotienting by the group axioms and applying a normalization procedure. The raw term algebra contains many more formal expressions.

### 37.3. Semilattices and Idempotence

> [!example] Example 37.7: Join-Semilattice Presentation
> A join-semilattice with bottom has binary $\vee$ and nullary $0$ satisfying associativity, commutativity, idempotence,
> $$
> x\vee x\approx x,
> $$
> and bottom identity,
> $$
> x\vee 0\approx x.
> $$
> The free join-semilattice with bottom on $X$ is represented by finite subsets of $X$ with union as join and $\emptyset$ as bottom.

> [!remark] Remark 37.8: Idempotence as Multiplicity Collapse
> The idempotence equation identifies multiple occurrences of the same generator. Thus occurrence count in raw syntax does not descend to the free semilattice, while support does.

### 37.4. Boolean Algebras and Propositional Equivalence

> [!example] Example 37.9: Boolean Algebra Quotient of Formula Syntax
> Propositional formulas modulo Boolean algebra equivalence form a Boolean algebra. If $P$ is finite, this algebra is finite and is isomorphic to the algebra of Boolean functions
> $$
> \{0,1\}^{\{0,1\}^P}.
> $$

> [!warning] Warning 37.10: Formula Size Versus Boolean Function Size
> Distinct formulas can denote the same Boolean function. The quotient by semantic equivalence may be exponentially smaller or larger in representational complexity depending on the chosen normal form.

## 38. Rewrite Systems as Computational Quotient Presentations

### 38.1. Reduction Relations

> [!definition] Definition 38.1: Reflexive Transitive Closure
> For a relation $\to$ on a set $A$, its reflexive transitive closure $\to^*$ is the least reflexive transitive relation containing $\to$.

> [!definition] Definition 38.2: Joinability
> Elements $a,b\in A$ are joinable with respect to $\to$ if there exists $c\in A$ such that
> $$
> a\to^* c
> \qquad\text{and}\qquad
> b\to^* c.
> $$

> [!definition] Definition 38.3: Local Confluence
> A relation $\to$ is locally confluent if whenever
> $$
> a\to b
> $$
> and
> $$
> a\to c,
> $$
> then $b$ and $c$ are joinable.

> [!theorem] Theorem 38.4: Newman's Lemma
> If a rewrite relation is terminating and locally confluent, then it is confluent.

> [!warning] Warning 38.5: Local Checks Require Termination
> Local confluence alone does not imply confluence. Termination is the additional hypothesis in Newman's lemma.

### 38.2. Rewrite Congruences

> [!definition] Definition 38.6: Conversion Relation
> The conversion relation generated by $\to$ is the reflexive, symmetric, transitive closure of $\to$. It is denoted $\leftrightarrow^*$.

> [!definition] Definition 38.7: Rewrite System Presents a Congruence
> A rewrite system presents a congruence $\theta$ on $T_{\Omega}(X)$ if
> $$
> s\leftrightarrow^* t
> \quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad
> (s,t)\in\theta
> $$
> for all $s,t\in T_{\Omega}(X)$.

> [!proposition] Proposition 38.8: Normalization Decides Presented Congruence
> If $\to$ is terminating, confluent, and presents $\theta$, then
> $$
> (s,t)\in\theta
> $$
> if and only if the unique normal forms of $s$ and $t$ are structurally equal.

> [!definition] Definition 38.9: Critical Pair
> A critical pair is a pair of terms arising from an overlap between left-hand sides of rewrite rules. A formal critical-pair definition requires a chosen term-position calculus and unification procedure for the term language.

> [!remark] Remark 38.10: Critical Pair Analysis
> For many first-order term rewrite systems, confluence can be studied by checking joinability of critical pairs under termination hypotheses. The exact criterion depends on the rewrite-system format.

### 38.3. Orientation Failure

> [!warning] Warning 38.11: Symmetric Equations May Not Orient Well
> An equation such as commutativity,
> $$
> x\cdot y\approx y\cdot x,
> $$
> cannot be oriented into a terminating rewrite rule without additional ordering or canonicalization data, because the reverse pattern remains equally valid.

> [!warning] Warning 38.12: Termination and Confluence Are Independent
> A rewrite system may terminate without being confluent, or be confluent without terminating. Unique normal forms require both termination and confluence, or a separate canonical-representative theorem.

## 39. Detailed Computability Boundaries

### 39.1. Decidable Presentations

> [!definition] Definition 39.1: Decidable Signature Presentation
> A signature presentation is decidable if operation symbols can be recognized, arities can be computed, and equality of operation symbols is decidable.

> [!definition] Definition 39.2: Decidable Term Representation
> A term representation is decidable if well-formedness, equality of codes, outer constructor recovery, and immediate-subterm extraction are decidable operations.

> [!proposition] Proposition 39.3: Decidable Structural Functions
> In a decidable finite-arity term representation, the functions height, size, support, occurrence count for a fixed variable, and structural equality are computable.

> [!warning] Warning 39.4: Infinite Signatures Require Symbol Algorithms
> If $\Omega$ is infinite, effective term manipulation requires algorithms for recognizing operation-symbol codes and computing arities. Set-theoretic existence of $\Omega$ is not enough for implementation.

### 39.2. Semi-Decidability of Generated Congruences

> [!definition] Definition 39.5: Semi-Decidable Relation
> A relation $R\subseteq A\times A$ is semi-decidable if there is an algorithm that halts and accepts exactly the pairs in $R$, with no requirement to halt on pairs outside $R$.

> [!proposition] Proposition 39.6: Enumerability of Finitely Generated Equational Consequences
> If $E$ is a computably enumerable set of equations over a decidable term representation, then the generated congruence $\theta_E$ is computably enumerable.

> [!warning] Warning 39.7: Enumerable Does Not Mean Decidable
> Even when all derivable equalities can be enumerated, there may be no algorithm deciding non-equality modulo $E$.

### 39.3. Quotient Data Structures

> [!definition] Definition 39.8: Canonical Representative Function
> A canonical representative function for a quotient $A/\theta$ is a function
> $$
> n:A\to A
> $$
> such that:
> 
> 1. $(a,n(a))\in\theta$ for every $a\in A$;
> 2. if $(a,b)\in\theta$, then $n(a)=n(b)$.

> [!proposition] Proposition 39.9: Canonical Representatives Decide Equality
> If a computable canonical representative function exists for $A/\theta$ and equality in $A$ is decidable, then equality in $A/\theta$ is decidable by comparing representatives.

> [!warning] Warning 39.10: Choosing Representatives May Require Nonconstructive Choice
> Set-theoretically, quotient classes may admit choice functions under choice principles. Such functions need not be definable or computable.

## 40. Extended Logic Application: Algebraic and Non-Algebraic Layers

### 40.1. Formula Algebra and Lindenbaum Quotients

> [!definition] Definition 40.1: Lindenbaum Congruence
> For a deductive system or semantic consequence relation on formulas, the Lindenbaum congruence identifies formulas $\varphi,\psi$ when each entails the other according to the chosen consequence relation. In propositional Boolean semantics, this is truth-functional equivalence.

> [!construction] Construction 40.2: Lindenbaum Algebra
> The Lindenbaum algebra of a propositional logic is the quotient of the formula algebra by the Lindenbaum congruence, when the consequence relation is compatible with the formula constructors.

> [!warning] Warning 40.3: Compatibility of Consequence Is Required
> If a formula equivalence relation is not preserved by connectives, the quotient does not inherit well-defined connective operations.

### 40.2. Tautological Consequence Versus Equational Validity

> [!definition] Definition 40.4: Formula Evaluation Kernel for a Single Valuation
> For a valuation $v:P\to\{0,1\}$, the kernel
> $$
> \ker(\overline v)
> $$
> identifies formulas with the same truth value under $v$.

> [!definition] Definition 40.5: Truth-Functional Equivalence Kernel
> The semantic equivalence relation of propositional formulas is
> $$
> \bigcap_{v:P\to\{0,1\}}\ker(\overline v).
> $$
> This is the kernel intersection over all valuations.

> [!proposition] Proposition 40.6: Tautology as Constant-One Evaluation
> A formula $\varphi$ is a tautology if and only if its term operation on the Boolean algebra $\mathbf 2$ is the constant function with value $1$.

### 40.3. First-Order Satisfaction Is Indexed by Assignments

> [!definition] Definition 40.7: Satisfaction Relation
> For a first-order structure $\mathcal M$, satisfaction is a relation
> $$
> \mathcal M,s\models\varphi
> $$
> between assignments $s$ and formulas $\varphi$. It is not a single homomorphism from the full formula syntax algebra to $\{0,1\}$ independent of assignment.

> [!proposition] Proposition 40.8: Assignment Dependence on Free Variables
> If assignments $s,s':V\to M$ agree on $\operatorname{FV}(\varphi)$, then
> $$
> \mathcal M,s\models\varphi
> \quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad
> \mathcal M,s'\models\varphi.
> $$

> [!warning] Warning 40.9: Closed Sentences and Open Formulas
> A sentence has no free variables and its truth in a structure is independent of assignment. An open formula generally denotes a definable set or relation over assignments, not a fixed truth value without an assignment.

## 41. Master Failure-Mode Catalogue

### 41.1. Structural Failures

> [!warning] Warning 41.1: Failure of Arity Correctness
> If a constructor labelled by $f\in\Omega_n$ is applied to a tuple of length different from $n$, the resulting object is not an $\Omega$-term unless an explicit variadic or ill-formed syntax layer is being studied.

> [!warning] Warning 41.2: Failure of Disjointness
> If generator codes and constructor codes overlap, a syntax object may be both a generator and a compound object. Unique decomposition then fails unless the overlap is removed by tagging or quotient rules are specified.

> [!warning] Warning 41.3: Failure of Constructor Injectivity
> If two different input tuples to a constructor produce the same represented object, immediate subterms cannot be recovered from the object alone. Structural recursion by immediate components is not well-defined without choosing representatives.

> [!warning] Warning 41.4: Failure of Constructor Range Decidability
> If constructor ranges are not decidable in an implementation representation, an abstract destructor may exist without an algorithm for applying it to external input data.

### 41.2. Semantic Failures

> [!warning] Warning 41.5: Failure of Faithful Evaluation
> If $\ker(\operatorname{ev}_g)$ is nontrivial, syntactically distinct terms denote the same semantic value. Any semantic operation that attempts to recover syntax from values is invalid without choosing representatives or proving freeness.

> [!warning] Warning 41.6: Failure of Complete Presentation
> If $\theta_E\subsetneq\ker(\operatorname{ev}_g)$, the equations $E$ are sound but incomplete for the concrete generated algebra. Equality in the concrete algebra is stronger than equality derivable from the presentation.

> [!warning] Warning 41.7: Failure of Sound Presentation
> If $E\nsubseteq\ker(\operatorname{ev}_g)$, at least one defining equation of the presentation is false under the valuation. The valuation does not factor through the presented algebra.

### 41.3. Computational Failures

> [!warning] Warning 41.8: Failure of Termination
> A rewrite procedure that does not terminate cannot serve as a total normalization algorithm, even if its rewrite steps preserve the intended congruence.

> [!warning] Warning 41.9: Failure of Confluence
> A terminating rewrite procedure that is not confluent may produce different normal forms from the same term. Normal-form equality then fails to decide quotient equality.

> [!warning] Warning 41.10: Failure of Parser Completeness
> A parser that recognizes only some well-formed strings cannot be used as a full inverse to flattening for the intended string presentation.

> [!warning] Warning 41.11: Failure of Parser Soundness
> A parser that accepts ill-formed strings or returns a tree whose flattening is not the input does not validate the string representation.

## 42. Final Formal Summary

The treatise has developed the following mathematical object chain:

$$
(\Omega,X)
\leadsto
\mathbf T_{\Omega}(X)
\leadsto
\mathbf R\cong\mathbf T_{\Omega}(X)
\leadsto
\operatorname{ev}_g:\mathbf T_{\Omega}(X)\to\mathbf B
\leadsto
\langle g[X]\rangle_{\mathbf B}
\cong
\mathbf T_{\Omega}(X)/\ker(\operatorname{ev}_g).
$$

The core principle is that syntax is first a free algebra; concrete representations are isomorphic presentations of that algebra; evaluation is a homomorphism out of that algebra; generated concrete structures are images of evaluation; semantic identifications are kernels; quotient presentations impose congruences; recursion and induction are governed by freeness and by descent through quotients; relational generation replaces functional constructors with witnessed relations; logic is a specialization with extra binding and semantic structure; and computability requires additional algorithmic hypotheses beyond the abstract universal properties.

> [!theorem] Theorem 42.1: Single-Spine Summary Theorem
> Let $\Omega$ be a finitary signature, $X$ a set, $\mathbf R$ a concrete syntax representation over $X$, $\mathbf B$ an $\Omega$-algebra, and $g:X\to |\mathbf B|$ a valuation. If $\mathbf R$ is isomorphic to $\mathbf T_{\Omega}(X)$ over $X$, then every evaluation of represented syntax into $\mathbf B$ is obtained uniquely by transporting the homomorphism $\operatorname{ev}_g$ across that isomorphism. Its image is the generated subalgebra $\langle g[X]\rangle_{\mathbf B}$, its kernel is the semantic equality relation among represented terms, and the generated subalgebra is canonically the quotient of free syntax by that kernel.

> [!corollary] Corollary 42.2: Diagnostic Rule
> In any problem involving terms, trees, strings, generated objects, or semantic values, identify the relevant comparison map first. Surjectivity gives generatedness, injectivity gives freeness or faithful representation, the kernel gives identifications, and quotienting by the kernel gives the corresponding concrete image.


## 43. Congruence Generation as a Closure Process

### 43.1. Closure Rules for Congruence Generation

> [!definition] Definition 43.1: Congruence-Closure Rule System
> Let $R\subseteq |\mathbf A|^2$. The congruence-closure rule system generated by $R$ consists of the following closure requirements on relations $Q\subseteq |\mathbf A|^2$:
> 
> 1. containment of $R$;
> 2. reflexivity: $(a,a)\in Q$;
> 3. symmetry: $(a,b)\in Q\Rightarrow(b,a)\in Q$;
> 4. transitivity: $(a,b),(b,c)\in Q\Rightarrow(a,c)\in Q$;
> 5. operation compatibility: if $(a_i,b_i)\in Q$ for all $i<n$, then
> $$
> (f^{\mathbf A}(a_0,\dots,a_{n-1}),f^{\mathbf A}(b_0,\dots,b_{n-1}))\in Q.
> $$

> [!proposition] Proposition 43.2: Congruence as Least Rule-Closed Relation
> The generated congruence $\operatorname{Cg}_{\mathbf A}(R)$ is the least relation on $|\mathbf A|$ closed under the rules in Definition 43.1.

> [!construction] Construction 43.3: Iterated Congruence Closure Operator
> Define an operator $\Lambda$ on binary relations over $|\mathbf A|$ by adjoining to a relation $Q$ all pairs required by one application of reflexivity, symmetry, transitivity, and operation compatibility. Then
> $$
> \operatorname{Cg}_{\mathbf A}(R)=\bigcup_{m<\omega}\Lambda^m(R)
> $$
> when the finitary closure process is sufficient; in general, transfinite iteration may be used to reach the least fixed point.

> [!warning] Warning 43.4: Transitivity Can Force Unbounded Chains
> Even for finitary signatures, the transitive closure component of congruence generation may require arbitrarily long finite chains. A bounded finite number of closure rounds need not suffice uniformly for all pairs.

### 43.2. Contextual View of Generated Congruences

> [!definition] Definition 43.5: Elementary Context Pair
> Given a one-hole context $C$ and a pair $(a,b)$ of terms, the elementary context pair is
> $$
> (C[a],C[b]).
> $$

> [!proposition] Proposition 43.6: Contextual Generation of Term Congruences
> In a term algebra, the congruence generated by $E$ is the equivalence closure of the set of all elementary context pairs generated from pairs in $E$.

> [!definition] Definition 43.7: Substitution-Context Pair
> If equations are treated schematically, the generating pairs are
> $$
> (C[\overline\sigma(s)],C[\overline\sigma(t)]),
> $$
> where $(s,t)\in E$, $\sigma$ is a substitution, and $C$ is a one-hole context.

> [!warning] Warning 43.8: Fixed Equations and Schematic Equations Differ
> The equation $x\approx y$ over a two-variable set as a fixed relation identifies two named generators. As a schematic identity it identifies every pair of terms after substitution, which collapses the entire term algebra in many signatures.

## 44. Substitution on Quotient Syntax

### 44.1. Substitution Respecting a Congruence

> [!definition] Definition 44.1: Substitution-Compatible Congruence
> A congruence $\theta$ on $\mathbf T_{\Omega}(X)$ is substitution-compatible relative to a class $\Sigma$ of substitutions if for every induced endomorphism $\overline\sigma$ in $\Sigma$ and every $(s,t)\in\theta$,
> $$
> (\overline\sigma(s),\overline\sigma(t))\in\theta.
> $$

> [!proposition] Proposition 44.2: Induced Substitution on a Quotient
> If $\theta$ is preserved by a substitution homomorphism
> $$
> \overline\sigma:\mathbf T_{\Omega}(X)\to\mathbf T_{\Omega}(X),
> $$
> then there exists a unique homomorphism
> $$
> \overline\sigma_\theta:\mathbf T_{\Omega}(X)/\theta\to\mathbf T_{\Omega}(X)/\theta
> $$
> satisfying
> $$
> \overline\sigma_\theta([t]_\theta)=[\overline\sigma(t)]_\theta.
> $$

> [!warning] Warning 44.3: Quotient Syntax May Not Admit All Substitutions
> A quotient by relations among named generators may fail to be stable under arbitrary substitutions. Full substitution on quotient syntax requires a fully invariant congruence or a specified restricted substitution class.

### 44.2. Presented Algebras and Term Interpretation

> [!definition] Definition 44.4: Interpretation of Terms in a Presented Algebra
> In $\langle X\mid E\rangle$, the interpretation of a term $t\in T_{\Omega}(X)$ is its class
> $$
> [t]_{\theta_E}.
> $$

> [!proposition] Proposition 44.5: Generators of a Presented Algebra
> The elements
> $$
> [\eta_X(x)]_{\theta_E}
> $$
> for $x\in X$ generate $\langle X\mid E\rangle$ as an $\Omega$-algebra.

> [!proposition] Proposition 44.6: Kernel of the Presentation Projection
> The quotient projection
> $$
> q_E:\mathbf T_{\Omega}(X)\to\langle X\mid E\rangle
> $$
> has kernel
> $$
> \ker(q_E)=\theta_E.
> $$

> [!remark] Remark 44.7: Presentation Projection as Universal Model of Relations
> The presented algebra is the universal algebra generated by $X$ in which all equations in $E$ hold. Every other such algebra receives a unique homomorphic image of this universal quotient according to its valuation of $X$.

## 45. Formal Treatment of Images, Embeddings, and Subobjects

### 45.1. Induced Structure on Images

> [!definition] Definition 45.1: Induced Image Operation
> Let $h:\mathbf A\to\mathbf B$ be a homomorphism. For $f\in\Omega_n$ and $b_i\in h[|\mathbf A|]$, choose $a_i\in |\mathbf A|$ with $h(a_i)=b_i$. The operation on the image is
> $$
> f^{\operatorname{im}(h)}(b_0,\dots,b_{n-1})=f^{\mathbf B}(b_0,\dots,b_{n-1}).
> $$
> This is independent of choices because the image is a subset of $\mathbf B$ and operations are restricted from $\mathbf B$.

> [!proposition] Proposition 45.2: Homomorphic Image as Quotient
> Every homomorphic image of $\mathbf A$ is isomorphic to a quotient of $\mathbf A$ by the kernel congruence of the homomorphism.

> [!proposition] Proposition 45.3: Quotient as Homomorphic Image
> Every quotient $\mathbf A/\theta$ is a homomorphic image of $\mathbf A$ under the quotient projection.

> [!corollary] Corollary 45.4: Homomorphic Images and Quotients Coincide up to Isomorphism
> The class of homomorphic images of an algebra $\mathbf A$, considered up to isomorphism, is exactly the class of quotient algebras $\mathbf A/\theta$ for $\theta\in\operatorname{Con}(\mathbf A)$.

### 45.2. Embeddings and Subalgebra Identification

> [!definition] Definition 45.5: Inclusion Homomorphism
> If $\mathbf S\leq\mathbf A$, the inclusion function
> $$
> i:|\mathbf S|\hookrightarrow |\mathbf A|
> $$
> is a homomorphism $\mathbf S\to\mathbf A$.

> [!proposition] Proposition 45.6: Embedding Image
> If $h:\mathbf A\to\mathbf B$ is injective, then $h[|\mathbf A|]$ carries a subalgebra of $\mathbf B$ and $h$ is an isomorphism from $\mathbf A$ onto that image subalgebra.

> [!warning] Warning 45.7: Injective Function Without Homomorphism Is Not an Embedding
> An injective function between carriers is not an algebraic embedding unless it preserves all operations and identifies the source operations with the induced operations on its image.

## 46. Algebraic Closure and Galois-Style Perspectives

### 46.1. Closure from Operators and Invariants

> [!definition] Definition 46.1: Invariant Subset Under a Family of Maps
> Let $B$ be a set and let $\mathcal F$ be a family of finitary operations on $B$. A subset $C\subseteq B$ is invariant under $\mathcal F$ if it is closed under every operation in $\mathcal F$.

> [!construction] Construction 46.2: Invariant Closure
> For $S\subseteq B$, define
> $$
> \operatorname{InvCl}_{\mathcal F}(S)=\bigcap\{C\subseteq B:S\subseteq C\text{ and }C\text{ is invariant under }\mathcal F\}.
> $$
> This is the generated subalgebra closure when $\mathcal F$ is the family of basic operations of an algebra.

> [!definition] Definition 46.3: Property-Defined Closure
> Let $\mathcal P$ be a family of predicates on subsets of $B$. A property-defined closure sends $S$ to the least subset containing $S$ satisfying all predicates in $\mathcal P$, when such least subsets exist by intersection closure.

> [!remark] Remark 46.4: Algebraic Generation as One Closure Pattern
> Generated subalgebras are one instance of a broader pattern: close a seed set under specified operations, relations, rules, or invariance requirements, then use minimality to obtain induction principles.

### 46.2. Galois Connections from Syntax and Semantics

> [!definition] Definition 46.5: Equation Set Valid in an Algebra Class
> For a class $\mathcal K$ of algebras, define
> $$
> \operatorname{Eq}(\mathcal K)=\{s\approx t:\mathbf A\models s\approx t\text{ for every }\mathbf A\in\mathcal K\}.
> $$

> [!definition] Definition 46.6: Models of an Equation Set
> For an equation set $E$, define
> $$
> \operatorname{Mod}(E)=\{\mathbf A:\mathbf A\models s\approx t\text{ for every }s\approx t\in E\}.
> $$

> [!proposition] Proposition 46.7: Equational Galois Connection
> The assignments $E\mapsto\operatorname{Mod}(E)$ and $\mathcal K\mapsto\operatorname{Eq}(\mathcal K)$ form an order-reversing Galois connection between equation sets and classes of algebras:
> $$
> E\subseteq\operatorname{Eq}(\mathcal K)
> \quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad
> \mathcal K\subseteq\operatorname{Mod}(E).
> $$

> [!corollary] Corollary 46.8: Equational Closure
> The composite
> $$
> E\mapsto\operatorname{Eq}(\operatorname{Mod}(E))
> $$
> is a closure operator on equation sets. It sends axioms to their semantic equational consequences.

> [!warning] Warning 46.9: Semantic Consequence Depends on the Model Class
> The same equation set may have different consequences depending on whether models are all $\Omega$-algebras, a variety, a finite class, a single algebra, or algebras satisfying additional non-equational constraints.

## 47. Implementation-Oriented Invariants Without Code

### 47.1. Abstract Data Type Conditions

> [!definition] Definition 47.1: Constructor Interface
> A constructor interface for syntax over $(\Omega,X)$ consists of operations that create variable nodes and operation nodes, together with predicates or destructors identifying the corresponding cases.

> [!definition] Definition 47.2: Representation Invariant
> A representation invariant is a predicate on raw implementation data specifying exactly which data values represent valid syntax objects.

> [!definition] Definition 47.3: Abstraction Function
> An abstraction function maps valid implementation data to mathematical syntax objects, typically
> $$
> \alpha:D_{\mathrm{valid}}\to T_{\Omega}(X)
> $$
> or to a concrete presentation isomorphic to $T_{\Omega}(X)$.

> [!proposition] Proposition 47.4: Correctness of an Implementation Representation
> An implementation representation is mathematically correct when its abstraction function is surjective onto the intended syntax, respects constructors, and identifies implementation data exactly according to the intended equality convention.

> [!warning] Warning 47.5: Pointer Identity Is Not Structural Equality Without an Invariant
> Pointer identity agrees with structural equality only under an invariant such as hash-consing uniqueness. Without such an invariant, two different memory nodes may represent equal syntax.

### 47.2. Destructors and Pattern Matching

> [!definition] Definition 47.6: Total Destructor Family
> A total destructor family for a syntax representation partitions valid syntax objects into cases and returns the unique data associated with each case. For terms, the cases are variable, nullary operation, and positive-arity operation application.

> [!definition] Definition 47.7: Partial Destructor
> A partial destructor for a constructor $C_f$ is defined only on the range of $C_f$ and returns the unique input tuple when the input lies in that range.

> [!proposition] Proposition 47.8: Destructors and Constructor Injectivity
> A constructor $C_f:R^n\to R$ has a partial inverse destructor on its range if and only if $C_f$ is injective. A total case-splitting destructor for all constructors additionally requires disjointness of constructor ranges and separation from generators.

> [!warning] Warning 47.9: Pattern Matching Assumes Unique Readability
> Pattern matching on syntax is mathematically justified only when each pattern corresponds to a decidable and disjoint constructor case, or when the matching semantics explicitly handles overlap and failure.

## 48. Additional Structural Comparison Results

### 48.1. Reducts, Expansions, and Term Translation

> [!proposition] Proposition 48.1: Reduct of a Term Algebra
> If $\Omega'\subseteq\Omega$, then the $\Omega'$-reduct of $\mathbf T_{\Omega}(X)$ contains terms using operation symbols outside $\Omega'$ in its carrier. It is generally not equal to $\mathbf T_{\Omega'}(X)$.

> [!construction] Construction 48.2: Inclusion of Smaller Term Algebra
> If $\Omega'\subseteq\Omega$, there is an injective homomorphism
> $$
> \mathbf T_{\Omega'}(X)\to (\mathbf T_{\Omega}(X))|_{\Omega'}
> $$
> whose image consists of terms using only symbols from $\Omega'$.

> [!warning] Warning 48.3: Reduct Does Not Delete Old Terms
> Passing to a reduct removes operations from the algebraic structure; it does not remove carrier elements. Thus a reduct of a term algebra may contain terms that cannot be generated using the smaller signature from $X$.

> [!definition] Definition 48.4: Signature Translation
> A signature translation from $\Omega$ to $\Lambda$ assigns to each $f\in\Omega_n$ an $n$-ary $\Lambda$-term. Such a translation induces a functor from $\Lambda$-algebras to $\Omega$-algebras by interpreting each $f$ as the corresponding term operation.

> [!proposition] Proposition 48.5: Term Translation Induced by Signature Translation
> A signature translation from $\Omega$ to $\Lambda$ induces a homomorphism-like translation
> $$
> T_{\Omega}(X)\to T_{\Lambda}(X)
> $$
> recursively replacing each $\Omega$-symbol by its assigned $\Lambda$-term operation.

### 48.2. Expansions by Definitions

> [!definition] Definition 48.6: Definitional Expansion
> A definitional expansion of a signature $\Omega$ adds new operation symbols, each interpreted by a fixed term operation over the original signature.

> [!proposition] Proposition 48.7: Definitional Expansion Preserves Underlying Algebraic Content
> If $\Omega'$ is a definitional expansion of $\Omega$, then every $\Omega$-algebra has a unique compatible $\Omega'$-expansion. Homomorphisms of $\Omega$-algebras are exactly homomorphisms of the corresponding definitional expansions.

> [!warning] Warning 48.8: Arbitrary Expansion Adds Structure
> Adding operation symbols without defining them by old term operations changes the category of algebras and may change subalgebras, congruences, generated closures, and free algebras.

## 49. Additional Synthesis: The Four Kernels

> [!definition] Definition 49.1: Representation Kernel
> For a code map $e:T_{\Omega}(X)\to C$, the representation kernel is
> $$
> \ker(e)=\{(s,t):e(s)=e(t)\}.
> $$
> It measures collisions in the representation.

> [!definition] Definition 49.2: Evaluation Kernel
> For a valuation $g:X\to |\mathbf B|$, the evaluation kernel is $\ker(\operatorname{ev}_g)$. It measures semantic equality under $g$.

> [!definition] Definition 49.3: Presentation Kernel
> For equations $E$, the presentation kernel is $\theta_E$. It measures the equality imposed by the presentation.

> [!definition] Definition 49.4: Derivation Kernel
> For a derivation value map $\operatorname{val}:D\to C$, the derivation kernel is
> $$
> \ker(\operatorname{val})=\{(d,e):\operatorname{val}(d)=\operatorname{val}(e)\}.
> $$
> It measures nonunique witnesses.

> [!proposition] Proposition 49.5: Kernel Diagnostic Scheme
> In each case, the relevant map is faithful exactly when its kernel is diagonal. Nontrivial kernel is the formal location of collapse, ambiguity, identification, or nonunique representation.

| Kernel | Map | Collapse Type | Repair Strategy |
|---|---|---|---|
| representation kernel | terms to codes | coding collision | tagging, parser discipline, faithful encoding |
| evaluation kernel | terms to semantic values | semantic identification | quotient syntax, prove freeness, use representatives |
| presentation kernel | terms to quotient classes | imposed equations | normal forms, word-problem solver, congruence analysis |
| derivation kernel | witnesses to values | nonunique derivations | refine witnesses, orient rules, impose canonical certificates |

## 50. Terminal Checklist for Formalization

> [!remark] Remark 50.1: Formalization Checklist
> A complete formalization of a syntax-generated-semantics pipeline should answer all of the following questions:
> 
> 1. What is the signature, and what is the arity of every operation symbol?
> 2. Which symbols are generators, and which are nullary operation symbols?
> 3. What is the abstract free algebra?
> 4. What concrete representation is being used?
> 5. What is the representation map from the term algebra?
> 6. Is the representation map injective?
> 7. Is the representation map surjective onto the intended well-formed objects?
> 8. Are constructor ranges disjoint?
> 9. Are constructor maps injective?
> 10. Are destructors set-theoretic, computable, or both?
> 11. What is the target algebra?
> 12. What valuation interprets the generators?
> 13. What is the evaluation homomorphism?
> 14. What is its image?
> 15. What is its kernel?
> 16. Is the generated subalgebra free or merely generated?
> 17. Are any equations imposed by presentation?
> 18. Is the presentation sound for the intended semantics?
> 19. Is the presentation complete for the intended semantics?
> 20. Does recursion occur on raw syntax or quotient syntax?
> 21. If quotient syntax is used, what is the descent condition?
> 22. If strings are used, what is the parser and what is its domain?
> 23. If binding is used, what is the alpha-equivalence or nameless representation policy?
> 24. If computation is required, which equality problems are decidable?
> 25. If derivations are recorded, what is the value map and what is its kernel?

> [!warning] Warning 50.2: Do Not Infer Missing Structure
> None of the following may be inferred without explicit data: arity, sort, generator status, nullary-operation status, quotient congruence, parser, valuation, semantic target, computability, alpha-equivalence convention, or normal-form procedure.
